# Wycliffe Bible

Long before the printing press revolutionized the world, a group of dedicated scholars embarked on a perilous mission to translate the Bible into the common tongue. This endeavor challenged the established religious authorities and changed the course of English history. The resulting work became known as the Wycliffe Bible. It remains a landmark in the development of the English language and the history of the Reformation.

## Historical Context

The Wycliffe Bible emerged during a time when the Bible was only available in Latin. This version, known as the Vulgate (the Latin translation of the Bible used by the Roman Catholic Church), was inaccessible to the majority of people in England. John Wycliffe believed that every person should have the right to read the scriptures in their own language. He and his associates began the monumental task of translating the Latin text into Middle English. This work reflects the biblical principle that the word of God should be known by all people.

> And these words, which I command thee this day, shall be in thine heart: And thou shalt teach them diligently unto thy children, and shalt talk of them when thou sittest in thine house, and when thou walkest by the way, and when thou liest down, and when thou risest up. — Deuteronomy 6:6,7 (KJV)

Nicholas of Hereford and John Wycliffe completed the Early Version (EV) in 1382. This version followed the Latin syntax very closely. It resulted in a text that was often difficult to read. The translators prioritized a word-for-word approach. They wanted to ensure that no part of the sacred text was lost or misinterpreted. This dedication to the text aligns with the reverence shown for the name of the Almighty.

> I am the LORD, and there is none else, there is no God beside me: I girded thee, though thou hast not known me. — Isaiah 45:5 (KJV)

## Translation Versions

The Wycliffe Bible exists in two primary forms. These are the Early Version (EV) and the Later Version (LV). The Later Version was revised by John Purvey in 1388. This revision was much more than a simple correction of errors. Purvey focused on making the English flow more naturally. He moved away from the rigid Latin structure of the EV.

The Later Version became much more popular than its predecessor. It was easier to understand and circulated widely. Many scholars believe the LV represents a significant step in the evolution of English prose. It demonstrated that English was capable of expressing complex theological and philosophical ideas.

## Opposition and Legacy

The Roman Catholic Church strongly opposed the translation of the Bible into English. They believed that it would lead to heresy and the undermining of church authority. At the Oxford Convocation of 1408, the church formally banned any new English translations of the Bible without prior approval.

> At the Oxford Convocation of 1408, it was solemnly voted that in England no new translation of the Bible should be made without prior approval. — Wikipedia

John Wycliffe was posthumously condemned as a heretic. His remains were exhumed and burned years after his death. Despite this persecution, the Wycliffe Bible continued to be copied and read in secret. Many manuscripts survived and are held in collections such as the [British Library](https://www.bl.uk/collection-items/wycliffe-bible). It laid the groundwork for future English translations. The [King James Version](king-james-version.draft.md) and other subsequent Bibles owe a debt to the pioneering work of Wycliffe and his team. This spirit of making the word accessible is echoed in the Great Commission.

> Go ye therefore, and teach all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost: Teaching them to observe all things whatsoever I have commanded you: and, lo, I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world. Amen. — Matthew 28:19,20 (KJV)

The Wycliffe Bible remains a testament to the power of the word and the courage of those who sought to make it accessible to all.

> For the LORD is good; his mercy is everlasting; and his truth endureth to all generations. — Psalm 100:5 (KJV)

## Conclusion

The Wycliffe Bible stands as the first complete English translation of the scriptures from the Latin Vulgate. It was produced during a period of significant [historical context](#historical-context) where the common people lacked access to the Bible. The existence of two [translation versions](#translation-versions), the EV and LV, shows the evolution of English translation toward natural readability. Despite intense [opposition and legacy](#opposition-and-legacy) challenges from the religious authorities of the time, the work of Wycliffe and his associates paved the way for future English Bibles and the spread of biblical knowledge.
