# Exploring the Greek Roots of Biblical Forgiveness

Forgiveness is a central theme of the Christian faith, yet it is often misunderstood as a single, simple action. In the original Greek of the New Testament, however, several different words are used to describe the act of forgiving, each offering a unique perspective on what it means to be released from the past. Examining these terms uncovers a deeper understanding of how God deals with humanity and how individuals are called to deal with one another. The term "forgive" in common biblical translations often carries profound implications that go beyond simple forgetting.

Different Greek words with different meanings are translated as "forgive" in modern English Bibles. This often results in readers confusing these distinct concepts with one another.

## Apoluo

The Greek word *apoluó* (meaning to loose from or set free) means to formally release someone from their obligations, debt, or imprisonment.

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/630.htm)    |
|-------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| Original Word     | ἀπολύω                                                        |
| Part of Speech    | Verb                                                          |
| Transliteration   | apoluó                                                        |
| Phonetic Spelling | (ap-ol-oo'-o)                                                 |
| Definition        | to set free, release                                          |
| Usage             | I release, let go, send away, divorce, am rid; mid: I depart. |

> * 630 apolýō (from 575 /apó, "away from" and 3089 /lýō, "to loose, release") — properly, to let go; release (discharge), dismiss. This term implies the release (annulment) of an existing bond.
> * 630 /apolýō ("to release") is specifically used of divorcing a marital partner (Matthew 1:19; 5:31,32; 19:7-9 — parallels at Mark 10:2-12; Luke 16:18).
>
> — [HELPS Word-studies](https://biblehub.com/greek/630.htm)

"apoluó" is translated as "forgive" in:

>  Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: ***forgive*** *(apoluó)*, and ye shall be ***forgiven*** *(apoluó)*. — Luke 6:37 (KJV)

In this context Jesus was referring to enemies and refraining from spitefulness (Luke 6:27-36). Jesus was asking his disciples to release their enemies from the revenge that was due to them. This is more clear in a parallel scripture recorded by Matthew 5:38-48.

Other translations of the same word "apoluó":

| translation         | scriptures                                                        |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------|
| divorce             | Matthew 1:19, 5:31,32, 19:3,7-9; Mark 10:2                        |
| send away / dismiss | Matthew 14:15,22,23, 15:23,32; Mark 6:36,45, 8:3,9               |
| release (prisoner)  | Matthew 18:27, 27:15,17,21,26                                     |

## Charizomai

The Greek word *charizomai* (meaning to show favor or give freely) goes further than just dismissing the wrong others did. It refers to the act of showing undeserved favor or kindness instead of seeking revenge.

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/5483.htm)      |
|-------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Original Word     | χαρίζομαι                                                        |
| Part of Speech    | Verb                                                             |
| Transliteration   | charizomai                                                       |
| Phonetic Spelling | (khar-id'-zom-ahee)                                              |
| Definition        | to show favor, give freely                                       |
| Usage             | (a) I show favor to, (b) I pardon, forgive, (c) I show kindness. |

> * Cognate: 5483 xarízomai (from 5485 /xáris, "grace, extending favor") — properly, to extend favor ("grace"), freely give favor to grant forgiveness (pardon).
> * 5483 /xarízomai ("favor that cancels") is used of God giving His grace to pardon. This is freely done and therefore not based on any merit of the one receiving forgiveness.
> * [5483 (xarízomai) literally means, "to exercise grace, freely show favor," i. e. willingly ("graciously") bestow.]
>
> — [HELPS Word-studies](https://biblehub.com/greek/5483.htm)

"charizomai" is translated as "forgive" in the context of:

* Forgiving your enemy: 2 Corinthians 2:7-10
* Forgiving Paul: 2 Corinthians 12:13
* Forgiving believers: Ephesians 4:32; Colossians 3:13
* Christ forgiving his disciples: Colossians 2:13, 3:13

Other translations of the same word "charizomai":

| translation           | scriptures                                        |
|-----------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| bestowed              | Luke 7:21; Philippians 2:9 (ESV)                  |
| cancelled debt        | Luke 7:42-43 (ESV)                                |
| granted               | Acts 3:14, 27:24 (ESV)                            |
| "give up" (hand over) | Acts 25:11,16; Romans 8:32 (ESV)                  |
| give / gave (freely)  | 1 Corinthians 2:12; Galatians 3:18; Philemon 1:22 |

## Aphiemi

The Greek word *aphiémi* (meaning to send away or remit) has a broader meaning which depends on the context.

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/863.htm)                                            |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Original Word     | ἀφίημι                                                                                                |
| Part of Speech    | Verb                                                                                                  |
| Transliteration   | aphiémi                                                                                               |
| Phonetic Spelling | (af-ee'-ay-mee)                                                                                       |
| Definition        | to send away, leave alone, permit                                                                     |
| Usage             | (a) I send away, (b) I let go, release, permit to depart, (c) I remit, forgive, (d) I permit, suffer. |

> **[NAS Word Usage](https://www.biblestudytools.com/lexicons/greek/nas/aphiemi.html)** — Total: 146
>
> * to send away
>   * to bid going away or depart
>     * of a husband divorcing his wife
>   * to send forth, yield up, to expire
>   * to let go, let alone, let be
>     * to disregard
>     * to leave, not to discuss now, (a topic) 1c
> * of teachers, writers and speakers
>     * to omit, neglect
>   * to let go, give up a debt, forgive, to remit
>   * to give up, keep no longer
> * to permit, allow, not to hinder, to give up a thing to a person
> * to leave, go way from one
>   * in order to go to another place
>   * to depart from any one
>   * to depart from one and leave him to himself so that all mutual claims are abandoned
>   * to desert wrongfully
>   * to go away leaving something behind
>   * to leave one by not taking him as a companion
>   * to leave on dying, leave behind one
>   * to leave so that what is left may remain, leave remaining
>   * abandon, leave destitute

The Greek word *aphiémi* is translated as "forgive" in the following contexts:

* God the Father forgiving the sins of those who repent (meaning to turn away from sin): Acts 8:22; Romans 4:7; 1 John 1:9
* God the Father forgiving the debts or trespasses (meaning sins or offenses) of those who pray: Acts 8:22; Romans 4:7; 1 John 1:9
    * This is conditional on the petitioner also forgiving others: Matthew 6:12-15; Mark 11:25; Luke 11:4
* Blasphemy (meaning to speak irrelevantly or impiously about God) against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven: Matthew 12:31-32; Mark 3:28-29
* Jesus forgiving sins on behalf of the Father: Matthew 9:2-6; Mark 2:6-10; Luke 5:20-23
* Jesus forgiving sins committed against himself: Matthew 12:32
* Jesus requesting the Father to forgive people: Luke 23:34
* Jesus' expectation that disciples continue forgiving the repeated sins of others: Matthew 18:21; Luke 17:3-4
* The power of the disciples to forgive or withhold the sins of others: John 20:23

Critics sometimes argue that only God has the authority to forgive sins (Mark 2:7), and thus the idea of disciples "forgiving" sins seems contradictory. However, many scholars interpret John 20:23 as the disciples declaring the forgiveness that God has already granted based on a person's response to the Gospel, rather than the disciples themselves being the source of that forgiveness.

Other translations of the same word "aphiémi":

| translation   | scriptures                                     |
|---------------|------------------------------------------------|
| consented     | Matthew 3:15 (ESV)                             |
| left / leave  | Matthew 4:11,20,22, 5:24, 8:15,22, 13:36 (ESV) |
| "let" (allow) | Matthew 5:40, 7:4, 13:30 (ESV)                 |

## Conclusion

Understanding the nuances of the Greek words translated as "forgive" allows for a more complete grasp of biblical reconciliation (meaning the restoration of a relationship). The term *Apoluo* emphasizes the [release from legal or personal obligations](#apoluo). In contrast, *Charizomai* focuses on the [bestowal of gracious favor](#charizomai) that goes beyond mere dismissal of a wrong. Finally, *Aphiemi* covers the broad [remission of sins and the letting go](#aphiemi) of claims. Together, these terms paint a picture of a God who not only cancels human debts but also restores people through infinite grace.
