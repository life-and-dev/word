# The Biblical Meaning of "World"

Have you ever wondered if the "end of the world" refers to the destruction of our planet or something entirely different? Many readers of the Bible are surprised to learn that the original Greek text often points to the close of an era rather than a cosmic catastrophe. This article explores how a single word can change our entire understanding of the future.

## The Conflict in Translation

The King James Version (KJV) and most Bible translations based on it often translate the Greek word "aión" (the Greek word for an age or cycle of time) as "the world." This is often misleading because in English "the world" usually refers to the physical earth or the human race. Dictionary.com provides these common definitions:

> * **the earth** or globe, considered as a planet: (often initial capital letter) a particular division of the earth
> * **the earth** or a part of it, with its inhabitants, affairs, etc., during a particular period: the ancient world
> * ...
>
> — [Dictionary.com](https://www.dictionary.com/browse/world)

## Defining the Greek Term Aion

However, Strong's Concordance (a Bible reference tool used to find the original Greek and Hebrew words) has a completely different definition for the word aion (G165):

| G165              | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/165.htm)                                                                                         |
|-------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Original Word     | αἰών, ῶνος, ὁ                                                                                                                                      |
| Part of Speech    | Noun, Masculine                                                                                                                                    |
| Transliteration   | aión                                                                                                                                               |
| Phonetic Spelling | (ahee-ohn')                                                                                                                                        |
| Definition        | a space of time, an age                                                                                                                            |
| Usage             | an age, a cycle (of time), especially of the present age as contrasted with the future age, and of one of a series of ages stretching to infinity. |

## Ages vs. Planets

These poorly translated verses are often used to argue that humanity currently needs to endure this world. The implication is often that the physical Earth is something to escape because the destination is [heaven](heaven.draft.md). For example:

> And whosoever speaketh a word against the Son of man, it shall be forgiven him;
> but whosoever speaketh against the Holy Ghost, it shall not be forgiven him, neither in this ***world** [aión]*, neither in the ***world** [aión]* to come.
>
> — Matthew 12:32 (KJV)

and

> Far above all principality, and power, and might, and dominion, and every name that is named, not only in this ***world** [aión]*, but also in that which is to come. — Ephesians 1:21 (KJV)

These passages do not suggest that a new planet is coming. Instead, they refer to the next age. Other examples where "age" is likely translated as "world" include:
* Matthew 24:3 (KJV)
* Mark 10:30 (KJV)
* Luke 16:8, 18:30, 20:34 (KJV)
* 1 Corinthians 2:6-8 (KJV)
* Hebrews 1:2, 11:3 (KJV)

## Living in the Current Age

Paul did not ask believers to act like aliens when he wrote:

> And be not conformed to this ***world** [aión]*: but be ye transformed by the renewing of your mind, that ye may prove what is that good, and acceptable, and perfect, will of God. — Romans 12:2 (KJV)

Instead, he was concerned about the matters of this current age which will pass away. People should focus their minds on the matters which have eternal value, like God's good acceptable and perfect will.

> Who gave himself for our sins, that he might deliver us from this present evil ***world** [aión]*, according to the will of God and our Father. — Galatians 1:4 (KJV)

Humanity does not live on an evil planet. People live in an age where evil reigns. Another example is found in the description of the "god of this world":

> In whom the god of this ***world** [aión]* hath blinded the minds of them which believe not, lest the light of the glorious gospel of Christ, who is the image of God, should shine unto them. — 2 Corinthians 4:4 (KJV)

## Spiritual Warfare and Sovereignty

> Unto him be glory in the church by Christ Jesus throughout all ages, ***world** [aión]* without end. — Ephesians 3:21 (KJV)

Paul intended to communicate that the reign of Christ will have no end. The word "world" in this sentence can be confusing to modern readers.

> For we wrestle not against flesh and blood, but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this ***world** [aión]*, against spiritual wickedness in high places. — Ephesians 6:12 (KJV)

While some interpret this as evil spirits having total control over the physical planet, the context of aión suggests their influence is limited to the current era. God did not hand over ownership to an evil ruler. He remains in control.

## Perspectives on Translation

Some scholars argue that "world" was an appropriate translation in the 17th century, as it could encompass the idea of a "world-age." However, in modern English, the distinction between a physical location and a period of time has become more rigid. Using "age" helps clarify that the Bible often discusses temporal dispensations rather than planetary destruction.

## Conclusion

The English word "world" often creates a [conflict in translation](#the-conflict-in-translation) when compared to the Greek term aión. This term refers to [specific periods of time](#defining-the-greek-term-aion) rather than the physical earth. Many verses commonly used to support the idea of escaping the planet actually point to the [transition between ages](#ages-vs-planets). Understanding this distinction shifts the focus from escaping an "evil planet" to [renewing the mind](#living-in-the-current-age) within the current era. While some [historical perspectives](#perspectives-on-translation) explain the choice of the word "world," it remains clear that God maintains [sovereignty over all time](#spiritual-warfare-and-sovereignty).
