# The Greek Preposition "En"

The Greek language often contains nuances that single-word English translations can overlook. One of the most common words in the New Testament is the preposition "en" (ἐν). While it is frequently translated as "in," its range of meaning is much broader. Understanding how this word functions can provide clarity on the relationship between God, Jesus, and humanity.

## The Versatility of "En"

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/1722.htm) |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------|
| Original Word     | ἐν                                                          |
| Part of Speech    | Preposition                                                 |
| Transliteration   | en                                                          |
| Phonetic Spelling | (en)                                                        |
| Definition        | in, on, at, by, with                                        |

Physical laws do not allow one person to be physically inside another person. This is only possible if the individual is a spiritual or divine being. [Trinitarian](https://son.ofgod.info/trinity) (the belief in one God in three persons) translators often choose "in" to support the idea of Jesus' divinity. They argue that Jesus can only be inside a large group of people simultaneously if he is a divine spirit.

However, the Greek word "en" also carries meanings such as "with," "among," and "by." These alternatives often fit the context more naturally.

### Translated as "With"

> When his mother Mary had been betrothed to Joseph, before they came together she was found to be **with** (ἐν) child from the Holy Spirit. — Matthew 1:18 (ESV)

> “Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel” (which means, God **with** (ἐν) us). — Matthew 1:23 (ESV)

### Translated as "Among"

> “‘And you, O Bethlehem, in the land of Judah, are by no means least **among** (ἐν) the rulers of Judah; for from you shall come a ruler who will shepherd my people Israel.’” — Matthew 2:6 (ESV)

> And he went throughout all Galilee, teaching in their synagogues and proclaiming the gospel of the kingdom and healing every disease and every affliction **among** (ἐν) the people. — Matthew 4:23 (ESV)

### Translated as "By"

> But I say to you, Do not take an oath at all, either **by** (ἐν) heaven, for it is the throne of God, or **by** (ἐν) the earth, for it is his footstool, or **by** (ἐν) Jerusalem, for it is the city of the great King. — Matthew 5:34-35 (ESV)

The use of "by" in this context makes logical sense. The audience was not literally "in" heaven at the time.

There are [many more examples](https://biblehub.com/greek/en_1722.htm) where "en" refers to something other than location.

## Fellowship and Communion

When Jesus speaks of being "in" his followers, he often refers to close communion and fellowship. This relationship is often described using covenant language (formal agreements between God and His people). In this context, being "in" means being a participant in that agreement. It indicates being part of the same family of God and living by his example as his representatives on Earth.

> When used in the sense of “in God,” or “in Christ,” the word “in” refers to a close communion, a tight fellowship. It was part of the covenant language of the day, when people spoke of being either “in” or “cut off from” the covenant.
> — Morgridge (pp. 116 to 117)
> — Racovian Catechism (pp. 142 to 143)
> — [Spirit & Truth Fellowship International](https://www.biblicalunitarian.com/verses/john-14-11)

## Theological Implications

Understanding these translation options changes how one reads passages like John 14-17 (ESV).

* If "en" is always translated as "in," it implies a literal or mystical "inside" relationship. Followers are seen as "inside" God and Jesus, while they are also "inside" their followers. This paradox is used to argue for the omnipresence of Jesus. However, this view can conflict with his own statements about going away and returning. These interpretations often make the text difficult to grasp.
* If "en" is understood as "with," the focus shifts to unity. It describes God, Jesus, and the disciples living in harmony because of their love for one another.

Critics might argue that "in" is necessary to describe a mystical union that transcends physical limitations. However, many scholars point out that the primary goal of these passages is to emphasize relational unity rather than metaphysical location.

## Conclusion

The Greek preposition "en" is a [versatile word](#the-versatility-of-en) with a wide range of meanings. While it is often translated as "in," it can also mean "with," "among," or "by" depending on the [context](#the-versatility-of-en). Proper translation emphasizes [fellowship and communion](#fellowship-and-communion) over physical location. Recognizing these nuances clarifies the [theological implications](#theological-implications) of many New Testament passages.
