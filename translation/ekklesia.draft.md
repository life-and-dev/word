# Ekklesia (Greek)

The term "church" is one of the most recognizable words in the Christian lexicon, yet the Greek term it translates, *ekklesia*, carries a rich history that predates the New Testament. In ancient times, this word was used in secular contexts before being adopted by biblical authors. Understanding the linguistic roots of *ekklesia* and how it was understood in the first century provides valuable context for how we view the assembly of believers today.

## Linguistic Definition

The Greek word that is translated as "church" in English translations of the Bible is defined as follows:

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/1577.htm) |
| ----------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Original Word     | ἐκκλησία, ας, ἡ                                             |
| Part of Speech    | Noun, Feminine                                              |
| Transliteration   | ekklésia                                                    |
| Phonetic Spelling | ek-klay-see'-ah                                             |
| Definition        | an assembly, a (religious) congregation                     |

## Traditional Institutional View

Traditional [Christianity](https://church.ofgod.info/terms/christian) often views a [church](https://church.ofgod.info) as a structured local congregation. In this institutional model, the primary focus is on corporate worship, prayer, financial support through [tithing](https://church.ofgod.info/terms/tithing), and maintaining organizational systems. These systems frequently support internal programs. Such programs include concerts, literature, schools, camps, and seminars to benefit the membership. While many congregations offer fellowship groups and counseling, some observers suggest that these activities can become inwardly focused, prioritizing the needs of their own members over external outreach.

## Active Assembly View

An alternative perspective focuses on the etymological roots of the Greek word. The term *ekklesia* is derived from the preposition [ek](https://biblehub.com/greek/1537.htm), which indicates the point from which an action proceeds, and the verb [kaleo](https://biblehub.com/greek/2564.htm), which means to call forth. Under this view, [Christ](https://kingdom.ofgod.info/christ) intended the assembly to be a dynamic gathering of disciples. These disciples represent [his body](https://kingdom.ofgod.info/christ/body). They are sent out from that source to carry forward his [calling](https://ofgod.info/calling). In the physical absence of Jesus, his body of disciples is expected to continue the work that he initiated. Paul writes about this unified calling in his letter to the Ephesians:

> There is [one](echad.md) **body** and [one](echad.md) Spirit—just as **you were called** to the [one](echad.md) hope that belongs to **your call** — Ephesians 4:4 (ESV)

[The "body"](https://kingdom.ofgod.info/christ/body) must submit to God to fulfill this calling. To support this view, proponents point to verses where Jesus sends his disciples into the world to act on his behalf. For example, Jesus commissioned his disciples to go and make disciples of all nations, as shown in Matthew 28:19-20. 

Jesus also states that he is sending his disciples just as the Father sent him:

> Jesus said to them again, "Peace be with you. As the Father has sent me, even so I am sending you." — John 20:21 (ESV)

The apostle Paul similarly reinforces this concept by describing believers as members of a single, functioning body:

> For as in one body we have many members, and the members do not all have the same function, so we, though many, are one body in Christ, and individually members one of another. — Romans 12:4-5 (ESV)

## Critical Perspectives and Defenses

Linguists and historians raise significant objections to the etymological argument for *ekklesia*. A common critique is the etymological fallacy. This fallacy is the assumption that a word always retains the meaning of its component parts. In classical Greek, the word *ekklesia* refers to a political assembly of citizens called out of their private homes to vote on public matters in a civic assembly. The word was not a theological term. It denoted a legal gathering of citizens. The author of the Acts of the Apostles uses the word in this secular sense when describing a confused civic assembly in Ephesus in Acts 19:32 (ESV) and a regular assembly in Acts 19:39 (ESV).

Furthermore, when the Hebrew scriptures were translated into Greek in the Septuagint, *ekklesia* was used to translate the Hebrew word *qahal*, which simply means an assembly or congregation of the people of Israel. For example, Psalm 22:22 (ESV) uses the term to describe praising God in the midst of the congregation. The author of Hebrews later quotes this verse in Hebrews 2:12 (ESV) using *ekklesia*. Therefore, critics argue that the New Testament writers chose the word *ekklesia* because it was the standard translation for a congregation or assembly, not to convey a specific theological message about proceeding out of a source.

Defenders of the active assembly view respond that while the etymological root fallacy is a valid warning in general linguistics, the overall theological context of the New Testament does emphasize an active commission. They argue that the early believers did not view themselves as a passive audience. They understood their role as an active body sent to continue the ministry of Jesus. Proponents argue that the choice of *ekklesia* rather than other terms for passive gatherings highlights the active nature of their assembly.

## Conclusion

Linguistic and contextual analysis of the term *ekklesia* reveals different understandings of the Christian congregation. The [Linguistic Definition](#linguistic-definition) establishes the basic meaning of the word as an assembly. While the [Traditional Institutional View](#traditional-institutional-view) focuses on a localized and organized body, the [Active Assembly View](#active-assembly-view) emphasizes the dynamic commission of the disciples.
