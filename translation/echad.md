# Echad (Hebrew) / Heis (Greek)

The concept of God's oneness is central to monotheistic faith, yet the specific words used in ancient scriptures carry nuances that spark deep theological discussions. How do the Hebrew and Greek terms for "one" shape our understanding of the divine nature? Exploring these linguistic roots reveals different perspectives on whether oneness implies an absolute mathematical singularity or a composite unity.

Biblical Unitarians argue that [there is only one God](https://son.ofgod.info/shema). However, [Trinitarians](https://son.ofgod.info/trinity) argue that "one" actually means "unified", which hints to a [Godhead](https://son.ofgod.info/godhead) of three united members.

## Composite Unity Argument

Trinitarians argue that the Hebrew word *echad* can refer to a composite unity where multiple parts form a single unit. For example, *echad* describes a unified whole in Genesis:

> Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one *[echad]* flesh. — Genesis 2:24 (ESV)

Based on this usage, Trinitarians apply the same "unified" meaning to *echad* in prophetic and theological declarations:

> "And the LORD will be king over all the earth; in that day the LORD will be the only one *[echad]*, and His name the only one *[echad]*." — Zechariah 14:9 (NASB)

and

> "Hear, O Israel! Yahweh our God, Yahweh is one *[echad]*!" — Deuteronomy 6:4 (LSB)

## Numerical Singularity in Greek

However, when Jesus quoted Deuteronomy 6:4, the Greek translation in the New Testament uses the word *heis*, which denotes a single or numerical one:

> And Jesus answered him, The first of all the commandments is, Hear, O Israel; The Lord our God is one *[heis]* Lord. — Mark 12:29 (KJV)

Another example of *heis* is found in the letter to Timothy:

> For there is one *[heis]* God, and there is one *[heis]* mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus... — 1 Timothy 2:5 (ESV)

This verse uses *heis* for God's oneness, and also refers to Jesus as the mediator. The same word cannot have two different meanings in the same sentence. If *heis* meant "united," then the mediator must also consist of multiple members. This would mean there is more than one mediator between God and humanity, which contradicts Jesus' statement in John 14:6.

## Numerical Singularity in Hebrew

Another passage where *echad* denotes a single, non-composite entity is in the book of Malachi:

> Have we not all one *[echad]* Father? Has not one *[echad]* God created us? Why then are we faithless to one another, profaning the covenant of our fathers? — Malachi 2:10 (ESV)

In the same sentence, *echad* is used for both the Father and God. Therefore, *echad* must carry the same meaning for both. If *echad* meant a unified or composite one, it would mean that the Father is also a composite of multiple members. Malachi argued that his people come from the same source (one Father, one Creator), yet they act faithlessly to one another.

## Contextual and Scholarly Analysis

The standard usage of *echad* in the Hebrew Bible represents a single numerical entity. While composite unity is a possible meaning under specific contextual conditions (such as the joining of husband and wife), numerical oneness remains the primary and most common meaning. For example, *echad* is used for simple numerical oneness in:
- Genesis 42:11,13 where the brothers refer to themselves as sons of "one" (*echad*) man, and note that "one" (*echad*) of their brothers is no more.
- Exodus 26:6 where the curtains are coupled together so that the tabernacle forms "one" (*echad*) structure.

Jason Dulle argues that context determines the meaning of the word:

> Echad is used nearly 1000 times in the OT, and almost always refers to a single numerical entity.  There are times when it is used of a composite entity (Genesis 2:24).  It functions just like the English word “one,” which can be used of single or composite entities, although it most often refers to a single, solitary thing.  Only the context can determine how echad is being used.  Given the rarity with which echad is used to refer to a composite entity, we should understand echad as referring to a single entity unless there are good contextual clues that warrant the uncommon meaning.  So given the lexical data alone, the best one could argue is that the semantical domain of echad allows for a Trinitarian understanding of “one,” but by no means does it prove it, and by no means does it rule out the understanding of God’s oneness as an absolute unity.
>
> The question, then, comes down to context.  Is there anything in the context of Deuteronomy 6:4 – or any other passage of Scripture in which God is described as being echad – that requires the meaning of composite unity?  Meaning is not determined by a words semantical domain, but by the context.  To demonstrate that echad means a composite entity with reference to God, the context must make it clear that this is the meaning intended by the author.  For example, in Genesis 2:24 man and woman are described as being “echad flesh.”  It is physically impossible for man and woman to be considered a single physical entity, so the author must mean “one” in the sense of a composite entity.  Are there similar contextual clues that make it clear that echad is being used in this way in Deuteronomy 6:4?  No.  Indeed, given how God’s oneness is described in passages like Isaiah 42:8 and 44:24, we have very good grounds for understanding the nature of God’s oneness to be that of a numerically single entity.
>
> ...
>
> Yachid is also used to describe the emotion of feeling alone (Psalm 25:16) or being alone (Psalm 68:6), and even the uniqueness or precious nature of something (Psalm 22:21; 35:17).  The word is never used as a general term for “one.”  Its meaning is more akin to “unique” or “only.”  Indeed, Isaac is described as Abraham’s yachid even though Isaac was not his only son (Ishmael was born earlier).  While God could have been described using yachid, it would not necessarily tell us how many gods there are, but rather what kind of God YHWH is: a unique God.  If we want to know how many gods there are, the most appropriate word is echad. — [Jason Dulle](https://www.onenesspentecostal.com/echad.htm)

## Critical Perspectives and Defenses

Critics of the Unitarian interpretation argue that because the New Testament writers quoted the Greek Septuagint translation of Deuteronomy 6:4, they used *heis* simply as the standard translation of *echad* without intending to rule out any composite nature of the Godhead. They assert that the full biblical revelation of God as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit must inform how the word "one" is understood, rather than relying strictly on lexical definitions of *heis* or *echad*.

In response, defenders of the Unitarian view point out that the Greek language has specific words and grammatical structures to express composite unity or shared purpose (such as the neuter *hen* in John 10:30). The consistent use of the masculine singular *heis* in the New Testament declarations of God's oneness (e.g., Mark 12:29; 1 Timothy 2:5) emphasizes a singular, personal identity. Furthermore, they argue that when Jesus agreed with the scribe in Mark 12:32-34 that "God is one and there is no other but Him," he confirmed that the Jewish understanding of absolute numerical singularity was correct.

## Conclusion

Linguistic analysis shows that biblical words for "one" have different implications. The [Linguistic Definitions](#linguistic-definitions) indicate that *echad* and *heis* carry a primarily numerical meaning, while *hen* is used for unified purpose. The [Composite Unity Argument](#composite-unity-argument) relies on specific, plural-subject contexts to argue for a composite Godhead, but both [Numerical Singularity in Greek](#numerical-singularity-in-greek) and [Numerical Singularity in Hebrew](#numerical-singularity-in-hebrew) provide strong evidence for God as a single, indivisible entity. Through [Contextual and Scholarly Analysis](#contextual-and-scholarly-analysis), it is clear that numerical oneness is the default meaning, which is further supported by [Critical Perspectives and Defenses](#critical-perspectives-and-defenses) clarifying the debate for readers to examine.
