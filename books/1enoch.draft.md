# 1 Enoch

## Authorship

The Book of 1 Enoch is traditionally attributed to Enoch, the seventh patriarch from Adam mentioned briefly in the Hebrew Bible (Genesis 5:18-24). According to Genesis, Enoch "walked with God" and was taken by God without experiencing death, having lived 365 years. This mysterious figure became the focal point of extensive Jewish apocalyptic literature, with 1 Enoch being the most significant example.

However, modern scholarly consensus unanimously identifies 1 Enoch as [pseudepigraphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudepigrapha) literature—that is, writings attributed to a venerated ancient figure but actually composed by later, anonymous authors. According to Ephraim Isaac, editor and translator in *The Old Testament Pseudepigrapha*, 1 Enoch is "clearly composite representing numerous periods and writers." The earliest known fragments of 1 Enoch, discovered among the [Dead Sea Scrolls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls) at Qumran, date to approximately 200-150 BCE based on paleographic analysis. This dating makes it chronologically impossible for the biblical Enoch (who would have lived millennia earlier) to be the actual author.

The work is not a unified composition but rather a collection of five distinct literary units, each with its own authorship, dating, and theological emphases. These five sections are:

1. **The Book of the Watchers** (chapters 1-36) - The oldest section, with fragments dating to 200-150 BCE, though the work likely existed in some form in the 3rd century BCE
2. **The Book of Parables/Similitudes** (chapters 37-71) - The most controversial section regarding dating; absent from the Dead Sea Scrolls, suggesting a later composition, possibly after 40 BCE or even as late as the 1st century CE
3. **The Astronomical Book** (chapters 72-82) - Like the Book of the Watchers, this section dates to the 3rd-2nd century BCE based on Qumran fragments
4. **The Book of Dream Visions** (chapters 83-90) - Generally dated to the Maccabean period (c. 165-160 BCE)
5. **The Epistle of Enoch** (chapters 91-108) - Dated to approximately 105-100 BCE

George W. E. Nickelsburg, a leading scholar on 1 Enoch, writes that "1 Enoch is a collection of Jewish apocalyptic traditions that date from the last three centuries before the common era." The consensus view is that these five books were originally independent works by different authors, themselves products of considerable editorial arrangement, and were only later redacted together into what is now known as 1 Enoch. This final redaction likely occurred during the book's transmission in Greek, when an editor compiled the various treatises of Enochic literature.

One widely accepted theory regarding the authorship is that much of 1 Enoch was written by members of the [Essenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essenes), a Jewish sectarian movement that flourished from the 2nd century BCE to the 1st century CE. The discovery of multiple copies of 1 Enoch among the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran—a site associated with the Essenes—lends support to this theory. The texts found at Qumran include fragments from all sections of 1 Enoch except the Book of Parables, which has led scholars to conclude that this section was either composed later or was not accepted by the Qumran community.

The actual identities of the authors remain unknown. What is clear is that these writers were learned Jewish scribes deeply versed in Hebrew scripture, apocalyptic traditions, and the theological concerns of Second Temple Judaism. They chose to write under the name of Enoch likely because of his biblical reputation as one who had special knowledge of divine mysteries and heavenly realms, making him an ideal spokesperson for revelatory literature about cosmology, angelology, eschatology, and divine judgment.

## Historical Environment

The Book of 1 Enoch emerged during the [Second Temple period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Temple_period), one of the most formative and turbulent eras in Jewish history. This period spans approximately 600 years (516 BCE – 70 CE), beginning with the return from Babylonian exile and the rebuilding of the Jerusalem Temple, and ending with the Roman destruction of the Second Temple. The various sections of 1 Enoch were composed during a critical subset of this era, primarily from the 3rd century BCE through the 1st century CE, reflecting the dramatic political, cultural, and religious changes that transformed Judaism during this time.

### Political and Cultural Context

The earliest sections of 1 Enoch (the Book of the Watchers and the Astronomical Book) were written during the early Hellenistic period, following Alexander the Great's conquest of the Persian Empire (332 BCE). After Alexander's death, his empire was divided among his generals, resulting in ongoing conflicts between the Ptolemaic dynasty in Egypt and the [Seleucid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seleucid_Empire) based in Syria. The land of Judea found itself caught between these rival powers, changing hands multiple times and experiencing increasing Hellenistic cultural influence.

This Hellenization—the spread of Greek language, culture, philosophy, and religious practices—became a source of profound tension within Jewish society. Some Jews, particularly among the aristocracy and priesthood, embraced Greek culture and sought to modernize Jewish practice. Others, traditionalists who would later include movements like the Essenes and Pharisees, viewed Hellenization as a threat to Jewish identity and covenant faithfulness. The Book of 1 Enoch reflects this tension, particularly in its emphasis on divine judgment against those who abandon God's laws and follow foreign customs.

### The Maccabean Crisis

The most dramatic historical event reflected in 1 Enoch is the [Maccabean Revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maccabean_Revolt) (168-160 BCE). In 175 BCE, [Antiochus IV Epiphanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiochus_IV_Epiphanes) became king of the Seleucid Empire. His policies toward the Jews escalated from cultural pressure to outright persecution. In 167 BCE, Antiochus outlawed Jewish religious practices, desecrated the Jerusalem Temple by erecting an altar to Zeus and sacrificing pigs, and executed Jews who refused to abandon their traditions. This period became known as the "abomination of desolation" and sparked the Jewish revolt led by Judas Maccabeus and his family.

The Book of Dream Visions (chapters 83-90) was composed during this crisis period, around 165-160 BCE. This section contains the "Animal Apocalypse," a symbolic retelling of Israel's history that culminates in events clearly referencing the Maccabean Revolt. The apocalyptic framework of 1 Enoch—its emphasis on divine intervention, the coming judgment of the wicked, and the vindication of the righteous—provided theological hope to Jews suffering under Seleucid oppression. At the very moment when traditional covenantal theology seemed to be failing (the wicked were prospering while the righteous suffered), apocalyptic literature like 1 Enoch assured readers that God remained in control and would soon reverse the cosmic order.

### The Qumran Community and Sectarian Judaism

The discovery of eleven Aramaic manuscripts of 1 Enoch among the [Dead Sea Scrolls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls) at Qumran (all fragments found in Cave 4, representing all sections except the Book of Parables) demonstrates the profound importance of this text to at least one significant Jewish community. Most scholars identify the Qumran community with the Essenes or a radical subset of that movement—a priestly sect that separated from mainstream Judaism, particularly from the Jerusalem Temple establishment.

The [Qumran community](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qumran) rejected participation in Temple sacrifices, which they considered polluted by the illegitimate priesthood controlling Jerusalem. According to some scholars, these were likely Zadokite priests who believed that the current high priests were following the wrong calendar, maintaining improper purity rules, and officiating incorrectly before the Lord. Significantly, the Qumran community adopted the 364-day solar calendar promoted in 1 Enoch's Astronomical Book, rather than the lunisolar calendar used by the Jerusalem Temple. This calendrical disagreement had profound implications, as it meant the community celebrated festivals and holy days at different times than other Jews, fundamentally separating them from mainstream practice.

Some scholars, including Gabriele Boccaccini, argue that the Qumran community was part of a broader "Enochic Judaism"—a stream of Jewish thought that gave special authority to Enochic literature and its distinctive theology, particularly its developed angelology, demonology, and deterministic views of history. The prominence of 1 Enoch at Qumran (with more copies found than most biblical books) suggests it held quasi-canonical status for this community.

### Geographic and Social Setting

The authors and audience of 1 Enoch were Palestinian Jews, likely centered in or around Jerusalem, though the text found a receptive audience among diaspora Jews as well (evidenced by its preservation in Greek and later Ethiopic translations after it fell out of use in rabbinic Judaism). The social location of the authors appears to be among learned scribes and priests who possessed detailed knowledge of scripture, astronomical calculations, and apocalyptic traditions.

The intended audience seems to be pious Jews who were troubled by the prosperity of the wicked, the suffering of the righteous, and the apparent delay of divine justice. The repeated emphasis on the coming judgment, the fate of sinners, and the reward of the righteous suggests readers facing persecution, marginalization, or at least deep dissatisfaction with the current religious and political order. The text addresses those who might be tempted to abandon covenant faithfulness in the face of Hellenistic pressure or the success of Jewish apostates who had embraced Greek culture.

### Dating Summary by Section

The historical environment varied for each section:
- **Book of the Watchers & Astronomical Book** (3rd-2nd century BCE): Early Hellenistic period, growing Greek cultural influence
- **Book of Dream Visions** (c. 165-160 BCE): Height of the Maccabean crisis under Antiochus IV Epiphanes
- **Epistle of Enoch** (c. 105-100 BCE): Post-Maccabean period, Hasmonean rule
- **Book of Parables** (1st century BCE to possibly 1st century CE): Roman period, date debated

This extended period of composition means that 1 Enoch reflects the evolving concerns of Jewish communities facing Hellenization, persecution, political oppression, and questions about theodicy and divine justice over several centuries.

## Purpose

The Book of 1 Enoch serves multiple interconnected purposes, addressing some of the most pressing theological, cosmological, and practical questions facing Second Temple Judaism. As a composite work spanning several centuries, different sections address different concerns, but all share common themes related to theodicy, divine justice, cosmological order, and the ultimate fate of humanity.

### Explaining the Origin and Persistence of Evil (Theodicy)

Perhaps the most fundamental purpose of 1 Enoch is to provide a comprehensive explanation for the existence and intensity of evil in the world—a question that troubled ancient Jews who believed in an omnipotent, benevolent God. The book's answer is found primarily in the Book of the Watchers (chapters 1-36), which dramatically expands on the cryptic reference in Genesis 6:1-4 to the "sons of God" taking human wives.

According to 1 Enoch, two hundred angels called "Watchers," led by Shemihazah and Azazel, descended to Mount Hermon and rebelled against God by taking human wives and revealing forbidden heavenly secrets to humanity. Their offspring were the Nephilim—giants who consumed humanity's resources and eventually turned to cannibalism. This angelic rebellion introduced corruption, violence, idolatry, witchcraft, warfare, and sexual immorality into the world. By attributing evil's origin to rebellious supernatural beings rather than to God or purely to human nature, 1 Enoch absolves God of responsibility for evil while explaining its supernatural intensity and persistence.

This theodicy served to reassure readers that evil was not part of God's original creation nor evidence of divine weakness, but rather the temporary result of cosmic rebellion that God would ultimately judge and eliminate.

### Vindicating the Righteous and Warning the Wicked

A central concern throughout 1 Enoch is the apparent prosperity of the wicked and suffering of the righteous—a classic problem in Jewish wisdom literature (compare Job, certain Psalms, and Ecclesiastes). The book repeatedly addresses communities of faithful Jews who were being oppressed, marginalized, or tempted to apostasy. The apocalyptic framework provides assurance that current circumstances do not reflect the final reality.

The Epistle of Enoch (chapters 91-108) particularly emphasizes the coming reversal of fortunes. The righteous who currently suffer will be vindicated, rewarded, and elevated, while the wicked who currently prosper will face eternal judgment. This message provided encouragement to remain faithful despite persecution or the temptation to compromise with Hellenistic culture. The book functions as both comfort to the oppressed and warning to potential apostates.

The Book of Parables (chapters 37-71) develops this theme by introducing the figure of the "Chosen One" or "Son of Man" who will execute God's judgment, dethrone earthly kings and rulers, and establish divine justice. This messianic figure provides hope that God has not abandoned history but will intervene decisively.

### Providing Hope During Persecution and Crisis

Written during periods of intense pressure—from Hellenistic cultural dominance to outright persecution under Antiochus IV Epiphanes—1 Enoch functions as "resistance literature." The Book of Dream Visions (chapters 83-90), composed during the Maccabean crisis, uses symbolic animal imagery to retell Israel's history culminating in the contemporary persecution. This section assures readers that their current suffering fits within God's sovereign plan and will soon culminate in divine intervention and the establishment of God's kingdom.

The apocalyptic genre itself serves this purpose: by revealing that current earthly circumstances are temporary and that heavenly realities will soon break through, the text encourages perseverance and faithfulness. The repeated visions of judgment, the punishment of oppressors (both human and angelic), and the reward of the faithful provided psychological and theological resources for enduring persecution.

### Preserving Correct Cosmological and Astronomical Knowledge

The Astronomical Book (chapters 72-82), also called the Book of the Heavenly Luminaries, serves a distinctive purpose: to reveal the true structure of the cosmos and the correct calendar for calculating holy days and festivals. This section presents detailed information about the movements of the sun, moon, and stars, the gates through which they pass at the edges of the earth, and the proper calculation of time.

Crucially, this section promotes a 364-day solar calendar (52 weeks of exactly 7 days, with all festivals falling on the same day of the week each year) as opposed to the lunar-solar calendar used by the Jerusalem Temple establishment. This calendar is presented not as human wisdom but as divine revelation given to Enoch by the angel Uriel. The purpose is both theological and practical: using the wrong calendar means celebrating festivals on the wrong days, which constitutes disobedience to God's cosmic order.

For communities like the Qumran sect, this calendrical disagreement was fundamental enough to justify separating from the Jerusalem Temple. The Astronomical Book thus serves to legitimate alternative religious practice based on claims to superior heavenly knowledge.

### Developing Angelology and Demonology

1 Enoch provides one of the most detailed and influential accounts of angelic and demonic beings in ancient Jewish literature. It names specific archangels (Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Uriel) and assigns them specific functions in God's cosmic administration. It also provides names and roles for fallen angels and explains the origin of demons as the spirits of the deceased Nephilim.

This developed angelology serves several purposes: it provides a framework for understanding intermediate spiritual beings between God and humanity; it explains spiritual warfare and temptation; it offers a cosmic context for understanding evil as an organized supernatural conspiracy rather than random human wickedness; and it establishes a hierarchy of spiritual authority. This angelological framework would prove enormously influential on later Jewish and Christian thought.

### Promoting Wisdom and Ethical Living

Beyond its apocalyptic visions, 1 Enoch contains significant wisdom material, particularly in the Epistle of Enoch. These sections exhort readers to righteous living, condemn specific sins (oppression of the poor, dishonest business practices, blasphemy, idolatry), and promise rewards for those who follow wisdom. The book presents Enoch as a wisdom teacher who has access to divine mysteries and cosmic secrets, making him an authoritative source for both ethical instruction and revealed knowledge.

The purpose here is practical: to shape the behavior and worldview of the community, distinguishing between righteous and wicked conduct, and providing motivation (both positive rewards and negative consequences) for choosing the path of righteousness.

### Warning Against Apostasy and Hellenization

Implicitly throughout and explicitly in certain sections, 1 Enoch warns against abandoning Jewish tradition and covenant faithfulness. The repeated condemnations of those who "alter the words of truth" and "lead many astray" likely refer to Jewish Hellenizers who compromised traditional practices. The emphasis on revealed cosmic order, angelic hierarchies, and coming judgment functions to reinforce traditional Jewish distinctiveness against the pressure to assimilate into Hellenistic culture.

### Summary of Purposes by Section

Different sections emphasize different purposes:
- **Book of the Watchers**: Theodicy, origin of evil, angelology
- **Book of Parables**: Messianic hope, vindication of righteous, judgment of oppressors
- **Astronomical Book**: Correct calendar, cosmological knowledge, legitimating alternative practice
- **Book of Dream Visions**: Hope during persecution, God's control of history
- **Epistle of Enoch**: Ethical instruction, warning to wicked, encouragement to righteous

Collectively, these purposes address a community (or communities) facing external persecution, internal temptation to apostasy, theological questions about evil and justice, and practical questions about how to remain faithful to God in difficult circumstances. The book provides both comfort and challenge, both cosmic explanation and practical guidance, both future hope and present ethical demands.
