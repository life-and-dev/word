# Matthew

## Authorship

The question of who wrote the Gospel of Matthew has generated substantial scholarly debate, with traditional Christian attribution to the apostle Matthew contrasting with modern critical scholarship's majority view of anonymous authorship. This debate involves both external testimony from early church fathers and internal evidence from the Gospel itself.

**Early Church Testimony**: The earliest explicit attribution of this Gospel to Matthew comes from Papias of Hierapolis (c. 60-130 CE), bishop and Apostolic Father, whose testimony is preserved by Eusebius of Caesarea (circa 320 CE). Eusebius quotes Papias as stating that "Matthew compiled the Oracles [logia] in the Hebrew language, and everyone translated them as well as he could." This cryptic statement has generated considerable interpretive debate: Does "Oracles" refer to the complete Gospel, a collection of Jesus' sayings, or an earlier Aramaic/Hebrew source? Does Papias refer to our canonical Greek Matthew, or to a different composition? Conservative scholars often argue that Papias refers to an original Hebrew/Aramaic Gospel that Matthew composed, which was subsequently translated into the Greek text we possess. Critical scholars question whether Papias actually knew the authorship firsthand or was passing along later tradition.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130-202 CE), writing around 180 CE in *Against Heresies* 3.1.1, provides more explicit testimony: "Matthew also issued a written Gospel among the Hebrews in their own dialect while Peter and Paul were preaching at Rome and laying the foundations of the church." Irenaeus, who had personal connection to Polycarp (who knew the Apostle John), represents an important link in the chain of tradition. His testimony places Matthew's composition during the ministries of Peter and Paul in Rome (c. 60-67 CE), suggesting an earlier date than most modern scholars accept. All surviving Greek manuscripts with intact superscriptions bear the title "According to Matthew" (ΚΑΤΑ ΜΑΘΘΑΙΟΝ), demonstrating universal early attribution to Matthew in the manuscript tradition.

**Traditional Authorship Arguments**: Conservative scholarship defends Matthean authorship based on several considerations: (1) The unanimous early church tradition attributing the Gospel to Matthew, with no competing tradition suggesting alternative authorship; (2) The Gospel's detailed knowledge of Jewish customs, geography, and Scripture, consistent with a Jewish author; (3) The emphasis on financial matters and specific monetary references (such as [Matthew 17:24-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#24) regarding the temple tax), potentially reflecting Matthew's background as a tax collector; (4) The inclusion of the calling narrative in [Matthew 9:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#9) where Jesus calls "Matthew" (compared to "Levi" in Mark and Luke), possibly indicating authorial modesty or self-reference; (5) The lack of compelling reason why the early church would attribute authorship to a relatively minor apostle rather than a more prominent figure if the attribution were fabricated.

**Critical Scholarship's Anonymous Author Theory**: The majority of contemporary New Testament scholars conclude that the Gospel was composed by an anonymous Jewish-Christian author, probably not an eyewitness to Jesus' ministry. Primary arguments include: (1) The Gospel shows clear literary dependence on Mark's Gospel (using approximately 90% of Mark's content with stylistic improvements), which seems unlikely if the author were himself an eyewitness who could rely on personal memory; (2) The sophisticated Greek literary structure, careful arrangement of material into five major discourse sections (paralleling the Pentateuch), and polished style suggest an educated writer working decades after the events rather than a Galilean tax collector writing in the 60s; (3) The Gospel reflects concerns of a later Jewish-Christian community dealing with separation from the synagogue and defining its identity, suggesting composition after 70 CE; (4) The use of the title "Matthew" may simply reflect scribal tradition or association with Matthean source material (perhaps the "logia" mentioned by Papias) rather than complete Gospel authorship.

**Dating Considerations**: Traditional scholarship, following Papias and Irenaeus, dates Matthew to the 60s CE, before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. Advocates point to [Matthew 24:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#2) as genuine prophecy rather than *vaticinium ex eventu* (prophecy after the fact), and note the Gospel's emphasis on Jewish temple practices that would have diminished relevance after 70 CE. The majority of critical scholars date Matthew between 80-95 CE, arguing that: (1) The Gospel reflects the final separation between church and synagogue occurring around 85-90 CE; (2) Matthew's use of Mark (typically dated around 70 CE) as a source requires a later date; (3) The description of Jerusalem's destruction in [Matthew 22:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#7) appears to reflect knowledge of the actual historical event in 70 CE. A minority of scholars (including N.T. Wright, John Wenham, and John A.T. Robinson) challenge late dating, arguing that evidence for Markan priority is not conclusive, that Matthew could date to the 40s-50s, and that the supposed "later" concerns actually reflect first-generation Jewish-Christian tensions.

**Compositional Process and Sources**: Beyond the question of final authorship, scholars debate Matthew's compositional process. The prevailing "Two-Source Hypothesis" proposes that Matthew used two primary sources: Mark's Gospel and a hypothetical sayings source designated "Q" (from German *Quelle*, "source"), shared with Luke but not found in Mark. Additionally, Matthew contains material unique to this Gospel (designated "M"), including the infancy narrative ([Matthew 1-2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1)), several parables (the weeds, the hidden treasure, the pearl of great value, the net, the unforgiving servant, the workers in the vineyard, the two sons, the wedding feast, the ten virgins, the talents, the sheep and the goats), and portions of the Sermon on the Mount. Whether "M" represents written sources, oral tradition, or the author's own composition remains debated. Some scholars (particularly advocates of Matthean priority like the Griesbach hypothesis) argue that Mark used Matthew, not vice versa, thus eliminating the primary argument against apostolic authorship.

**Conclusion on Authorship**: The authorship question ultimately divides along methodological lines. Those who grant substantial weight to early church testimony and allow for the possibility of eyewitness authorship despite use of written sources tend to affirm traditional Matthean authorship. Those who prioritize internal literary and historical evidence, assuming redactional activity and later dating, lean toward anonymous authorship by a Jewish-Christian scribe writing for a specific community context. Both positions acknowledge that the Gospel presents itself as authoritative apostolic testimony about Jesus Christ, and both traditions affirm its inspiration and canonical status regardless of the specific identity of the human author. The early church's universal attribution to Matthew, preserved in all manuscript traditions, testifies to the church's conviction that this Gospel authentically represents Matthew's testimony—whether through direct composition, superintendence of a scribe, or preservation of distinctly Matthean source material that formed the Gospel's foundation.

## Historical Environment

The Gospel of Matthew emerged during a critical period of transition for both Judaism and the nascent Christian movement. Understanding the historical, social, and religious environment that shaped this Gospel is essential for grasping its distinctive emphases and theological concerns.

**The Judean-Roman Context**: Matthew's Gospel is set during the final years of the Second Temple period, when Palestine existed under Roman occupation. The narrative opens during the reign of Herod the Great (37-4 BCE), the Idumean client-king appointed by Rome ([Matthew 2:1-22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#1)). Herod's paranoid brutality—exemplified in the massacre of Bethlehem's infants ([Matthew 2:16-18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#16))—illustrates the oppressive political environment into which Jesus was born. Following Herod's death, Palestine was divided among his sons: Archelaus ruled Judea, Samaria, and Idumea (4 BCE - 6 CE, [Matthew 2:22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#22)); Herod Antipas governed Galilee and Perea (4 BCE - 39 CE, appearing in [Matthew 14:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#1) regarding John the Baptist's execution); and Philip ruled territories northeast of Galilee. After Archelaus's removal, Judea came under direct Roman rule through prefects, most notoriously Pontius Pilate (26-36 CE), who appears prominently in the passion narrative ([Matthew 27:1-26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#1)).

**Jewish Religious and Social Structure**: First-century Judaism was characterized by diverse sects and movements, several of which appear in Matthew's narrative. The Pharisees, a lay movement emphasizing oral Torah and purity observance, receive extensive (often critical) attention throughout Matthew ([Matthew 3:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#7); [5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20); [9:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#11), [34](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#34); [12:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#2), [14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#14), [24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#24), [38](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#38); [15:1-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#1); [16:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#1); [19:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#3); chapters [22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#1)). The Sadducees, representing the aristocratic priestly establishment centered in the Jerusalem temple, appear in debates about resurrection ([Matthew 3:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#7); [16:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#1); [22:23-33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#23)). The scribes (grammateis), experts in Jewish law, frequently appear alongside Pharisees as Jesus' opponents ([Matthew 5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20); [7:29](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#29); [9:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#3); [12:38](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#38); [15:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#1); [23:2-36](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#2)).

**Messianic Expectation and Political Hope**: First-century Judaism nurtured intense messianic expectations, anticipating God's intervention to deliver Israel from oppression and restore the Davidic kingdom. Various messianic models circulated: the royal warrior-messiah who would defeat Rome and establish Israel's political sovereignty; the priestly messiah who would purify temple worship; the prophetic messiah like Moses or Elijah who would lead a new exodus. Matthew deliberately engages these expectations, presenting Jesus as the Davidic Messiah ([Matthew 1:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1), [6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#6), [20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#20); [9:27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#27); [12:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#23); [15:22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#22); [20:30-31](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/20#30); [21:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#9), [15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#15); [22:42-45](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#42)) while dramatically redefining messianic expectations through emphasis on suffering, service, and spiritual redemption rather than political revolution.

**The Composition Date Context**: If Matthew was composed in the 60s CE (traditional dating), the Gospel reflects the tense final decade before the First Jewish-Roman War (66-73 CE), a period of escalating Jewish nationalism, Roman oppression, and internal Jewish conflicts. If composed in the 80s-90s CE (majority scholarly dating), the Gospel reflects the traumatic aftermath of Jerusalem's destruction in 70 CE, when the temple was burned, the sacrificial system ended, and Pharisaic Judaism reorganized at Yavneh (Jamnia) under Johanan ben Zakkai. This later dating context helps explain Matthew's polemical edge against the Pharisees—possibly reflecting conflict between the Matthean community and the emerging rabbinic Judaism that claimed exclusive legitimacy as Israel's authentic continuation.

**Geographic Setting**: Most scholars propose that Matthew was composed in or near Antioch in Syria (modern Antakya, Turkey), the third-largest city in the Roman Empire. Antioch hosted a substantial Jewish population and became a major center of early Christianity ([Acts 11:19-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/11#19); [13:1-3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/13#1); [15:22-35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/15#22); [Galatians 2:11-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/galatians/2#11)). The bilingual urban environment (Greek-speaking but with strong Jewish presence) fits Matthew's characteristics: Greek literary composition with thorough knowledge of Hebrew Scriptures, sensitivity to Jewish concerns while writing for a broader audience. Ignatius of Antioch (c. 35-107 CE) demonstrates early knowledge of Matthew's Gospel, supporting Syrian provenance.

**The Matthean Community's Social Location**: The Gospel reflects a community experiencing identity crisis and boundary definition. Internal evidence suggests a predominantly Jewish-Christian congregation that included both former Pharisees (possibly indicated by [Matthew 13:52](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#52): "every scribe who has been trained for the kingdom of heaven") and less observant Jews, along with God-fearing Gentiles. The community faced external pressure from the broader Jewish community, evident in references to "their synagogues" ([Matthew 4:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#23); [9:35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#35); [10:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#17); [12:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#9); [13:54](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#54))—linguistic distancing suggesting separation or expulsion. Jesus' warning that disciples will be "flogged in their synagogues" ([Matthew 10:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#17)) and "delivered up to councils" ([Matthew 10:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#17); [24:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#9)) likely reflects the community's lived experience of persecution.

**Literary and Liturgical Context**: The Gospel's careful literary structure—five major discourse sections ([5-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#1), [10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#1), [13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#1), [18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#1), [24-25](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#1)) each ending with the formulaic phrase "when Jesus had finished these sayings" (7:28; 11:1; 13:53; 19:1; 26:1)—suggests composition for liturgical reading and catechetical instruction. The Gospel served not merely as historical record but as authoritative teaching document for Christian community formation. The emphasis on church discipline ([Matthew 18:15-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#15)), the Lord's Prayer ([Matthew 6:9-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#9)), and baptismal formula ([Matthew 28:19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#19)) indicate the Gospel's liturgical function.

This historical environment—characterized by Roman political oppression, diverse Jewish sectarian movements, intense messianic expectation, temple-centered religious life (or its traumatic absence after 70 CE), urban bilingual culture, and emerging Jewish-Christian identity negotiations—shaped Matthew's distinctive portrayal of Jesus as the Jewish Messiah whose kingdom transcends and fulfills all previous covenantal expectations while opening salvation to all nations.
