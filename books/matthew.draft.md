# Matthew

## Authorship

The question of who wrote the Gospel of Matthew has generated substantial scholarly debate, with traditional Christian attribution to the apostle Matthew contrasting with modern critical scholarship's majority view of anonymous authorship. This debate involves both external testimony from early church fathers and internal evidence from the Gospel itself.

**Early Church Testimony**: The earliest explicit attribution of this Gospel to Matthew comes from Papias of Hierapolis (c. 60-130 CE), bishop and Apostolic Father, whose testimony is preserved by Eusebius of Caesarea (circa 320 CE). Eusebius quotes Papias as stating that "Matthew compiled the Oracles [logia] in the Hebrew language, and everyone translated them as well as he could." This cryptic statement has generated considerable interpretive debate: Does "Oracles" refer to the complete Gospel, a collection of Jesus' sayings, or an earlier Aramaic/Hebrew source? Does Papias refer to our canonical Greek Matthew, or to a different composition? Conservative scholars often argue that Papias refers to an original Hebrew/Aramaic Gospel that Matthew composed, which was subsequently translated into the Greek text we possess. Critical scholars question whether Papias actually knew the authorship firsthand or was passing along later tradition.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130-202 CE), writing around 180 CE in *Against Heresies* 3.1.1, provides more explicit testimony: "Matthew also issued a written Gospel among the Hebrews in their own dialect while Peter and Paul were preaching at Rome and laying the foundations of the church." Irenaeus, who had personal connection to Polycarp (who knew the Apostle John), represents an important link in the chain of tradition. His testimony places Matthew's composition during the ministries of Peter and Paul in Rome (c. 60-67 CE), suggesting an earlier date than most modern scholars accept. All surviving Greek manuscripts with intact superscriptions bear the title "According to Matthew" (ΚΑΤΑ ΜΑΘΘΑΙΟΝ), demonstrating universal early attribution to Matthew in the manuscript tradition.

**Traditional Authorship Arguments**: Conservative scholarship defends Matthean authorship based on several considerations: (1) The unanimous early church tradition attributing the Gospel to Matthew, with no competing tradition suggesting alternative authorship; (2) The Gospel's detailed knowledge of Jewish customs, geography, and Scripture, consistent with a Jewish author; (3) The emphasis on financial matters and specific monetary references (such as [Matthew 17:24-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#24) regarding the temple tax), potentially reflecting Matthew's background as a tax collector; (4) The inclusion of the calling narrative in [Matthew 9:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#9) where Jesus calls "Matthew" (compared to "Levi" in Mark and Luke), possibly indicating authorial modesty or self-reference; (5) The lack of compelling reason why the early church would attribute authorship to a relatively minor apostle rather than a more prominent figure if the attribution were fabricated.

**Critical Scholarship's Anonymous Author Theory**: The majority of contemporary New Testament scholars conclude that the Gospel was composed by an anonymous Jewish-Christian author, probably not an eyewitness to Jesus' ministry. Primary arguments include: (1) The Gospel shows clear literary dependence on Mark's Gospel (using approximately 90% of Mark's content with stylistic improvements), which seems unlikely if the author were himself an eyewitness who could rely on personal memory; (2) The sophisticated Greek literary structure, careful arrangement of material into five major discourse sections (paralleling the Pentateuch), and polished style suggest an educated writer working decades after the events rather than a Galilean tax collector writing in the 60s; (3) The Gospel reflects concerns of a later Jewish-Christian community dealing with separation from the synagogue and defining its identity, suggesting composition after 70 CE; (4) The use of the title "Matthew" may simply reflect scribal tradition or association with Matthean source material (perhaps the "logia" mentioned by Papias) rather than complete Gospel authorship.

**Dating Considerations**: Traditional scholarship, following Papias and Irenaeus, dates Matthew to the 60s CE, before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. Advocates point to [Matthew 24:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#2) as genuine prophecy rather than *vaticinium ex eventu* (prophecy after the fact), and note the Gospel's emphasis on Jewish temple practices that would have diminished relevance after 70 CE. The majority of critical scholars date Matthew between 80-95 CE, arguing that: (1) The Gospel reflects the final separation between church and synagogue occurring around 85-90 CE; (2) Matthew's use of Mark (typically dated around 70 CE) as a source requires a later date; (3) The description of Jerusalem's destruction in [Matthew 22:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#7) appears to reflect knowledge of the actual historical event in 70 CE. A minority of scholars (including N.T. Wright, John Wenham, and John A.T. Robinson) challenge late dating, arguing that evidence for Markan priority is not conclusive, that Matthew could date to the 40s-50s, and that the supposed "later" concerns actually reflect first-generation Jewish-Christian tensions.

**Compositional Process and Sources**: Beyond the question of final authorship, scholars debate Matthew's compositional process. The prevailing "Two-Source Hypothesis" proposes that Matthew used two primary sources: Mark's Gospel and a hypothetical sayings source designated "Q" (from German *Quelle*, "source"), shared with Luke but not found in Mark. Additionally, Matthew contains material unique to this Gospel (designated "M"), including the infancy narrative ([Matthew 1-2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1)), several parables (the weeds, the hidden treasure, the pearl of great value, the net, the unforgiving servant, the workers in the vineyard, the two sons, the wedding feast, the ten virgins, the talents, the sheep and the goats), and portions of the Sermon on the Mount. Whether "M" represents written sources, oral tradition, or the author's own composition remains debated. Some scholars (particularly advocates of Matthean priority like the Griesbach hypothesis) argue that Mark used Matthew, not vice versa, thus eliminating the primary argument against apostolic authorship.

**Conclusion on Authorship**: The authorship question ultimately divides along methodological lines. Those who grant substantial weight to early church testimony and allow for the possibility of eyewitness authorship despite use of written sources tend to affirm traditional Matthean authorship. Those who prioritize internal literary and historical evidence, assuming redactional activity and later dating, lean toward anonymous authorship by a Jewish-Christian scribe writing for a specific community context. Both positions acknowledge that the Gospel presents itself as authoritative apostolic testimony about Jesus Christ, and both traditions affirm its inspiration and canonical status regardless of the specific identity of the human author. The early church's universal attribution to Matthew, preserved in all manuscript traditions, testifies to the church's conviction that this Gospel authentically represents Matthew's testimony—whether through direct composition, superintendence of a scribe, or preservation of distinctly Matthean source material that formed the Gospel's foundation.

## Historical Environment

The Gospel of Matthew emerged during a critical period of transition for both Judaism and the nascent Christian movement. Understanding the historical, social, and religious environment that shaped this Gospel is essential for grasping its distinctive emphases and theological concerns.

**The Judean-Roman Context**: Matthew's Gospel is set during the final years of the Second Temple period, when Palestine existed under Roman occupation. The narrative opens during the reign of Herod the Great (37-4 BCE), the Idumean client-king appointed by Rome ([Matthew 2:1-22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#1)). Herod's paranoid brutality—exemplified in the massacre of Bethlehem's infants ([Matthew 2:16-18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#16))—illustrates the oppressive political environment into which Jesus was born. Following Herod's death, Palestine was divided among his sons: Archelaus ruled Judea, Samaria, and Idumea (4 BCE - 6 CE, [Matthew 2:22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#22)); Herod Antipas governed Galilee and Perea (4 BCE - 39 CE, appearing in [Matthew 14:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#1) regarding John the Baptist's execution); and Philip ruled territories northeast of Galilee. After Archelaus's removal, Judea came under direct Roman rule through prefects, most notoriously Pontius Pilate (26-36 CE), who appears prominently in the passion narrative ([Matthew 27:1-26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#1)).

**Jewish Religious and Social Structure**: First-century Judaism was characterized by diverse sects and movements, several of which appear in Matthew's narrative. The Pharisees, a lay movement emphasizing oral Torah and purity observance, receive extensive (often critical) attention throughout Matthew ([Matthew 3:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#7); [5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20); [9:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#11), [34](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#34); [12:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#2), [14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#14), [24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#24), [38](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#38); [15:1-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#1); [16:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#1); [19:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#3); chapters [22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#1)). The Sadducees, representing the aristocratic priestly establishment centered in the Jerusalem temple, appear in debates about resurrection ([Matthew 3:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#7); [16:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#1); [22:23-33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#23)). The scribes (grammateis), experts in Jewish law, frequently appear alongside Pharisees as Jesus' opponents ([Matthew 5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20); [7:29](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#29); [9:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#3); [12:38](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#38); [15:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#1); [23:2-36](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#2)).

**Messianic Expectation and Political Hope**: First-century Judaism nurtured intense messianic expectations, anticipating God's intervention to deliver Israel from oppression and restore the Davidic kingdom. Various messianic models circulated: the royal warrior-messiah who would defeat Rome and establish Israel's political sovereignty; the priestly messiah who would purify temple worship; the prophetic messiah like Moses or Elijah who would lead a new exodus. Matthew deliberately engages these expectations, presenting Jesus as the Davidic Messiah ([Matthew 1:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1), [6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#6), [20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#20); [9:27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#27); [12:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#23); [15:22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#22); [20:30-31](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/20#30); [21:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#9), [15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#15); [22:42-45](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#42)) while dramatically redefining messianic expectations through emphasis on suffering, service, and spiritual redemption rather than political revolution.

**The Composition Date Context**: If Matthew was composed in the 60s CE (traditional dating), the Gospel reflects the tense final decade before the First Jewish-Roman War (66-73 CE), a period of escalating Jewish nationalism, Roman oppression, and internal Jewish conflicts. If composed in the 80s-90s CE (majority scholarly dating), the Gospel reflects the traumatic aftermath of Jerusalem's destruction in 70 CE, when the temple was burned, the sacrificial system ended, and Pharisaic Judaism reorganized at Yavneh (Jamnia) under Johanan ben Zakkai. This later dating context helps explain Matthew's polemical edge against the Pharisees—possibly reflecting conflict between the Matthean community and the emerging rabbinic Judaism that claimed exclusive legitimacy as Israel's authentic continuation.

**Geographic Setting**: Most scholars propose that Matthew was composed in or near Antioch in Syria (modern Antakya, Turkey), the third-largest city in the Roman Empire. Antioch hosted a substantial Jewish population and became a major center of early Christianity ([Acts 11:19-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/11#19); [13:1-3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/13#1); [15:22-35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/15#22); [Galatians 2:11-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/galatians/2#11)). The bilingual urban environment (Greek-speaking but with strong Jewish presence) fits Matthew's characteristics: Greek literary composition with thorough knowledge of Hebrew Scriptures, sensitivity to Jewish concerns while writing for a broader audience. Ignatius of Antioch (c. 35-107 CE) demonstrates early knowledge of Matthew's Gospel, supporting Syrian provenance.

**The Matthean Community's Social Location**: The Gospel reflects a community experiencing identity crisis and boundary definition. Internal evidence suggests a predominantly Jewish-Christian congregation that included both former Pharisees (possibly indicated by [Matthew 13:52](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#52): "every scribe who has been trained for the kingdom of heaven") and less observant Jews, along with God-fearing Gentiles. The community faced external pressure from the broader Jewish community, evident in references to "their synagogues" ([Matthew 4:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#23); [9:35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#35); [10:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#17); [12:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#9); [13:54](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#54))—linguistic distancing suggesting separation or expulsion. Jesus' warning that disciples will be "flogged in their synagogues" ([Matthew 10:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#17)) and "delivered up to councils" ([Matthew 10:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#17); [24:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#9)) likely reflects the community's lived experience of persecution.

**Literary and Liturgical Context**: The Gospel's careful literary structure—five major discourse sections ([5-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#1), [10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#1), [13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#1), [18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#1), [24-25](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#1)) each ending with the formulaic phrase "when Jesus had finished these sayings" (7:28; 11:1; 13:53; 19:1; 26:1)—suggests composition for liturgical reading and catechetical instruction. The Gospel served not merely as historical record but as authoritative teaching document for Christian community formation. The emphasis on church discipline ([Matthew 18:15-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#15)), the Lord's Prayer ([Matthew 6:9-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#9)), and baptismal formula ([Matthew 28:19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#19)) indicate the Gospel's liturgical function.

This historical environment—characterized by Roman political oppression, diverse Jewish sectarian movements, intense messianic expectation, temple-centered religious life (or its traumatic absence after 70 CE), urban bilingual culture, and emerging Jewish-Christian identity negotiations—shaped Matthew's distinctive portrayal of Jesus as the Jewish Messiah whose kingdom transcends and fulfills all previous covenantal expectations while opening salvation to all nations.

## Purpose

The Gospel of Matthew serves multiple interconnected purposes, all oriented toward demonstrating that Jesus of Nazareth is the promised Messiah who fulfills Old Testament prophecy and establishes God's kingdom. Understanding these purposes illuminates the Gospel's distinctive emphases and theological contributions.

**Proving Jesus is the Jewish Messiah**: Matthew's primary purpose is establishing Jesus' messianic identity for a Jewish audience. The Gospel opens with a genealogy tracing Jesus' lineage through David to Abraham ([Matthew 1:1-17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1)), immediately positioning Him within Israel's covenantal history. The title "son of David" appears throughout ([Matthew 1:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1), [20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#20); [9:27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#27); [12:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#23); [15:22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#22); [20:30-31](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/20#30); [21:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#9), [15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#15); [22:42](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#42)), emphasizing Jesus' royal messianic credentials. Matthew employs extensive Old Testament quotations—more than any other Gospel, with approximately 60 citations—to demonstrate prophetic fulfillment.

**Fulfillment Formula Quotations**: Matthew uniquely employs "fulfillment citations" (appearing 10-14 times depending on scholarly count) with formulaic introductions like "this took place to fulfill what was spoken by the prophet." Key examples include: [Matthew 1:22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#22) (virgin birth fulfilling [Isaiah 7:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/7#14)); [Matthew 2:5-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#5) (Bethlehem birth fulfilling [Micah 5:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/micah/5#2)); [Matthew 2:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#15) (return from Egypt fulfilling [Hosea 11:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/hosea/11#1)); [Matthew 2:17-18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#17) (Herod's infanticide fulfilling [Jeremiah 31:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/jeremiah/31#15)); [Matthew 4:14-16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#14) (Galilean ministry fulfilling [Isaiah 9:1-2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/9#1)); [Matthew 8:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#17) (healing ministry fulfilling [Isaiah 53:4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/53#4)); [Matthew 12:17-21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#17) (servant ministry fulfilling [Isaiah 42:1-4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/42#1)); [Matthew 13:35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#35) (parabolic teaching fulfilling [Psalm 78:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/psalms/78#2)); [Matthew 21:4-5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#4) (triumphal entry fulfilling [Zechariah 9:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/zechariah/9#9)); [Matthew 27:9-10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#9) (thirty pieces of silver fulfilling [Zechariah 11:12-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/zechariah/11#12)). These citations demonstrate Matthew's theological conviction that Jesus' life, ministry, death, and resurrection represent the climactic fulfillment of God's redemptive purposes revealed in Scripture.

**Proclaiming the Kingdom of Heaven**: Matthew emphasizes "the kingdom of heaven" (used 32 times, unique to this Gospel) as Jesus' central message. From Jesus' inaugural proclamation—"Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" ([Matthew 4:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#17))—through the parables of the kingdom ([Matthew 13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#1)) to the eschatological discourse ([Matthew 24-25](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#1)), the Gospel presents Jesus as the King who inaugurates God's reign. The kingdom teaching redefines Jewish messianic expectations: the kingdom arrives not through military conquest but through Jesus' authoritative teaching, miraculous signs, sacrificial death, and resurrection. Entry into the kingdom requires repentance, faith, and righteousness exceeding that of the scribes and Pharisees ([Matthew 5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20)).

**Providing Catechetical Instruction**: Matthew's careful literary structure serves catechetical purposes. The five major discourses—Sermon on the Mount ([5-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#1)), Mission Discourse ([10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#1)), Parables Discourse ([13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#1)), Community Discourse ([18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#1)), and Eschatological Discourse ([24-25](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#1))—provide systematic teaching for Christian community formation. These discourses address ethics (5-7), mission (10), kingdom mysteries (13), church discipline (18), and eschatology (24-25), equipping believers for faithful discipleship.

**Establishing Church Identity and Practice**: Matthew is the only Gospel to use the term "church" (ekklēsia, [Matthew 16:18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#18); [18:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#17)), indicating concern for community formation. Jesus' promise to Peter—"on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it" ([Matthew 16:18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#18))—establishes the church's foundation and invincibility. The community discourse ([Matthew 18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#1)) provides procedures for conflict resolution, church discipline, and restoration, emphasizing humility, forgiveness, and communal authority. The Great Commission ([Matthew 28:18-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#18)) defines the church's mission: making disciples of all nations through baptism and teaching.

**Defending Against Jewish Objections**: Matthew addresses Jewish rejection of Jesus' messianic claims through careful argumentation. The Gospel counters objections that the Messiah would not come from Galilee ([Matthew 4:12-16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#12) fulfills [Isaiah 9:1-2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/9#1)), that He would not associate with sinners (Jesus explicitly came to call sinners, [Matthew 9:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#13)), that He would not violate Sabbath (Jesus is Lord of the Sabbath, [Matthew 12:8](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#8)), and that His disciples would not neglect ritual purity (true defilement comes from the heart, [Matthew 15:10-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#10)). The Gospel also addresses the charge that disciples stole Jesus' body ([Matthew 28:11-15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#11)), showing this as a deliberately fabricated lie.

**Addressing the Gentile Mission**: While emphasizing Jesus' Jewish identity, Matthew also prepares for the Gentile mission. The magi from the east worship the newborn king ([Matthew 2:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#1)); the centurion demonstrates greater faith than any in Israel ([Matthew 8:5-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#5)); the Canaanite woman's persistent faith receives commendation ([Matthew 15:21-28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#21)); and Jesus predicts that "many will come from east and west and recline at table with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob in the kingdom of heaven, while the sons of the kingdom will be thrown into the outer darkness" ([Matthew 8:11-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#11)). The Great Commission explicitly sends disciples to "all nations" ([Matthew 28:19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#19)), universalizing salvation while maintaining continuity with Israel's covenantal history.

**Correcting False Righteousness**: A persistent theme is contrasting authentic righteousness with the hypocritical righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees. Jesus' teaching, "unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven" ([Matthew 5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20)), introduces the Sermon on the Mount's radical ethical demands. The extended woe discourse against the Pharisees ([Matthew 23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#1)) exposes their hypocrisy: they teach but do not practice ([23:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#3)), they burden others while avoiding burdens themselves ([23:4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#4)), they perform religious acts for human praise ([23:5-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#5)), they prevent others from entering the kingdom ([23:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#13)), and they focus on externals while neglecting justice, mercy, and faithfulness ([23:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#23)).

In summary, Matthew's purpose is multifaceted: demonstrating Jesus as the Jewish Messiah through fulfillment of Scripture, proclaiming the kingdom of heaven, providing systematic teaching for Christian community formation, establishing church identity and practice, defending against Jewish objections, preparing for the Gentile mission, and contrasting authentic righteousness with religious hypocrisy. All purposes converge in presenting Jesus as the authoritative interpreter of Torah, the inaugurator of God's kingdom, and the foundation of the church sent to disciple all nations.

## Cross-References

The Gospel of Matthew contains extensive cross-references both to the Old Testament and to other New Testament writings. Matthew quotes or alludes to the Hebrew Scriptures more than any other Gospel, with approximately 60 direct citations and numerous additional allusions, establishing Jesus' ministry as the fulfillment of God's redemptive plan revealed through the prophets.

**Old Testament Fulfillment Quotations**: Matthew employs distinctive "fulfillment formula" citations (10-14 occurrences) that explicitly identify prophetic fulfillment. [Matthew 1:22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#22) cites [Isaiah 7:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/7#14) regarding the virgin birth: "Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel." [Matthew 2:5-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#5) quotes [Micah 5:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/micah/5#2) identifying Bethlehem as the Messiah's birthplace. [Matthew 2:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#15) applies [Hosea 11:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/hosea/11#1)—"Out of Egypt I called my son"—to Jesus' return from Egypt, interpreting Israel's exodus typologically. [Matthew 2:17-18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#17) sees Herod's massacre of infants fulfilling [Jeremiah 31:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/jeremiah/31#15) about Rachel weeping for her children.

[Matthew 4:14-16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#14) identifies Jesus' Galilean ministry as fulfilling [Isaiah 9:1-2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/9#1): "The people dwelling in darkness have seen a great light." Approximately 90% of Mark's content appears in Matthew, typically with stylistic improvements and additional material. The triple tradition (material in all three Synoptics) includes most major pericopes: Jesus' baptism ([Mark 1:9-11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/mark/1#9); [Luke 3:21-22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/3#21); [Matthew 3:13-17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#13)), the temptation ([Mark 1:12-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/mark/1#12); [Luke 4:1-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/4#1); [Matthew 4:1-11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#1)), Peter's confession ([Mark 8:27-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/mark/8#27); [Luke 9:18-21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/9#18); [Matthew 16:13-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#13)), the Transfiguration ([Mark 9:2-8](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/mark/9#2); [Luke 9:28-36](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/9#28); [Matthew 17:1-8](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#1)), and the passion narrative ([Mark 14-15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/mark/14#1); [Luke 22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/22#1); [Matthew 26-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#1)).

Matthew and Luke share approximately 235 verses not found in Mark, traditionally attributed to the hypothetical "Q" source. This material includes the Beatitudes ([Matthew 5:3-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#3); [Luke 6:20-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/6#20)), the Lord's Prayer ([Matthew 6:9-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#9); [Luke 11:2-4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/11#2)), the centurion's faith ([Matthew 8:5-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#5); [Luke 7:1-10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/7#1)), John's question about Jesus ([Matthew 11:2-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/11#2); [Luke 7:18-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/7#18)), and various sayings about discipleship, judgment, and the kingdom.

**Johannine Connections**: While John's Gospel follows different structure and theology, several connections exist. Both Matthew and John record Jesus' walking on water ([Matthew 14:22-33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#22); [John 6:16-21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/john/6#16)), though with different emphases. **Acts and Pauline Corpus**: [Acts 1:18-19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/1#18) provides an alternative account of Judas' death, complementing [Matthew 27:3-10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#3). Peter's Pentecost sermon in [Acts 2:34-35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/2#34) quotes the same [Psalm 110:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/psalms/110#1) that Jesus cites in [Matthew 22:44](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#44). Paul's teaching on marriage and divorce in [1 Corinthians 7:10-11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/1-corinthians/7#10) references Jesus' teaching found in [Matthew 5:32](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#32) and [19:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#9). Paul's discussion of the Lord's Supper in [1 Corinthians 11:23-26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/1-corinthians/11#23) parallels Matthew's institution narrative in [Matthew 26:26-29](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#26). The Great Commission's baptismal formula in [Matthew 28:19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#19) finds echoes throughout Acts and the epistles.

**James and General Epistles**: The Epistle of James shows remarkable affinity with Matthew's Sermon on the Mount. [James 1:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/james/1#2) echoes [Matthew 5:11-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#11) on rejoicing in trials. [James 1:4-5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/james/1#4) parallels [Matthew 5:48](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#48) on perfection. [James 1:22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/james/1#22) reflects [Matthew 7:21-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#21) on doing, not just hearing. [James 4:10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/james/4#10) parallels [Matthew 23:12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#12) on humility and exaltation. [James 5:12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/james/5#12) closely follows [Matthew 5:34-37](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#34) on not swearing oaths.

[1 Peter 2:12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/1-peter/2#12) echoes [Matthew 5:16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#16) about good works glorifying God. [1 Peter 3:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/1-peter/3#9) reflects [Matthew 5:38-48](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#38) on not repaying evil for evil. [1 Peter 3:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/1-peter/3#14) alludes to [Matthew 5:10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#10) regarding blessing in persecution. [1 Peter 4:12-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/1-peter/4#12) parallels [Matthew 5:11-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#11) on rejoicing in suffering for righteousness.

**Revelation**: The apocalyptic discourse in [Matthew 24-25](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#1) shares themes and imagery with Revelation, particularly regarding tribulation, cosmic signs, the Son of Man's coming in glory, and final judgment. [Matthew 24:30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#30) describing the Son of Man coming on clouds connects with [Revelation 1:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/revelation/1#7). The judgment scene in [Matthew 25:31-46](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#31) anticipates the great white throne judgment in [Revelation 20:11-15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/revelation/20#11).

This extensive network of cross-references demonstrates Matthew's role as the bridge between Old and New Testaments, anchoring Jesus' ministry firmly in Israel's scriptural heritage while establishing the foundation for Christian theology developed throughout the New Testament corpus.

## Canonical Status

The Gospel of Matthew has enjoyed uncontested canonical status since the earliest period of church history. Among the four canonical Gospels, Matthew appears to have achieved widespread recognition and liturgical use earlier than any other, making it the most frequently cited Gospel in early Christian literature.

**Early Patristic Attestation**: The earliest explicit testimony to Matthew's Gospel comes from Papias of Hierapolis (c. 60-130 CE), whose statement preserved by Eusebius identifies Matthew as the author: "Matthew compiled the Oracles in the Hebrew language, and everyone translated them as well as he could." Whether Papias refers to canonical Greek Matthew or an earlier Aramaic source remains debated, but his testimony establishes early recognition of Matthean material as authoritative.

Ignatius of Antioch (c. 35-107 CE) demonstrates knowledge of Matthew's Gospel in his letters written around 107-110 CE. His Epistle to the Smyrnaeans 1:1 alludes to [Matthew 3:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#15), and Epistle to Polycarp 2:2 echoes [Matthew 10:16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#16) ("Be wise as serpents and innocent as doves"). The Didache (c. 70-110 CE), an early Christian manual, quotes extensively from Matthew, including the Lord's Prayer ([Matthew 6:9-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#9)) in Didache 8:2 and baptismal instructions referencing [Matthew 28:19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#19) in Didache 7:1.

Justin Martyr (c. 100-165 CE) refers to the Gospels as "memoirs of the apostles" in his First Apology (c. 155-157 CE) and Dialogue with Trypho, citing material from Matthew more than any other Gospel. Though Justin rarely names the Gospels explicitly, his quotations demonstrate Matthew's authoritative status in mid-second-century Rome.

Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 130-202 CE), writing around 180 CE in Against Heresies 3.1.1, provides crucial testimony: "Matthew also issued a written Gospel among the Hebrews in their own dialect while Peter and Paul were preaching at Rome and laying the foundations of the church." Irenaeus explicitly affirms the fourfold Gospel canon, arguing that just as there are four winds and four corners of the earth, so there must be four Gospels (Against Heresies 3.11.8). His testimony establishes that by 180 CE, Matthew held undisputed place among the four canonical Gospels.

**Manuscript Evidence**: Matthew appears in all major early manuscripts of the New Testament. Papyrus fragments include P1 (3rd century, containing portions of Matthew 1), P45 (early 3rd century, containing portions of all four Gospels including Matthew), P53 (3rd century, Matthew 26), P64+67 (c. 200 CE, Matthew 3, 5, 26), P70 (3rd century, Matthew 2-3, 11-12, 24), P77 (late 2nd/early 3rd century, Matthew 23), and P104 (2nd century, Matthew 21). The second-century dating of some fragments (particularly P64+67 and P104) demonstrates Matthew's circulation within 100 years of composition.

The great uncial codices all include Matthew in the first position among the Gospels: Codex Sinaiticus (4th century, א/01), Codex Vaticanus (4th century, B/03), Codex Alexandrinus (5th century, A/02), Codex Bezae (5th century, D/05), and Codex Washingtonianus (5th century, W/032). Matthew's consistent placement first in these manuscripts reflects early tradition regarding Gospel order, likely based on its perceived apostolic authorship and comprehensive presentation of Jesus' teaching.

**Early Canonical Lists**: Matthew appears in every known early Christian canonical list. The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170-200 CE), though damaged at its beginning, includes reference to the Gospels and explicitly discusses the fourfold Gospel canon. Origen of Alexandria (c. 185-254 CE) lists Matthew first among the four Gospels in his Commentary on Matthew and Homilies, treating it as undisputed Scripture. Eusebius of Caesarea (c. 260-339 CE) in his Ecclesiastical History 3.25.1-7 places Matthew among the "recognized books" (homologoumena) that were universally accepted, contrasting these with disputed books (antilegomena).

Athanasius' 39th Festal Letter (367 CE) provides the earliest complete list of the 27-book New Testament canon, with Matthew listed first among the Gospels. The councils of Hippo (393 CE) and Carthage (397 CE, 419 CE) officially recognized the 27-book New Testament canon including Matthew. Jerome's Vulgate (late 4th/early 5th century) and Augustine's writings (late 4th/early 5th century) both affirm Matthew's canonical status without qualification.

**Liturgical Usage**: Matthew's Gospel achieved early prominence in Christian liturgy. Its comprehensive presentation of Jesus' teaching, particularly the Sermon on the Mount ([Matthew 5-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#1)), made it ideally suited for catechetical instruction. The Lord's Prayer from [Matthew 6:9-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#9) became standard in Christian worship. The Great Commission ([Matthew 28:18-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#18)) provided the baptismal formula. Early church orders and liturgical texts quote Matthew more frequently than other Gospels, indicating its central role in worship and instruction.

The church fathers' commentaries demonstrate Matthew's canonical authority. Origen wrote the first extensive Gospel commentary on Matthew (c. 246-248 CE). John Chrysostom's Homilies on Matthew (c. 390 CE) provided 90 sermons expounding the entire Gospel. Jerome, Augustine, and virtually every major patristic writer produced Matthew commentaries or extensively cited it, treating the Gospel as divinely inspired Scripture.

**No Canonical Disputes**: Unlike certain New Testament books that faced periods of questioning (such as Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, 2-3 John, Jude, and Revelation), Matthew never experienced canonical dispute. No orthodox Christian group ever questioned its authority. Even heretical groups like the Ebionites, who rejected much of the New Testament, accepted Matthew (though in modified form). Marcion (c. 85-160 CE), who rejected the Old Testament and most of the New Testament, included a Gospel in his canon (a redacted Luke), implicitly acknowledging Gospel authority, and his opponents used Matthew against him.

**Relationship to the Four-Gospel Canon**: By the mid-to-late second century, the fourfold Gospel canon (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John) had achieved universal recognition in orthodox Christianity. Irenaeus' defense of exactly four Gospels (Against Heresies 3.11.8) demonstrates this was not arbitrary but based on apostolic tradition and the Gospels' complementary witness to Christ. Tatian's Diatessaron (c. 160-175 CE), a harmony of the four Gospels, presupposes their recognized authority. The

 fourfold canon resisted both reductionist tendencies (like Marcion's single Gospel) and expansionist tendencies (rejecting apocryphal gospels like the Gospel of Thomas, Gospel of Peter, or Infancy Gospel of Thomas).

**Eastern and Western Agreement**: Both Eastern and Western churches universally accepted Matthew without variation. Greek-speaking Eastern churches, Latin-speaking Western churches, Syriac-speaking churches (as evidenced by the Old Syriac and Peshitta versions), Coptic churches, Ethiopian churches, Armenian churches, and Georgian churches all included Matthew in their canonical collections. This geographic and linguistic unanimity demonstrates that Matthew's canonical status did not depend on regional tradition but on universal apostolic recognition.

**Reformers' Affirmation**: The Protestant Reformers unanimously affirmed Matthew's canonical authority. Martin Luther's German translation (1522) included Matthew without question. John Calvin's Commentary on a Harmony of the Evangelists (1555) treated Matthew as inspired Scripture. The Westminster Confession of Faith (1646) listed Matthew among the canonical books "given by inspiration of God, to be the rule of faith and life." All major Protestant confessions affirm Matthew's place in the New Testament canon.

**Modern Ecumenical Consensus**: Despite scholarly debates about authorship and dating, all major Christian traditions—Roman Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Oriental Orthodox, and Protestant—unanimously recognize Matthew's canonical status. The Gospel appears in every Christian Bible worldwide, translated into thousands of languages. Ecumenical organizations and interdenominational translations consistently include Matthew as foundational Scripture.

In summary, Matthew's canonical status rests on: (1) earliest patristic attestation from the late first/early second century; (2) universal manuscript evidence from the second century onward; (3) inclusion in every early canonical list; (4) extensive liturgical usage demonstrating practical recognition; (5) complete absence of canonical disputes; (6) central role in the universally accepted fourfold Gospel canon; (7) unanimous Eastern and Western church recognition; (8) Reformers' affirmation; and (9) modern ecumenical consensus. Matthew stands among the most firmly established books in the Christian canon.

## Summary

The Gospel of Matthew presents Jesus of Nazareth as the Jewish Messiah who fulfills Old Testament prophecy, establishes God's kingdom, and sends His disciples to make disciples of all nations. The Gospel's 28 chapters systematically demonstrate Jesus' identity through genealogy, teaching, miracles, death, resurrection, and commission.

**Chapters 1-2: The Messiah's Birth and Infancy**: Matthew opens with Jesus' genealogy tracing His lineage through David to Abraham ([Matthew 1:1-17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1)), establishing His legal right to David's throne. The genealogy emphasizes Jesus as "the Christ, the son of David, the son of Abraham" ([Matthew 1:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#1)), immediately positioning Him within Israel's covenantal history. The virgin birth fulfills [Isaiah 7:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/7#14): "Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel" ([Matthew 1:22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#22)). An angel commands Joseph: "you shall call his name Jesus, for he will save his people from their sins" ([Matthew 1:21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/1#21)).

Magi from the east worship the newborn king ([Matthew 2:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#1)), while Herod's paranoid massacre of Bethlehem's infants ([Matthew 2:16-18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#16)) fulfills [Jeremiah 31:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/jeremiah/31#15). The family's flight to and return from Egypt fulfills [Hosea 11:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/hosea/11#1): "Out of Egypt I called my son" ([Matthew 2:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/2#15)).

**Chapters 3-4: Preparation and Temptation**: John the Baptist prepares the way, proclaiming, "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" ([Matthew 3:2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#2)), fulfilling [Isaiah 40:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/40#3). At Jesus' baptism, the heavens open and God declares, "This is my beloved Son, with whom I am well pleased" ([Matthew 3:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/3#17)). The Spirit leads Jesus into the wilderness where Satan tempts Him ([Matthew 4:1-11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#1)). Jesus defeats each temptation with Scripture, commanding Satan, "Be gone, Satan! For it is written, 'You shall worship the Lord your God and him only shall you serve'" ([Matthew 4:10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#10), quoting [Deuteronomy 6:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/deuteronomy/6#13)).

Jesus begins His Galilean ministry, proclaiming, "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" ([Matthew 4:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#17)). He calls His first disciples with the command, "Follow me, and I will make you fishers of men" ([Matthew 4:19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/4#19)).

**Chapters 5-7: The Sermon on the Mount**: Jesus delivers His first major discourse, presenting the kingdom's ethics and righteousness. The Beatitudes pronounce blessings: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" ([Matthew 5:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#3)); "Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted" ([5:4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#4)); through "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven" ([5:10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#10)). Jesus declares His disciples to be "the salt of the earth" and "the light of the world" ([Matthew 5:13-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#13)), commanding them to "let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven" ([Matthew 5:16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#16)).

Jesus teaches that He came not to abolish but to fulfill the Law and the Prophets ([Matthew 5:17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#17)), establishing that "unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven" ([Matthew 5:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#20)). Six antitheses demonstrate kingdom ethics: on murder ([5:21-26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#21)), adultery ([5:27-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#27)), divorce ([5:31-32](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#31)), oaths ([5:33-37](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#33)), retaliation ([5:38-42](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#38)), and love for enemies ([5:43-48](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#43)). Jesus commands, "Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you" ([Matthew 5:44](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#44)), concluding, "You therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect" ([Matthew 5:48](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/5#48)).

Chapter 6 addresses authentic piety: giving alms, praying, and fasting in secret rather than for human praise ([Matthew 6:1-18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#1)). Jesus teaches the Lord's Prayer: "Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name. Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven..." ([Matthew 6:9-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#9)). He commands, "Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth...but lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven" ([Matthew 6:19-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#19)), teaching that "you cannot serve God and money" ([Matthew 6:24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#24)). Jesus addresses anxiety: "do not be anxious about your life" ([Matthew 6:25](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#25)), commanding instead, "seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness" ([Matthew 6:33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/6#33)).

Chapter 7 warns against judging others ([7:1-5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#1)), teaches prayer persistence ([7:7-11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#7)), and presents the Golden Rule: "whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them, for this is the Law and the Prophets" ([Matthew 7:12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#12)). Jesus commands, "Enter by the narrow gate" ([Matthew 7:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#13)), warning that "the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many" ([Matthew 7:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#13)). He warns against false prophets ([7:15-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#15)) and declares that many will claim, "Lord, Lord," but only "the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven" will enter the kingdom ([Matthew 7:21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#21)). The discourse concludes with the parable of the wise and foolish builders ([7:24-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/7#24)), emphasizing that hearing and doing Jesus' words provides secure foundation.

**Chapters 8-9: Mighty Works and Growing Opposition**: Jesus demonstrates His authority through ten miracles: cleansing a leper ([8:1-4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#1)), healing the centurion's servant ([8:5-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#5)), healing Peter's mother-in-law and many others ([8:14-17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#14)), calming the storm ([8:23-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#23)), casting out demons from the Gadarene demoniacs ([8:28-34](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/8#28)), healing a paralytic ([9:1-8](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#1)), raising Jairus' daughter ([9:18-26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#18)), healing the woman with a discharge of blood ([9:20-22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#20)), healing two blind men ([9:27-31](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#27)), and healing a mute demoniac ([9:32-34](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#32)).

Jesus calls Matthew the tax collector with the simple command, "Follow me" ([Matthew 9:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#9)). When Pharisees question His association with sinners, Jesus declares, "Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick" ([Matthew 9:12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#12)), commanding them, "Go and learn what this means, 'I desire mercy, and not sacrifice.' For I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" ([Matthew 9:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/9#13), quoting [Hosea 6:6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/hosea/6#6)).

**Chapter 10: The Mission Discourse**: Jesus commissions the Twelve, giving them "authority over unclean spirits, to cast them out, and to heal every disease and every affliction" ([Matthew 10:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#1)). He commands them: "Go nowhere among the Gentiles and enter no town of the Samaritans, but go rather to the lost sheep of the house of Israel" ([Matthew 10:5-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#5)). Their message: "The kingdom of heaven is at hand" ([Matthew 10:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#7)). Jesus instructs, "Heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse lepers, cast out demons. You received without paying; give without pay" ([Matthew 10:8](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#8)).

Jesus warns of coming persecution: "Behold, I am sending you out as sheep in the midst of wolves, so be wise as serpents and innocent as doves" ([Matthew 10:16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#16)). He promises, "do not be anxious how you are to speak or what you are to say, for what you are to say will be given to you in that hour. For it is not you who speak, but the Spirit of your Father speaking through you" ([Matthew 10:19-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#19)). Jesus declares, "Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell" ([Matthew 10:28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#28)). He warns, "whoever denies me before men, I also will deny before my Father who is in heaven" ([Matthew 10:33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#33)), yet promises, "Whoever receives you receives me, and whoever receives me receives him who sent me" ([Matthew 10:40](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/10#40)).

**Chapters 11-12: Mounting Opposition**: When John sends disciples asking if Jesus is "the one who is to come," Jesus responds by pointing to His works fulfilling Isaiah's prophecies ([Matthew 11:2-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/11#2)). Jesus testifies about John, declaring, "among those born of women there has arisen no one greater than John the Baptist" ([Matthew 11:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/11#11)), yet "the one who is least in the kingdom of heaven is greater than he" ([Matthew 11:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/11#11)).

Jesus pronounces woes on unrepentant cities ([Matthew 11:20-24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/11#20)), then issues His gracious invitation: "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me, for I am gentle and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls. For my yoke is easy, and my burden is light" ([Matthew 11:28-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/11#28)).

Conflict escalates over Sabbath observance ([Matthew 12:1-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#1)). Jesus declares, "the Son of Man is lord of the Sabbath" ([Matthew 12:8](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#8)). When Pharisees accuse Him of casting out demons by Beelzebul, Jesus warns against blasphemy of the Holy Spirit: "whoever speaks against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven, either in this age or in the age to come" ([Matthew 12:32](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#32)). He teaches that "out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaks" ([Matthew 12:34](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#34)), warning, "on the day of judgment people will give account for every careless word they speak" ([Matthew 12:36](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/12#36)).

**Chapter 13: The Parables Discourse**: Jesus presents seven parables revealing kingdom mysteries. The parable of the sower ([13:1-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#1)) teaches that the word produces varying results based on receptivity. Jesus explains that He speaks in parables to reveal truth to disciples while concealing it from hard-hearted hearers ([Matthew 13:10-17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#10)). The parables of the weeds ([13:24-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#24), explained in [13:36-43](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#36)), the mustard seed ([13:31-32](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#31)), and the leaven ([13:33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#33)) illustrate the kingdom's nature and growth. The parables of the hidden treasure ([13:44](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#44)) and the pearl of great value ([13:45-46](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#45)) demonstrate the kingdom's surpassing worth. The parable of the net ([13:47-50](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/13#47)) depicts final judgment separating righteous from wicked.

**Chapters 14-17: Miracles and Revelation**: John the Baptist's beheading ([14:1-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#1)) demonstrates opposition's intensity. Jesus feeds five thousand ([14:13-21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#13)), walks on water ([14:22-33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#22)), and heals many ([14:34-36](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/14#34)). Conflict with Pharisees continues over tradition ([15:1-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#1)). Jesus teaches, "it is not what goes into the mouth that defiles a person, but what comes out of the mouth; this defiles a person" ([Matthew 15:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#11)).

The Canaanite woman's persistent faith receives commendation ([15:21-28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#21)). Jesus feeds four thousand ([15:32-39](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/15#32)) and warns against the leaven of the Pharisees and Sadducees ([16:5-12](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#5)).

At Caesarea Philippi, Peter confesses, "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God" ([Matthew 16:16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#16)). Jesus responds, "Blessed are you, Simon Bar-Jonah! For flesh and blood has not revealed this to you, but my Father who is in heaven. And I tell you, you are Peter, and on this rock I will build my church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it. I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven" ([Matthew 16:17-19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#17)). Jesus begins predicting His death and resurrection ([16:21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#21)), commanding disciples, "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his cross and follow me" ([Matthew 16:24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/16#24)).

The Transfiguration ([17:1-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#1)) reveals Jesus' glory as God declares, "This is my beloved Son, with whom I am well pleased; listen to him" ([Matthew 17:5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#5)). Jesus heals an epileptic boy ([17:14-21](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#14)), again predicts His death ([17:22-23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#22)), and provides temple tax through miraculous means ([17:24-27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/17#24)).

**Chapter 18: The Community Discourse**: Jesus teaches kingdom humility: "unless you turn and become like children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven" ([Matthew 18:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#3)). He warns, "whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a great millstone fastened around his neck and to be drowned in the depth of the sea" ([Matthew 18:6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#6)). The parable of the lost sheep ([18:10-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#10)) demonstrates God's concern for each believer.

Jesus establishes church discipline procedures ([18:15-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#15)), promising, "where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among them" ([Matthew 18:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#20)). He teaches unlimited forgiveness through the parable of the unforgiving servant ([18:21-35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#21)), warning that God will not forgive those who refuse to forgive others ([Matthew 18:35](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/18#35)).

**Chapters 19-23: Journey to Jerusalem and Confrontation**: Jesus teaches God's design for marriage: "Have you not read that he who created them from the beginning made them male and female, and said, 'Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh'? So they are no longer two but one flesh. What therefore God has joined together, let not man separate" ([Matthew 19:4-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#4), quoting [Genesis 1:27](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/genesis/1#27) and [2:24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/genesis/2#24)). He blesses children ([19:13-15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#13)) and teaches the rich young ruler about eternal life ([19:16-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#16)), declaring, "it is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God" ([Matthew 19:24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/19#24)).

The parable of the workers in the vineyard ([20:1-16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/20#1)) teaches kingdom grace. Jesus predicts His death a third time ([20:17-19](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/20#17)) and teaches servant leadership: "whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever would be first among you must be your slave, even as the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" ([Matthew 20:26-28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/20#26)).

Jesus enters Jerusalem triumphantly ([21:1-11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#1)), fulfilling [Zechariah 9:9](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/zechariah/9#9). He cleanses the temple, declaring, "My house shall be called a house of prayer, but you make it a den of robbers" ([Matthew 21:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#13), quoting [Isaiah 56:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/isaiah/56#7) and [Jeremiah 7:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/jeremiah/7#11)). Through parables—the two sons ([21:28-32](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#28)), the wicked tenants ([21:33-46](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/21#33)), and the wedding feast ([22:1-14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#1))—Jesus pronounces judgment on Israel's leaders while warning that "many are called, but few are chosen" ([Matthew 22:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#14)).

Religious leaders test Jesus with questions about taxes ([22:15-22](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#15)), resurrection ([22:23-33](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#23)), and the greatest commandment ([22:34-40](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#34)). Jesus summarizes the Law: "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind. This is the great and first commandment. And a second is like it: You shall love your neighbor as yourself. On these two commandments depend all the Law and the Prophets" ([Matthew 22:37-40](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/22#37), quoting [Deuteronomy 6:5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/deuteronomy/6#5) and [Leviticus 19:18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/leviticus/19#18)).

Matthew 23 contains Jesus' extensive denunciation of the scribes and Pharisees, pronouncing seven woes against their hypocrisy. Jesus condemns them for teaching but not practicing ([23:3](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#3)), burdening others while avoiding burdens ([23:4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#4)), performing religious acts for praise ([23:5-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#5)), shutting the kingdom against people ([23:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#13)), making converts twice the sons of hell ([23:15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#15)), neglecting justice, mercy, and faithfulness while tithing minutiae ([23:23](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#23)), appearing righteous outwardly while full of hypocrisy inwardly ([23:27-28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#27)), and persecuting prophets ([23:29-36](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#29)). Jesus laments over Jerusalem: "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, the city that kills the prophets and stones those who are sent to it! How often would I have gathered your children together as a hen gathers her brood under her wings, and you were not willing!" ([Matthew 23:37](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/23#37)).

**Chapters 24-25: The Eschatological Discourse**: Jesus predicts the temple's destruction ([24:1-2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#1)) and describes signs preceding His return: false messiahs ([24:5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#5), [11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#11), [24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#24)), wars ([24:6-7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#6)), famines and earthquakes ([24:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#7)), persecution ([24:9-10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#9)), and gospel proclamation to all nations ([24:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#14)). Jesus describes the great tribulation ([24:15-28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#15)) and His glorious return: "they will see the Son of Man coming on the clouds of heaven with power and great glory" ([Matthew 24:30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#30)). He commands watchfulness: "stay awake, for you do not know on what day your Lord is coming" ([Matthew 24:42](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#42)).

Three parables emphasize readiness: the faithful and wise servant ([24:45-51](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/24#45)), the ten virgins ([25:1-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#1)), and the talents ([25:14-30](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#14)). The judgment scene ([25:31-46](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#31)) depicts the Son of Man separating sheep from goats based on treatment of "the least of these." The King declares, "Come, you who are blessed by my Father, inherit the kingdom prepared for you from the foundation of the world" ([Matthew 25:34](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#34)) to those who fed the hungry, gave drink to the thirsty, welcomed the stranger, clothed the naked, and visited the sick and imprisoned. To the cursed He says, "Depart from me, you cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels" ([Matthew 25:41](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#41)), concluding, "these will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into eternal life" ([Matthew 25:46](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/25#46)).

**Chapters 26-27: Passion and Death**: Jewish leaders plot Jesus' death ([26:1-5](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#1)). A woman anoints Jesus at Bethany ([26:6-13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#6)); Judas agrees to betray Him for thirty pieces of silver ([26:14-16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#14)). At the Last Supper, Jesus institutes the Lord's Supper, saying, "Take, eat; this is my body" ([Matthew 26:26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#26)) and "Drink of it, all of you, for this is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins" ([Matthew 26:27-28](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#27)).

In Gethsemane, Jesus prays, "My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as you will" ([Matthew 26:39](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#39)). Judas betrays Him with a kiss ([26:47-56](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#47)). Before the high priest, Jesus affirms His identity: "You have said so. But I tell you, from now on you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of Power and coming on the clouds of heaven" ([Matthew 26:64](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#64), referencing [Psalm 110:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/psalms/110#1) and [Daniel 7:13](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/daniel/7#13)). Peter denies Jesus three times ([26:69-75](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/26#69)).

Judas, filled with remorse, returns the thirty pieces of silver and hangs himself ([27:3-10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#3)). Jesus stands before Pilate, who asks, "Are you the King of the Jews?" Jesus answers, "You have said so" ([Matthew 27:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#11)). The crowd chooses Barabbas over Jesus ([27:15-26](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#15)). Soldiers mock Jesus, placing a crown of thorns on His head and a reed in His hand, kneeling before Him saying, "Hail, King of the Jews!" ([Matthew 27:27-31](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#27)).

Jesus is crucified at Golgotha ([27:32-44](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#32)). From the cross He cries, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" ([Matthew 27:46](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#46), quoting [Psalm 22:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/psalms/22#1)). At His death, "the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom. And the earth shook, and the rocks were split" ([Matthew 27:51](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#51)). The centurion declares, "Truly this was the Son of God!" ([Matthew 27:54](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#54)). Joseph of Arimathea buries Jesus in his own new tomb ([27:57-61](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#57)). Religious leaders seal and guard the tomb ([27:62-66](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/27#62)).

**Chapter 28: Resurrection and Commission**: On the third day, an angel rolls back the stone, declaring to the women, "Do not be afraid, for I know that you seek Jesus who was crucified. He is not here, for he has risen, as he said" ([Matthew 28:5-6](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#5)). The angel commands them, "go quickly and tell his disciples that he has risen from the dead" ([Matthew 28:7](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#7)). Jesus appears to the women, greeting them and commanding, "go and tell my brothers to go to Galilee, and there they will see me" ([Matthew 28:10](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#10)).

Guards report to the chief priests, who bribe them to spread the false story that disciples stole the body ([28:11-15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#11)). The Gospel concludes with the Great Commission ([28:16-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#16)). Jesus declares, "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" ([Matthew 28:18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#18)), then commands His disciples: "Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you" ([Matthew 28:19-20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#19)). Jesus' final promise concludes the Gospel: "And behold, I am with you always, to the end of the age" ([Matthew 28:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/matthew/28#20)).

Throughout these 28 chapters, Matthew presents Jesus as the authoritative teacher whose words carry divine authority, the miracle-working Messiah who demonstrates God's kingdom power, the suffering servant who gives His life as a ransom, and the risen Lord who commissions His church to disciple all nations. Every major teaching, command, and promise emphasizes Jesus' role as the fulfillment of God's redemptive purposes and the foundation of the new covenant community.
