# Genesis

## Authorship

The authorship of Genesis has been one of the most debated topics in biblical scholarship, representing a fundamental divide between traditional and critical approaches to understanding the Pentateuch.

### Traditional View: Mosaic Authorship

For centuries, Jewish and Christian traditions have unanimously attributed the authorship of Genesis, along with the other four books of the Pentateuch (Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy), to [Moses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosaic_authorship). This traditional view remained essentially uncontested until the 19th century. Proponents of Mosaic authorship typically date the composition of Genesis to around 1450-1410 BCE, during the forty-year period of Israel's wilderness wanderings following the Exodus from Egypt.

Several lines of evidence support traditional Mosaic authorship:

1. **Biblical Testimony**: Various passages in both the Old and New Testaments attribute the Pentateuch to Moses (Exodus 17:14; 24:4; 34:27; Numbers 33:2; Deuteronomy 31:9; Joshua 1:7-8; 1 Kings 2:3; 2 Chronicles 25:4; Ezra 6:18; Nehemiah 13:1; Mark 12:26; Luke 16:29; John 5:46-47).

2. **Ancient Literary Forms**: Some scholars note that treaty accounts in Genesis follow diplomatic formats used from 1800-1700 BCE, and much of the Pentateuch adheres to treaty patterns from 1400-1200 BCE, which would support composition during Moses' lifetime or shortly thereafter.

3. **Early Jewish and Christian Consensus**: Both [Philo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philo) and [Josephus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josephus), prominent Jewish scholars of the first century CE, affirmed Mosaic authorship, as did early church fathers.

### Critical Scholarship: The Documentary Hypothesis

Beginning in the 17th and 18th centuries, Mosaic authorship began to be questioned by scholars who noticed various textual phenomena that seemed difficult to reconcile with single authorship. By the 19th century, this led to the development of the [Documentary Hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Documentary_hypothesis), which proposed that the Pentateuch was not written by a single author but was compiled from multiple sources over several centuries.

The classic formulation, associated primarily with German scholar [Julius Wellhausen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Wellhausen), identified four main sources:

1. **J (Jahwist/Yahwist)**: Dating to approximately the 9th century BCE in Judah, this source consistently uses the divine name "Yahweh" (YHWH).

2. **E (Elohist)**: Dating to the 8th century BCE in the northern kingdom of Israel, this source prefers the term "Elohim" for God.

3. **D (Deuteronomist)**: Dating to the 7th century BCE, primarily comprising the book of Deuteronomy, possibly originating from reforms under King Josiah.

4. **P (Priestly source)**: Dating to the 5th century BCE during or after the Babylonian exile, characterized by genealogies, ritual laws, and systematic theology.

According to this theory, these sources were later woven together by editors (redactors), with the final form of the Pentateuch emerging during the [Persian period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_ancient_Israel_and_Judah#Persian_period) (539-333 BCE), possibly as late as the 5th century BCE.

### Evidence for Multiple Authorship

Critical scholars cite several types of evidence:

1. **Duplicate Narratives**: Genesis contains parallel accounts of the same events (e.g., two creation stories in Genesis 1-2, three wife-sister narratives involving patriarchs).

2. **Linguistic Variations**: Different names for God, varying vocabulary, and distinct literary styles suggest multiple authors.

3. **Chronological Anachronisms**: References to Philistines (Genesis 21:32) and other peoples who may not have been present in the patriarchal period.

4. **Development of Hebrew Language**: Historical linguistic evidence suggests that certain Hebrew forms in Genesis developed after Moses' time.

### Current State of Scholarship

The Documentary Hypothesis dominated biblical scholarship for most of the 20th century and was "almost universally accepted" during that period. However, contemporary scholarship has become more nuanced and diverse. While the classic four-source JEDP model is now being reconsidered in its specifics, most critical scholars continue to recognize:

1. **Deuteronomy as a Distinct Source**: There is widespread agreement that Deuteronomy originated from law-codes produced at the court of King Josiah.

2. **Some Form of Priestly Source**: Most scholars acknowledge a Priestly source, though its exact extent remains debated.

3. **Final Redaction in Persian Period**: There is general consensus that the Pentateuch reached its final form during the Persian period (539-333 BCE), based on earlier traditions and sources.

However, it should be noted that there is currently "general non-consensus among biblical scholars working in Pentateuchal criticism," and the field is characterized by ongoing debate and revision of earlier theories. The trend in recent scholarship is to recognize the final form of the Torah as a literary and ideological unity, while acknowledging its complex compositional history.
