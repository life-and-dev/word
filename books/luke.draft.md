# Luke

## Authorship

The Gospel of Luke, the longest of the four canonical Gospels and the first volume of a two-part work (Luke-Acts), has been traditionally attributed to Luke, a physician and companion of the Apostle Paul. While the text itself is anonymous, early church testimony consistently identifies Luke as the author, and substantial internal evidence supports this attribution. However, modern critical scholarship debates the historical accuracy of this tradition, particularly regarding the author's relationship to Paul and the work's dating. This section examines the evidence for and against Lukan authorship, the work's dating, and the author's identity and background.

**Traditional Attribution**: Early Christian tradition uniformly attributes the third Gospel to Luke. The earliest explicit attribution comes from Irenaeus of Lyons (c. 180 CE), who writes: "Luke also, the companion of Paul, recorded in a book the Gospel preached by him" (Against Heresies 3.1.1). Irenaeus's testimony carries weight as he claimed connection to Polycarp, who knew the apostle John, potentially preserving second-century tradition. The Muratorian Fragment (c. 170-200 CE), listing canonical books, states: "The third book of the Gospel, that according to Luke. This physician Luke after Christ's ascension, as Paul had taken him with him as an expert in the way [of the teaching], composed it in his own name according to [his] thinking." This earliest known canonical list thus identifies Luke as physician, Paul's companion, and author writing under his own authority. The Anti-Marcionite Prologue to Luke (late second century, though dating disputed) similarly identifies the author as Luke from Syrian Antioch, a physician, Paul's companion, who wrote in Achaia and died unmarried at age 84. Tertullian (c. 200 CE) refers to Luke's Gospel as illuminated by Paul's authority. Origen (c. 185-254 CE) and Eusebius (c. 260-340 CE) consistently attribute the Gospel to Luke without hint of controversy.

This unanimous early testimony is significant. Unlike disputed books (Hebrews, 2 Peter, Revelation), Luke faced no authorship controversy in the early church. The attribution to Luke—a relatively minor figure, not an apostle—argues for authenticity. If the church were inventing apostolic attributions, selecting Luke rather than an apostle or more prominent figure seems unlikely. The criterion of embarrassment suggests the tradition preserves genuine memory rather than pious invention.

**Internal Evidence from the Prologue**: Luke opens with a formal literary prologue unique among the Gospels: "Inasmuch as many have undertaken to compile a narrative of the things that have been accomplished among us, just as those who from the beginning were eyewitnesses and ministers of the word have delivered them to us, it seemed good to me also, having followed all things closely for some time past, to write an orderly account for you, most excellent Theophilus, that you may have certainty concerning the things you have been taught" ([Luke 1:1-4](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/1#1)). This prologue establishes several key points: (1) The author is not an eyewitness to Jesus' ministry but depends on eyewitness testimony transmitted through tradition. This fits Luke, a second-generation Christian and Paul's companion rather than one of the Twelve. (2) The author has carefully researched sources, investigating "all things closely" (παρηκολουθηκότι ἄνωθεν πᾶσιν ἀκριβῶς). This systematic approach suggests education and historiographical intent. (3) The work addresses "most excellent Theophilus," using a formal title suggesting high social status. The dedication to a patron fits Greco-Roman literary conventions and suggests the author moved in educated circles. (4) The purpose is to provide "certainty" (ἀσφάλειαν) about Christian teaching—apologetic and didactic aims compatible with Luke's traditional role as Paul's companion introducing Gentiles to Christianity.

**The "We" Passages in Acts**: The strongest internal evidence for identifying the author comes from Acts, the Gospel's sequel. Three passages in Acts shift from third-person to first-person plural ("we"): [Acts 16:10-17](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/16#10) (Troas to Philippi), [Acts 20:5-21:18](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/20#5) (return to Jerusalem), and [Acts 27:1-28:16](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/27#1) (voyage to Rome). These passages suggest the author personally accompanied Paul during these portions of his journeys. If the same author wrote Luke and Acts (virtually undisputed based on style, theology, and Luke 1:1-4 and Acts 1:1-2 explicitly linking the works), the "we" passages identify the author as Paul's traveling companion. Among Paul's companions mentioned in his letters, Luke fits best. [Colossians 4:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/colossians/4#14) identifies "Luke the beloved physician" as with Paul during imprisonment. [Philemon 24](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/philemon/1#24) calls Luke a "fellow worker." [2 Timothy 4:11](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/2timothy/4#11) states "Luke alone is with me," placing him with Paul during his final imprisonment. The "we" passages' chronology aligns with times when Luke could have been present, particularly the final journey to Rome for Paul's appeal to Caesar.

However, critical scholars question this straightforward reading. Some argue: (1) The "we" passages may be literary convention borrowed from sources (a travel diary incorporated without fully converting to third person) rather than indicating authorship. Ancient historians sometimes used first-person for dramatic effect without personal presence. (2) The author of Acts demonstrates geographical and chronological errors regarding Paul's missionary journeys, surprising if written by Paul's actual companion. (3) Acts' theology differs significantly from Paul's letters—particularly regarding Law observance, salvation mechanics, and christology—suggesting the author didn't know Paul intimately. (4) The "we" passages may be rhetorical device creating verisimilitude rather than genuine memoir. Despite these objections, the traditional interpretation—that the author personally accompanied Paul during these journeys—remains defensible and widely held, though no longer commanding universal scholarly consensus.

**The Medical Language Debate**: Older scholarship frequently argued that Luke's vocabulary demonstrates medical knowledge, supporting identification with Luke the physician ([Colossians 4:14](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/colossians/4#14)). William Ramsay, Adolf von Harnack, and others cataloged supposedly technical medical terms. However, Henry Cadbury's landmark study (1926) demonstrated that Luke's vocabulary, while educated, was not distinctively medical. The terms cited occur commonly in educated Hellenistic Greek literature without medical specialization. Luke's vocabulary demonstrates literary education and familiarity with Septuagintal Greek but doesn't prove medical training. Modern scholarship generally rejects the "medical language" argument as decisive evidence. Nevertheless, some scholars note that Luke demonstrates interest in healing narratives, often providing medical details absent from parallel accounts (e.g., [Luke 4:38](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/4#38) specifies Peter's mother-in-law suffered from "high fever"), potentially consistent with medical interest if not proving medical profession.

**Stylistic and Theological Unity with Acts**: The stylistic, linguistic, and theological unity between Luke's Gospel and Acts strongly supports common authorship. Both works: (1) Demonstrate sophisticated Greek literary style, the most polished in the New Testament, with complex periodic sentences and classical vocabulary. (2) Use similar vocabulary, grammatical constructions, and stylistic patterns throughout. (3) Share theological emphases: Holy Spirit's role, prayer's importance, concern for poor and marginalized, women's prominence, universal salvation, Jerusalem's centrality. (4) Employ similar narrative techniques and literary structure (geographical progression, parallel episodes). (5) Contain explicit literary links: Luke concludes with ascension and promise of Holy Spirit; Acts opens with the same events and moves forward. The prologue to Acts explicitly references "the first book" (Acts 1:1), clearly referring to Luke's Gospel. This two-volume unity is virtually undisputed in scholarship. The question becomes: who is this single author of Luke-Acts?

**Dating Evidence**: Determining Luke's date affects authorship questions. Conservative scholars often date Luke to the early 60s CE, before Paul's martyrdom (c. 64-67 CE), noting that Acts ends with Paul under house arrest in Rome without recording his death—suggesting composition before that event. If Luke wrote while Paul lived, identification with Paul's companion becomes more plausible. However, most critical scholars date Luke to the 70s or 80s CE based on several factors: (1) Luke's use of Mark as a source. If Markan priority holds (scholarly consensus), and Mark dates to around 70 CE, Luke must be later—probably 75-85 CE. (2) Luke's apparent knowledge of Jerusalem's destruction in 70 CE, reflected in modifications to Mark's apocalyptic discourse. [Luke 21:20](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/luke/21#20) ("But when you see Jerusalem surrounded by armies") seems to describe historical events rather than purely prophetic vision, contrasting with Mark's "desolating sacrilege" (Mark 13:14). (3) The prologue's reference to "many" previous written accounts suggests developed written tradition, pointing to later date. (4) Theological development, particularly regarding delayed parousia and church organization, suggests later generation. (5) Acts' ending doesn't necessarily indicate composition during Paul's imprisonment; the author may have chosen to end with Paul preaching in Rome as a fitting theological conclusion regardless of later events.

The dating debate significantly impacts authorship. A date in the 60s CE makes Lukan authorship (Luke as Paul's companion) historically plausible. A date in the 80s CE creates difficulties: Luke would have been quite elderly by then, raising questions about whether Paul's companion would still be active. However, even a later date doesn't absolutely exclude Lukan authorship—merely makes it less certain. The strongest external testimony (Irenaeus, Muratorian Fragment) comes from the late second century, potentially reflecting accurate tradition about a work written decades earlier.

**Arguments Against Lukan Authorship**: Modern critical scholarship presents several arguments questioning traditional attribution: (1) **Theological differences from Paul's letters**: Acts portrays Paul differently than Paul presents himself. Acts' Paul is a wonder-worker and powerful orator; Paul's letters emphasize weakness and unappealing speech. Acts presents smoother relationships between Paul and Jerusalem apostles than Paul's letters suggest (compare [Acts 15](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/15#1) with [Galatians 2](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/galatians/2#1)). Most significantly, Paul's central theological themes—justification by faith, Law's obsolescence, forensic righteousness—receive muted treatment in Luke-Acts, replaced with emphasis on repentance, forgiveness, and Holy Spirit baptism. Would Paul's close companion miss or ignore these central Pauline emphases? (2) **Historical inaccuracies**: Critics note alleged errors in Acts regarding Paul's movements, Roman governance, and Palestinian geography, unexpected if the author personally accompanied Paul. (3) **Literary dependence on Mark**: Luke's extensive use of Mark suggests the author compiled sources rather than relying on firsthand apostolic testimony, raising questions about proximity to events. (4) **Anachronisms and theological development**: The work's theology, church organization references, and treatment of Law observance reflect later second-generation concerns rather than the apostolic period.

**Alternative Theories**: Some scholars propose alternative authorship theories: (1) **Anonymous Pauline-circle author**: The author was someone connected to Pauline Christianity but not necessarily the Luke mentioned in Paul's letters—perhaps a later admirer compiling traditions. (2) **Lucius of Cyrene**: [Acts 13:1](https://eternal.family.net.za/bible/books/acts/13#1) mentions Lucius, possibly the author, leading to confusion with Luke. (3) **Intentional pseudonymity**: A second-century author wrote under Luke's name to gain apostolic authority, a common ancient practice. However, these alternatives face difficulties explaining the unanimous early attribution to Luke specifically, the "we" passages' purpose, and the lack of any competing tradition.

**Scholarly Consensus and Nuances**: Contemporary scholarship presents a spectrum of views: (1) **Traditional view**: Luke the physician, Paul's companion, wrote Luke-Acts in the 60s or early 70s, using Mark and other sources. Held by many evangelical and some mainstream scholars. (2) **Modified traditional view**: Luke (Paul's companion) wrote in the 70s-80s, accounting for some theological development and Mark's use while maintaining essential reliability. (3) **Anonymous author in Pauline tradition**: An unknown author from Paul's circle or influenced by Pauline Christianity wrote Luke-Acts, perhaps with access to some Pauline tradition, but not Paul's intimate companion. The early church accurately identified connection to Pauline tradition but imprecisely specified "Luke the physician" as author. (4) **Late anonymous author**: A second-generation or third-generation Christian unconnected to Paul wrote Luke-Acts in the 80s-90s CE, with early church tradition inaccurately attributing it to Luke. The author demonstrates literary skill, theological sophistication, and Hellenistic education but no direct apostolic connection.

**Conclusion**: The question of Lukan authorship involves weighing early unanimous tradition (favoring Luke) against critical analysis of theology, style, and historical reliability (raising questions). The traditional attribution to Luke enjoys strong external support from early church testimony and reasonable internal support from the "we" passages in Acts. The lack of any competing ancient tradition and the attribution to a non-apostle support authenticity. However, theological differences from Paul's letters, later dating by most scholars, and historical-critical concerns create genuine difficulties. Modern scholarship leans toward skepticism about identifying the author as Paul's literal companion, preferring "anonymous author from Pauline tradition" or simply "anonymous but early and reliable." Nevertheless, even critical scholars acknowledge substantial historical value in Luke-Acts and recognize that the traditional attribution, while unprovable, remains plausible. The church's historical identification of Luke as author, whether strictly accurate or approximating a more complex reality, reflects early recognition of the work's connection to apostolic tradition and its authoritative place in Christian Scripture.
