# The Books of Maccabees (1-4 Maccabees)

## Authorship

The four Books of Maccabees represent distinct literary works written by different anonymous authors across different time periods and geographical locations, each with unique theological and literary perspectives.

### 1 Maccabees

The author of [1 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Maccabees) remains anonymous and unknown to scholars. However, based on internal evidence and the book's perspective, scholars have drawn several conclusions about the author's identity and background. The author was likely a **Palestinian Jew** who was fluent in Hebrew and Aramaic, writing in the newly independent Hasmonean kingdom during the reign of High Priest John Hyrcanus I (reigned approximately 135/134–104 BCE). Some scholars suggest the author may have been the **Hasmonean court historian** or someone with an equivalent official position, given his access to official documents and his distinctly pro-Hasmonean perspective.

The book was **originally composed in Hebrew**, as attested by both Origen and Jerome, who claimed to have seen Hebrew manuscripts. However, this original Hebrew version has been lost, and the work survives only in its Greek translation preserved in the Septuagint. The [Jewish Encyclopedia](https://www.jewishencyclopedia.com/articles/10237-maccabees-books-of) notes that the primary reason for the disappearance of Hebrew versions has to do with the unpopularity of the Hasmonean dynasty in the centuries following the Maccabean Revolt.

Scholars universally agree that 1 Maccabees was **written before 63 BCE**, as the author displays great admiration toward Rome and shows no knowledge of the Roman general Pompey conquering Jerusalem and reducing the Hasmonean kingdom to a client state of the Roman Republic in that year. The author's nationalism and the exaggerated importance given to Judean events have led some scholars to question his objectivity, particularly noting his anti-Seleucid bias. Nevertheless, the [historical integrity of the book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Books-of-the-Maccabees) is generally respected, as it was compiled from official written sources, oral tradition, and eyewitness reporting, with an absence of most conventions of the Hellenistic rhetorical school of historiography.

The work is distinctly **pro-Hasmonean**, propagating what some scholars call "Hasmonean propaganda" in upholding the righteousness of the Hasmoneans and their just claim to rule. The author seeks to promote the view that the Hasmoneans were God's newly chosen rulers in line with the heroes of the Hebrew Bible, regarding them as chosen by Providence "to give deliverance unto Israel." Some scholarly debate exists about the sectarian identification of the author—Geiger's thesis that 1 Maccabees was Sadducean did not win much support, as the work does not explicitly mention Zadokites, Sadducees, or Pharisees.

### 2 Maccabees

The author of [2 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Maccabees) is also not identified, but he explicitly claims to be **abridging a five-volume work by Jason of Cyrene**. This makes 2 Maccabees unique among the Maccabean books in that it acknowledges its source material, though Jason's original work has been lost. The author was an **unknown diaspora Jew living in Hellenistic Egypt**, writing in Greek with no evidence of an earlier Hebrew version.

Scholars suggest 2 Maccabees was composed between **150–100 BCE**, though some argue for dates as late as 124 BCE based on letters inserted at the beginning of the book (dated to 188 Seleucid Era, or ~124 BCE). The book must have been written before 40 CE and certainly before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. Unlike 1 Maccabees, this work shows less concern for political history and more interest in theological reflection, miraculous interventions, and the temple cult, reflecting its different authorial perspective and audience.

### 3 Maccabees

[3 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Maccabees) was written by an **Alexandrian Jew** who composed the work in Greek. All scholars agree on the Greek composition, which is evident from the free, idiomatic, and rather bombastic character of the language in the Septuagint. The book is actually **misnamed**, as it has nothing to do with the Maccabees or the Maccabean Revolt. Instead, it recounts a story of persecution of Jews in Egypt under Ptolemy IV Philopator (222–205 BCE).

Though it contains some historical elements, the story is largely considered **historical fiction** rather than historical record. The precise date of authorship is unknown, but the widest plausible range is between **100 BCE and 70 CE**. [Some scholars](https://www.internationalstandardbible.com/M/maccabees-books-of-3-5.html), including Ewald, Hausrath, and Reuss, believed it was written during the reign of Emperor Caligula (37–41 CE), when Alexandrian Jews faced persecution, suggesting the book was written to comfort and encourage Jews during that crisis. Neither 3 nor 4 Maccabees has any claim to canonicity in most Christian traditions, though 3 Maccabees received favorable consideration in some Eastern Churches for a time and is included in some Orthodox canons.

### 4 Maccabees

[4 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Maccabees) is fundamentally different in genre from the other books—it is a **Jewish-Stoic philosophical treatise** or sermon on the question of "Whether pious reason is absolute master of the passions." The work uses the martyrdom stories of Eleazar and the seven brothers from 2 Maccabees 6:18–7 to illustrate the author's philosophical thesis.

The authorship was once incorrectly ascribed to **Josephus** by Eusebius, Jerome, and others, but this attribution is universally rejected by modern scholars. The author was probably a **diaspora Jew**, though the specific location is debated. The author was likely not from Roman Judea, as the book includes a minor geographical mistake about the layout of Jerusalem, and the tone suggests the defense of a cultural minority in a Hellenistic environment. While Alexandria was the largest center of literary Greek Jewish works in the era, scholars such as Hans Freudenthal and Eduard Norden argue that the religious outlook of the book does not easily match Alexandrian Jewish thought.

The **first century CE** is generally accepted as the date of composition, primarily because the book must have been written before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. The work has a clearly **Stoic stamp**, seeking to demonstrate that "pious reason exercises mastery over the emotions" (4 Maccabees 1:1). The adjective "pious" is critically important: the author is modifying the common Stoic topic ("reason can master the emotions") to suggest that it is specifically the mind trained in Jewish piety and the practices of the Jewish Law that is equipped to exercise the mastery that Greek ethicists praised. This represents a sophisticated synthesis of Greek philosophical thought with Jewish theology.

In summary, the four Books of Maccabees represent diverse authorial perspectives: 1 Maccabees as Palestinian court history, 2 Maccabees as diaspora theological history, 3 Maccabees as Alexandrian didactic fiction, and 4 Maccabees as philosophical-religious discourse. Their anonymous authors wrote from different locations (Palestine and the diaspora), in different languages (Hebrew and Greek), at different times (spanning roughly 150 BCE to 70 CE), and for different purposes, making them a rich collection of Second Temple Jewish literature rather than a unified work.

## Historical Environment

The Books of Maccabees emerge from vastly different historical environments spanning nearly three centuries of Jewish experience under Hellenistic and Roman rule. Each book reflects the unique political, cultural, and religious conditions of its particular setting.

### 1 Maccabees: The Maccabean Revolt and Hasmonean Independence (175-63 BCE)

[1 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maccabean_Revolt) was written during the **Hasmonean dynasty** (c. 141-37 BCE), specifically during the reign of High Priest John Hyrcanus I (135/134-104 BCE). However, the book narrates events from an earlier period—the Maccabean Revolt that began in 167 BCE and the subsequent establishment of Jewish independence.

**The Seleucid Crisis (175-167 BCE):** The historical environment begins with Judea under the control of the [Seleucid Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasmonean_dynasty), one of the successor kingdoms to Alexander the Great's empire. The Seleucid king **Antiochus IV Epiphanes** (175-164 BCE) initiated an aggressive program of Hellenization, attempting to unify his empire culturally and religiously. Most modern scholars argue that the king was intervening in a **civil war between traditionalist Jews** in the countryside and **Hellenized Jews** in Jerusalem, who had adopted Greek customs, language, and some religious practices.

In 167 BCE, Antiochus escalated his policies dramatically. He outlawed core Jewish practices including circumcision, Sabbath observance, and dietary laws. Most infamously, he desecrated the Second Temple in Jerusalem by dedicating it to Zeus Olympios and sacrificing pigs on the altar—an act known as the "abomination of desolation." This persecution sparked the Maccabean Revolt when a priest named Mattathias from the village of Modein refused to offer pagan sacrifice and killed both a fellow Jew who was about to comply and the Seleucid official overseeing the ceremony.

**The Revolt and Independence (167-141 BCE):** Led initially by Mattathias and then by his son **Judah Maccabee** ("the Hammer"), the rebellion became a guerrilla war against Seleucid forces. The Maccabees successfully reclaimed and rededicated the Temple in 164 BCE—an event commemorated in the Jewish festival of Hanukkah. The struggle continued until 141 BCE, when Simon Maccabeus achieved full political independence and was recognized as high priest and ethnarch, establishing the Hasmonean dynasty.

**The Author's Context:** Writing during the reign of John Hyrcanus I, the author lived in a newly independent Jewish state that had expanded its territory significantly. [Between c. 141 and c. 116 BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasmonean_dynasty), the dynasty ruled Judea semi-autonomously within the Seleucid sphere, and from roughly 110 BCE gained further autonomy and expanded into neighboring regions. The political environment was characterized by ongoing conflicts between the Hasmonean high priests and kings versus the Zadokite priestly establishment, the Pharisees, and the Essenes who seceded from the Jerusalem temple. The **Pharisees and Sadducees** were rival political-religious factions throughout the Hasmonean period, creating internal tensions within Jewish society.

**Audience:** [1 Maccabees has a Judean orientation](https://www.thetorah.com/article/judea-versus-judaism-between-1-and-2-maccabees) and was meant to serve the interests of the Hasmonean dynasty that ruled Judea. It functions as Hasmonean propaganda in its editorial slant on events, presenting the ruling family as divinely chosen deliverers of Israel. The book shows great admiration toward Rome (which had not yet conquered Jerusalem) and presents the Hasmoneans in the tradition of biblical judges and kings.

### 2 Maccabees: Diaspora Perspective on the Revolt (150-100 BCE)

[2 Maccabees](https://www.thetorah.com/article/judea-versus-judaism-between-1-and-2-maccabees) covers much of the same historical period as 1 Maccabees (175-161 BCE) but was written from a very different geographical and ideological context. The author was a **diaspora Jew in Hellenistic Egypt**, likely in Alexandria, writing between 150-100 BCE.

**Different Geographic Setting:** Unlike 1 Maccabees' Palestinian focus, 2 Maccabees is a **diasporic work** interested in "Judaism" as a religious and cultural identity—a word that appears several times in 2 Maccabees but is absent from 1 Maccabees. The author's concern was not primarily the political independence of Judea but rather the preservation of Jewish religious identity and practice in a Hellenistic environment.

**Cultural Environment:** The author lived in Ptolemaic Egypt, where Jews formed a significant minority population, especially in Alexandria. This environment required Jews to negotiate their identity in relation to Greek culture while maintaining their distinct religious traditions. The book reflects this tension by emphasizing theological themes, divine intervention, miracles, and the sanctity of the Temple cult—concerns particularly relevant to diaspora Jews separated from the Jerusalem Temple.

**Audience:** The book was addressed to diaspora Jewish communities, particularly those facing questions about how to maintain Jewish identity in Hellenistic contexts. Its emphasis on martyrdom, resurrection, and divine judgment provided theological resources for Jews under cultural and occasionally physical pressure to assimilate.

### 3 Maccabees: Ptolemaic Egypt and Fictional History (100 BCE-70 CE)

[3 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Maccabees) is misnamed, as it has nothing to do with the Maccabees or the Maccabean period. Instead, it purportedly narrates events from **Ptolemaic Egypt** during the reign of [Ptolemy IV Philopator](https://www.jewishencyclopedia.com/articles/12423-ptolemy-iv) (222-205 BCE), though the book was actually composed much later, between 100 BCE and 70 CE.

**The Narrative Setting:** The story claims that after Ptolemy IV's victory against Seleucid king Antiochus III at the **Battle of Raphia in 217 BCE**, the pharaoh visited Jerusalem and the Second Temple, wishing to enter the Holy of Holies. When miraculously prevented from entering, he became enraged and returned to Alexandria determined to persecute Egyptian Jews. He allegedly ordered all Jews in the kingdom to be registered, taxed as slaves, and forced to worship Dionysus or face death. The book describes Jews being rounded up to be trampled by intoxicated elephants in the hippodrome, only to be saved by divine intervention.

**Historical Reality vs. Fiction:** No other ancient sources describe such a persecution under Ptolemy IV Philopator. [Most scholars classify the book as romantic fiction](https://www.newworldencyclopedia.org/entry/3_Maccabees), often linking it to the genre of Greek romance. However, while many elements are dubious, scholars acknowledge that memories of a genuine persecution might underlie the narrative, though distorted. **Josephus** records that many Jews were executed in Alexandria under **Ptolemy VIII Physcon** (146-117 BCE) due to their support for Cleopatra II, and this execution was indeed carried out by intoxicated elephants. Some scholars believe the author of 3 Maccabees transferred this historical incident to an earlier period and added a fictitious connection to Jerusalem.

**Author's Context:** The author was an **Alexandrian Jew** writing during a period when the Jewish community in Egypt faced periodic tensions with both Greek neighbors and Ptolemaic (and later Roman) authorities. The book was likely written to comfort and encourage Alexandrian Jews during times of persecution, possibly during the reign of Emperor **Caligula** (37-41 CE), when severe anti-Jewish violence occurred in Alexandria. The story purports to explain the origin of a Purim-like festival celebrated by Egyptian Jews.

### 4 Maccabees: Roman Period Philosophical Discourse (20-130 CE)

[4 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Maccabees) was composed in the **first century CE** during the Roman period, with scholars like Elias J. Bickerman suggesting a date between 20-54 CE, possibly around 40 CE during the reign of Emperor **Caligula**. The book must have been written before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE.

**The Roman Imperial Context:** By this period, Judea had been under direct or indirect Roman control for over a century (since Pompey's conquest in 63 BCE). Jews throughout the Mediterranean lived as a minority under Roman rule, negotiating between maintaining Jewish identity and participating in the dominant Greco-Roman culture. The author was probably not from Judea but from the **diaspora**, as evidenced by a minor geographical error about Jerusalem's layout and the book's sophisticated engagement with Greek philosophical discourse.

**Hellenistic Judaism:** The historical environment was characterized by mature **Hellenistic Judaism**—Jewish communities that had developed sophisticated ways of expressing Jewish theology using Greek philosophical categories. [While 2 Maccabees presented Judaism as the antithesis to Hellenism](https://www.thetorah.com/article/2-and-4-maccabees-evolving-responses-to-hellenism), 4 Maccabees uses Hellenistic ideas to encourage Jews to hold fast to their ancestral faith. The work defends the merits of Judaism in a Hellenized world and is written in fluent, complicated Greek using the rhetorical argumentative style characteristic of the period.

**Martyrdom and Philosophy:** The book elaborates on the martyrdom stories from 2 Maccabees—the deaths of the scribe Eleazar and the mother with seven sons under Antiochus IV Epiphanes. However, the author's concern is not primarily historical but philosophical and theological. **First-century Jews**—both those who followed Jesus and those who did not—shared common views on martyrdom that became very important in early Christianity. 4 Maccabees develops the notion of **substitutionary atonement**: the martyrs suffer to arouse God to forgive the sins of the rest of the people, a concept that would be central to Christian theology.

**Cultural Setting:** The book reflects a multicultural urban environment where Jews needed to articulate their distinctive identity while engaging constructively with the dominant culture. The author synthesizes **Stoic philosophy** with Jewish theology, arguing that Torah observance produces the rational self-mastery that Greek philosophers praised. This represents a sophisticated apologetic strategy: rather than rejecting Greek culture entirely, the author argues that authentic Judaism produces the very virtues that Greek philosophy values.

### Summary of Historical Environments

The four Books of Maccabees span nearly three centuries of Jewish history under Hellenistic and Roman rule:

- **1 Maccabees** (written c. 135-104 BCE): Hasmonean independence, court propaganda, Palestinian Jewish nationalism
- **2 Maccabees** (written c. 150-100 BCE): Diaspora perspective, theological reflection, emphasis on temple and martyrdom
- **3 Maccabees** (written c. 100 BCE-70 CE): Alexandrian Jewish fiction, response to persecution, liturgical origins
- **4 Maccabees** (written c. 20-70 CE): Roman period philosophical discourse, Hellenistic Judaism, martyrdom theology

Together, they provide a window into how Jewish communities in different times and places understood their history, articulated their identity, and responded to the challenges of maintaining distinctive religious practices under foreign rule and cultural pressure.
