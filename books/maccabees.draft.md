# The Books of Maccabees (1-4 Maccabees)

## Authorship

The four Books of Maccabees represent distinct literary works written by different anonymous authors across different time periods and geographical locations, each with unique theological and literary perspectives.

### 1 Maccabees

The author of [1 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Maccabees) remains anonymous and unknown to scholars. However, based on internal evidence and the book's perspective, scholars have drawn several conclusions about the author's identity and background. The author was likely a **Palestinian Jew** who was fluent in Hebrew and Aramaic, writing in the newly independent Hasmonean kingdom during the reign of High Priest John Hyrcanus I (reigned approximately 135/134–104 BCE). Some scholars suggest the author may have been the **Hasmonean court historian** or someone with an equivalent official position, given his access to official documents and his distinctly pro-Hasmonean perspective.

The book was **originally composed in Hebrew**, as attested by both Origen and Jerome, who claimed to have seen Hebrew manuscripts. However, this original Hebrew version has been lost, and the work survives only in its Greek translation preserved in the Septuagint. The [Jewish Encyclopedia](https://www.jewishencyclopedia.com/articles/10237-maccabees-books-of) notes that the primary reason for the disappearance of Hebrew versions has to do with the unpopularity of the Hasmonean dynasty in the centuries following the Maccabean Revolt.

Scholars universally agree that 1 Maccabees was **written before 63 BCE**, as the author displays great admiration toward Rome and shows no knowledge of the Roman general Pompey conquering Jerusalem and reducing the Hasmonean kingdom to a client state of the Roman Republic in that year. The author's nationalism and the exaggerated importance given to Judean events have led some scholars to question his objectivity, particularly noting his anti-Seleucid bias. Nevertheless, the [historical integrity of the book](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Books-of-the-Maccabees) is generally respected, as it was compiled from official written sources, oral tradition, and eyewitness reporting, with an absence of most conventions of the Hellenistic rhetorical school of historiography.

The work is distinctly **pro-Hasmonean**, propagating what some scholars call "Hasmonean propaganda" in upholding the righteousness of the Hasmoneans and their just claim to rule. The author seeks to promote the view that the Hasmoneans were God's newly chosen rulers in line with the heroes of the Hebrew Bible, regarding them as chosen by Providence "to give deliverance unto Israel." Some scholarly debate exists about the sectarian identification of the author—Geiger's thesis that 1 Maccabees was Sadducean did not win much support, as the work does not explicitly mention Zadokites, Sadducees, or Pharisees.

### 2 Maccabees

The author of [2 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_Maccabees) is also not identified, but he explicitly claims to be **abridging a five-volume work by Jason of Cyrene**. This makes 2 Maccabees unique among the Maccabean books in that it acknowledges its source material, though Jason's original work has been lost. The author was an **unknown diaspora Jew living in Hellenistic Egypt**, writing in Greek with no evidence of an earlier Hebrew version.

Scholars suggest 2 Maccabees was composed between **150–100 BCE**, though some argue for dates as late as 124 BCE based on letters inserted at the beginning of the book (dated to 188 Seleucid Era, or ~124 BCE). The book must have been written before 40 CE and certainly before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. Unlike 1 Maccabees, this work shows less concern for political history and more interest in theological reflection, miraculous interventions, and the temple cult, reflecting its different authorial perspective and audience.

### 3 Maccabees

[3 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_Maccabees) was written by an **Alexandrian Jew** who composed the work in Greek. All scholars agree on the Greek composition, which is evident from the free, idiomatic, and rather bombastic character of the language in the Septuagint. The book is actually **misnamed**, as it has nothing to do with the Maccabees or the Maccabean Revolt. Instead, it recounts a story of persecution of Jews in Egypt under Ptolemy IV Philopator (222–205 BCE).

Though it contains some historical elements, the story is largely considered **historical fiction** rather than historical record. The precise date of authorship is unknown, but the widest plausible range is between **100 BCE and 70 CE**. [Some scholars](https://www.internationalstandardbible.com/M/maccabees-books-of-3-5.html), including Ewald, Hausrath, and Reuss, believed it was written during the reign of Emperor Caligula (37–41 CE), when Alexandrian Jews faced persecution, suggesting the book was written to comfort and encourage Jews during that crisis. Neither 3 nor 4 Maccabees has any claim to canonicity in most Christian traditions, though 3 Maccabees received favorable consideration in some Eastern Churches for a time and is included in some Orthodox canons.

### 4 Maccabees

[4 Maccabees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_Maccabees) is fundamentally different in genre from the other books—it is a **Jewish-Stoic philosophical treatise** or sermon on the question of "Whether pious reason is absolute master of the passions." The work uses the martyrdom stories of Eleazar and the seven brothers from 2 Maccabees 6:18–7 to illustrate the author's philosophical thesis.

The authorship was once incorrectly ascribed to **Josephus** by Eusebius, Jerome, and others, but this attribution is universally rejected by modern scholars. The author was probably a **diaspora Jew**, though the specific location is debated. The author was likely not from Roman Judea, as the book includes a minor geographical mistake about the layout of Jerusalem, and the tone suggests the defense of a cultural minority in a Hellenistic environment. While Alexandria was the largest center of literary Greek Jewish works in the era, scholars such as Hans Freudenthal and Eduard Norden argue that the religious outlook of the book does not easily match Alexandrian Jewish thought.

The **first century CE** is generally accepted as the date of composition, primarily because the book must have been written before the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE. The work has a clearly **Stoic stamp**, seeking to demonstrate that "pious reason exercises mastery over the emotions" (4 Maccabees 1:1). The adjective "pious" is critically important: the author is modifying the common Stoic topic ("reason can master the emotions") to suggest that it is specifically the mind trained in Jewish piety and the practices of the Jewish Law that is equipped to exercise the mastery that Greek ethicists praised. This represents a sophisticated synthesis of Greek philosophical thought with Jewish theology.

In summary, the four Books of Maccabees represent diverse authorial perspectives: 1 Maccabees as Palestinian court history, 2 Maccabees as diaspora theological history, 3 Maccabees as Alexandrian didactic fiction, and 4 Maccabees as philosophical-religious discourse. Their anonymous authors wrote from different locations (Palestine and the diaspora), in different languages (Hebrew and Greek), at different times (spanning roughly 150 BCE to 70 CE), and for different purposes, making them a rich collection of Second Temple Jewish literature rather than a unified work.
