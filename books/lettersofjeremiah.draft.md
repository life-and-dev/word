# Letters of Jeremiah

## Authorship

The Letter of Jeremiah (also known as the Epistle of Jeremiah) claims to be written by the biblical prophet Jeremiah to Jewish exiles in Babylon, mirroring the authentic letter described in Jeremiah 29. However, scholars universally acknowledge that this text is [pseudepigraphical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudepigrapha)—falsely attributed to Jeremiah to lend it authority and legitimacy. As early as the 4th century CE, [Jerome explicitly called the work "pseudepigraphical"](https://www.newadvent.org/cathen/02319c.htm) in his preface to his commentary on Jeremiah.

The true author of the Letter of Jeremiah remains unknown. E. H. Gifford observed that "the fact that Jeremiah had written one such letter to the captives seems to have suggested the idea of dignifying by his name another letter not written in reality till many ages after his death." The historical prophet Jeremiah could not have written this text, as scholarly dating places its composition approximately 300 years after Jeremiah's prophetic ministry.

### Date of Composition

The scholarly consensus dates the Letter of Jeremiah to the [Hellenistic period, specifically around 300 BCE](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Letter-of-Jeremiah). More precisely, some scholars propose a date between 317 and 306 BCE, calculated as approximately 280 years after either the first deportation (597 BCE) or the second deportation (586 BCE) to Babylon. The 3rd century BCE is generally accepted as the most likely timeframe for the original composition.

A crucial piece of physical evidence comes from the [Dead Sea Scrolls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea_Scrolls), where a Greek fragment of the Letter of Jeremiah (designated 7QLXXEpJer) was discovered at Qumran. This fragment dates to approximately the 1st century BCE, confirming that the Greek translation was completed before the end of the 2nd century BCE. The Letter of Jeremiah is one of only four deuterocanonical books found among the Dead Sea Scrolls (the others being Psalm 151, Sirach, and Tobit).

### Original Language

The question of the Letter's original language has been a subject of scholarly debate. Traditionally, many scholars believed the text was originally composed in Hebrew or Aramaic and later translated into Greek, as was common for Jewish religious literature of the period. In recent years, however, opinion has shifted somewhat, with arguments made for both positions.

Those favoring a **Hebrew/Aramaic original** point to:
- Certain linguistic and stylistic elements characteristic of Semitic languages
- The general pattern of Jewish religious texts being composed in Hebrew during this period
- The conceptual framework rooted in Hebrew prophetic tradition

Those favoring a **Greek original** note that:
- [E. H. Gifford reported that "the great majority of competent and impartial critics"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter_of_Jeremiah) considered Greek to be the original language
- The Greek text contains "dozens of linguistic features available in Greek, but not in Hebrew," suggesting it is more than a simple translation
- O. F. Fritzsche stated, "If any one of the Apocryphal books was composed in Greek, this certainly was"
- The author may have been a Hellenistic Jew living in Alexandria, where Greek was the primary language

The current scholarly consensus leans toward an original Hebrew or Aramaic composition, though the debate continues.

### Author's Identity and Context

While the specific identity of the author cannot be determined from internal evidence alone, several clues point to the author's background:

- The author was likely a **Hellenistic Jew**, familiar with both Jewish scriptural tradition and the Greco-Roman world's idolatrous practices
- The author may have lived in **Alexandria, Egypt**, a major center of Hellenistic Jewish intellectual life
- The author was deeply concerned with the threat of idolatry facing Jewish communities in the diaspora
- J. T. Marshall noted that the use of "seven generations" rather than "seventy years" for the duration of exile "points away from Jeremiah towards one who deplored the long exile" and was writing from a later historical perspective

The text itself is structured as a polemic against idol worship, developed around Jeremiah 10:11, which states that false gods shall perish from the earth. This thematic focus suggests the author wrote during a period when Jewish communities faced significant pressure to participate in pagan religious practices, which fits well with the Hellenistic period when Greek culture and religion were spreading throughout the Mediterranean world.

### Traditional vs. Scholarly Views

**Traditional View**: Some religious communities have historically accepted the text's claim to Jeremianic authorship at face value, viewing it as an authentic letter from the prophet to the Babylonian exiles.

**Scholarly Consensus**: Modern biblical scholarship, including both critical and conservative scholars, unanimously rejects Jeremianic authorship based on:
- The Greek language and Hellenistic cultural context evident in the text
- The date of composition being approximately 300 years after Jeremiah's lifetime (circa 650-585 BCE)
- The literary style and theological concerns differing significantly from the authentic Book of Jeremiah
- The absence of the Letter from the Hebrew Bible canon, which includes the authentic writings attributed to Jeremiah

The pseudepigraphical nature of the Letter of Jeremiah does not necessarily diminish its religious or literary value for communities that regard it as scripture, but it does inform our understanding of its historical origins and the intentions of its unknown author.
