# Ephesians

## Introduction

The Epistle to the Ephesians stands as one of the most majestic and theologically profound documents in the New Testament. It offers a sweeping vision of God's eternal purposes, moving from the heights of cosmic redemption to the practicalities of daily Christian living. This letter explores how the diverse threads of humanity are woven together into a single, unified body under the supremacy of Christ. Readers are invited to consider a plan conceived before the foundation of the world, designed to unite all things in heaven and on earth.

## Authorship

The authorship of Ephesians is a highly debated topic in New Testament scholarship. The epistle explicitly claims Pauline authorship in Ephesians 1:1, 3:1. Internal evidence suggests a prison setting. This leads to the traditional view of Paul writing it around AD 60-62 during his Roman imprisonment. Early church fathers like Polycarp, Ignatius, and Irenaeus universally attributed it to Paul. It also appears in the [Muratorian Canon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muratorian_fragment) around AD 170.

However, modern critical scholarship is divided. Many scholars view Ephesians as [deutero-Pauline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authorship_of_the_Pauline_epistles) (written by a later follower of Paul). Arguments against Pauline authorship include significant stylistic differences such as long, complex sentences. The letter also contains a unique vocabulary including 38 hapax legomena (words that occur only once in the New Testament). Critics also point to a literary dependence on Colossians and a lack of specificity in addressing local issues. Furthermore, the letter displays a more developed ecclesiology (the study of the nature and structure of the Christian church) and eschatology (the part of theology concerned with the final destiny of humankind).

Arguments for Pauline authorship highlight early universal acceptance and the letter's explicit claims. Supporters also note the consistency with Paul's historical context and theological alignment despite some developments. Surveys show a significant split among scholars with no clear consensus. If it is deutero-Pauline, it is dated between AD 70 and 90.

## Historical Environment

Ephesians was likely written as a circular letter (a letter intended to be passed from one group to another) to churches in [Asia Minor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_(Roman_province)). This region included the prominent city of [Ephesus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ephesus). It was composed either by Paul from Roman imprisonment around AD 60-62 or by a disciple between 70 and 90 AD. The letter addresses a predominantly Gentile audience. Ephesus was the provincial capital and a major commercial hub. It was also a center for the cult of [Artemis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temple_of_Artemis) and various magical practices. This created a complex religious environment. The letter's emphasis on Christ's supremacy over cosmic powers directly counters this occult background. It also provides instructions on spiritual warfare. The central theme of Jew-Gentile unity in Christ addresses the primary social and theological challenges facing these diverse communities in the Greco-Roman world.

## Purpose

Ephesians serves multiple interconnected purposes by blending profound theology with practical application. Unlike most of Paul's letters which address specific local controversies, Ephesians presents a comprehensive vision of God's eternal plan and the church's role within it.

### Primary Theme: Unity in Christ

The central purpose of Ephesians is to proclaim and promote the unity of Jews and Gentiles in the one body of Christ. This theme pervades the entire letter. Christ's death accomplished cosmic reconciliation. It broke down the dividing wall of hostility between these two groups. This wall likely refers metaphorically to the ceremonial laws of the Torah that separated Jews and Gentiles. It may also refer to the physical barrier in the Jerusalem Temple that prohibited Gentiles from entering the inner courts.

Christ created one new humanity in place of the two. This was a radically new identity that transcended ethnic, cultural, and religious divisions. Paul describes his special revelation regarding the mystery of Christ. He defines this mystery in Ephesians 3:6 (ESV).

> the Gentiles are fellow heirs, members of the same body, and partakers of the promise in Christ Jesus through the gospel — Ephesians 3:6 (ESV)

This mystery was God's long-hidden plan. It is now revealed that salvation extends equally to all peoples without ethnic distinction.

### Theological Purpose: Revealing God's Eternal Plan

Ephesians unfolds God's cosmic purposes across the ages. The letter reveals that God chose his people in Christ before the foundation of the world. He predestined (determined beforehand) them for adoption as sons through Jesus Christ. This was not an afterthought but God's predetermined plan to unite all things in him.

Ephesians presents the church as the vehicle through which God displays His wisdom to the rulers and authorities in the heavenly places. The church becomes a teaching instrument for angelic beings. It demonstrates God's redemptive wisdom. The letter also establishes Christ's absolute supremacy over all cosmic powers. God seated Christ at his right hand in the heavenly places. He is far above all rule, authority, power, and dominion. This cosmic Christology addresses believers living in a world perceived as populated by spiritual powers.

### Practical Purpose: Walking Worthy

Ephesians divides naturally into two halves. The first three chapters provide a theological foundation. The final three chapters focus on ethical application. The transition occurs in Ephesians 4:1 (ESV).

> I therefore, a prisoner for the Lord, urge you to walk in a manner worthy of the calling to which you have been called — Ephesians 4:1 (ESV)

The word "walk" appears repeatedly to describe Christian conduct. Believers are encouraged to walk in unity and to walk differently from pagans. They should walk in love and as children of light. They must also walk wisely and in ordered relationships.

The letter addresses specific behavioral concerns relevant for Gentile converts from pagan backgrounds. These include moral purity, truthfulness, and controlled anger. It also mentions honest labor and edifying speech.

### Ecclesiological Purpose: Elevating the Church

Ephesians presents a very high view of the church. The church is described as Christ's body. Christ is the head over all things to the church. The church is his fullness. The letter also describes the church as God's building. Believers are built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets. Christ Jesus himself is the cornerstone. The church is also pictured as Christ's bride. Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her. This marriage relationship becomes a profound picture of Christ's relationship with His church. This exalted view reminds believers of their corporate identity and significance.

### Spiritual Warfare Purpose

Ephesians emphasizes spiritual warfare as an ongoing reality for believers. The closing section describes the Christian life as warfare against rulers and authorities. It is a struggle against cosmic powers and spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places. This emphasis would have resonated with Ephesian believers because of the city's reputation for magic. The letter provides an alternative paradigm. Believers resist evil spiritual forces through truth and righteousness. They also use the gospel of peace, faith, and salvation. God's word and prayer are also essential tools.

## Cross-References

Ephesians draws upon earlier Scripture and influences later New Testament writings. This creates a rich web of connections that illuminate its theological significance.

### Old Testament Quotations

Ephesians quotes the Old Testament several times. One significant quotation appears in Ephesians 4:8 (ESV).

> When he ascended on high he led a host of captives, and he gave gifts to men — Ephesians 4:8 (ESV)

This alludes to Psalm 68:18 (ESV).

> You ascended on high, leading a host of captives in your train and receiving gifts among men, even among the rebellious, that the LORD God may dwell there — Psalm 68:18 (ESV)

The Hebrew text says "receiving gifts" while Ephesians says "gave gifts." Paul applies this victory imagery to Christ's ascension and His giving of spiritual gifts to the church.

Ephesians 5:31 quotes Genesis 2:24 (ESV) in its discussion of marriage.

> Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh — Genesis 2:24 (ESV)

This foundational text is interpreted mystically. It refers to Christ and the church. Ephesians 6:2-3 (ESV) quotes the fifth commandment.

> Honor your father and mother (this is the first commandment with a promise), that it may go well with you and that you may live long in the land — Ephesians 6:2-3 (ESV)

This encourages children to honor their parents. Ephesians 5:14 (ESV) introduces a quotation with "Therefore it says."

> Awake, O sleeper, and arise from the dead, and Christ will shine on you — Ephesians 5:14 (ESV)

No exact Old Testament passage matches this quotation. Isaiah 60:1 (ESV) comes closest but the differences are substantial. Most scholars conclude this quotes an early Christian hymn or liturgical text.

### Extensive Parallels with Colossians

Ephesians has an extraordinary literary relationship with Colossians. Approximately half of the verses in Ephesians show verbal parallels with Colossians. Scholars conclude that Paul may have written both letters simultaneously. Alternatively, the author of Ephesians used Colossians as a source. Both letters might also depend on a common earlier source.

Some passages show remarkably close wording. Ephesians 6:21-22; Colossians 4:7-8 are virtually identical. They both describe Tychicus as the letter bearer. The letters also share structural and thematic parallels. These include themes like Christ as the head of the body and the mystery revealed. They both include household codes for wives, husbands, children, slaves, and masters.

## Canonical Status

Ephesians enjoys a very strong canonical status. It was universally accepted as authoritative Scripture from the earliest periods.

### Early and Universal Acceptance

Ephesians appears in every major list of canonical New Testament books from the second century onward. It was never mentioned among disputed writings. This stands in contrast to books like Hebrews or Revelation. The earliest Christian writers after the apostolic age demonstrate knowledge and use of Ephesians. Clement of Rome and Ignatius of Antioch show familiarity with its theology. Polycarp of Smyrna quotes Ephesians 2:8-9 (ESV) as authoritative Scripture.

Even the heretic Marcion included Ephesians in his canon around AD 140. He called it the letter to the Laodiceans. The Muratorian Fragment from the late second century explicitly includes Ephesians among Paul's authentic letters. Eusebius categorized it as a universally acknowledged book in the early fourth century.

### Manuscript Evidence

Manuscript tradition provides strong support for Ephesians. Papyrus 46 from around AD 200 contains the letter in full. This demonstrates its importance in early collections of Paul's epistles. It is also found in major fourth-century manuscripts like Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. The consistent presence of Ephesians in all major early manuscripts indicates its undisputed status.

## Summary

Ephesians presents a majestic vision of God's plan to unite all things in Christ through the church. It is an apostolic letter containing inspired teaching about God's revelation.

### Chapters 1-3: Theological Foundation

Paul begins by blessing God for the spiritual blessings given in Christ. He explains that God chose believers before the foundation of the world. They have redemption through Christ's blood and the forgiveness of sins. God's plan is to unite all things in heaven and on earth in Christ. Believers are sealed with the Holy Spirit as a guarantee of their inheritance. Paul prays for the readers to have wisdom and revelation to know the hope of their calling.

The letter describes how believers were once dead in their sins. They followed the course of this world and the prince of the power of the air. But God, being rich in mercy, made them alive together with Christ. Salvation is by grace through faith and is a gift of God. It is not a result of works. However, believers are created for good works prepared by God.

Paul emphasizes the unity of Jews and Gentiles. Christ has broken down the dividing wall of hostility. He created one new humanity and reconciled both groups to God in one body through the cross. Gentiles are no longer strangers but fellow citizens and members of God's household. The church is built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets with Christ as the cornerstone.

### Chapters 4-6: Ethical Application

The second half of the letter focuses on how believers should live. Paul urges them to walk in a manner worthy of their calling. This involves humility, gentleness, and patience. There is one body, one Spirit, one Lord, one faith, and one baptism. Christ gave gifts to the church including apostles, prophets, evangelists, shepherds, and teachers. These gifts are for equipping the saints and building up the body of Christ.

Believers must put off their old self and be renewed in their minds. They should put on the new self created after the likeness of God. This involves speaking truth and managing anger. They should work honestly and use edifying speech. All bitterness and wrath should be put away. Instead, they should be kind and forgiving.

The letter provides instructions for households. Wives are encouraged to submit to their husbands as the church submits to Christ. Husbands are commanded to love their wives as Christ loved the church. Children should obey their parents, and fathers should not provoke their children. Slaves should obey their masters as they would Christ, and masters should treat slaves justly.

The final section describes the armor of God. Believers must stand against the schemes of the devil. The struggle is against spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places. The armor includes the belt of truth and the breastplate of righteousness. It also includes the shoes of the gospel of peace and the shield of faith. The helmet of salvation and the sword of the Spirit are also essential. Paul concludes with a request for prayer and a final blessing.

## Unique Teachings

Ephesians contains several theological concepts that are unique to this letter. These distinctive teachings contribute significantly to Christian theology.

### In the Heavenly Places

The phrase "in the heavenly places" appears five times in Ephesians. It does not appear anywhere else in Paul's writings. This terminology reveals a unique spatial theology. Believers are blessed with spiritual blessings in these heavenly places. Christ is seated there at God's right hand. Believers are also said to be seated there with Christ. God's wisdom is made known to powers in the heavenly places through the church. Finally, spiritual warfare occurs in this realm.

### Realized Eschatology

Ephesians says that God has already raised believers and seated them with Christ in the heavenly places. In other letters, resurrection is often presented as a future reality. Ephesians uniquely declares that believers already participate in Christ's resurrection and enthronement. This is a present and completed reality.

### The Church's Cosmic Role

Ephesians 3:10 (ESV) presents a unique purpose for the church. The church makes known the manifold wisdom of God to the rulers and authorities in the heavenly places. This teaching function of the church toward angelic beings is unique. The church is God's demonstration project to the cosmos.

### Apostles and Prophets as the Foundation

The letter states that the church is built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets. In other writings, Christ is often called the only foundation. In Ephesians, Christ is the critical cornerstone while the apostles and prophets form the base. This emphasizes the authority of apostolic teaching.

### Marriage as a Mystery

Ephesians develops an extended typology of marriage. It reflects Christ's relationship with the church. Paul interprets the Genesis account of human marriage as pointing forward to Christ. Every Christian marriage becomes a living parable of this divine relationship.

## Textual Variants

Like all ancient documents, Ephesians exists in many manuscript copies that sometimes differ in wording. Most variants are minor.

### Destination of the Letter

The most debated variant concerns the destination. The phrase "in Ephesus" in the first verse is absent from some of the earliest and most reliable manuscripts. These include Papyrus 46, Codex Sinaiticus, and Codex Vaticanus. Early church fathers like Origen also knew of manuscripts without this phrase.

Most scholars conclude that "in Ephesus" was not original to the text. The letter was likely a circular epistle intended for several churches in Asia Minor. Scribes later added "in Ephesus" to clarify the destination. This explains why the letter lacks personal greetings despite Paul's long stay in the city.

### Members of His Body

In Ephesians 5:30 (ESV), some later manuscripts add the phrase "of his flesh and of his bones." This is reflected in the King James Version. However, the earliest manuscripts do not have this addition. It was likely a scribal addition influenced by the description of Eve in Genesis 2:23 (ESV).

## Textual Errors

Scholars have identified several passages where the text appears problematic. These include misquotations or unusual references.

### Quotation of Psalm 68:18 (ESV)

In Ephesians 4:8 (ESV), the author quotes Psalm 68:18 (ESV) but changes "receiving gifts" to "gave gifts." Some scholars view this as a misquotation. Some scholars suggest the author is following an Aramaic Targum tradition. The Targum is a Jewish paraphrase that also uses "gave." Others see it as a theological interpretation. Christ receives tribute as a conqueror and then distributes it as gifts to his people.

### Unknown Source in Ephesians 5:14 (ESV)

Ephesians 5:14 (ESV) quotes a text about a sleeper awaking and Christ shining on them. This does not match any specific Old Testament passage. Isaiah 60:1 (ESV) comes closest:

> Arise, shine, for your light has come, and the glory of the LORD has risen upon you — Isaiah 60:1 (ESV)

However, the differences are substantial. Isaiah focuses on the glory of the LORD arising like the sun, while Ephesians speaks of Christ shining on one who arises from the dead. Most scholars believe Ephesians 5:14 (ESV) quotes an early Christian hymn or a baptismal liturgy.

### First Commandment with a Promise

Ephesians 6:2-3 (ESV) calls the fifth commandment the "first commandment with a promise." Some commentators note it is not the first in order. The second commandment also contains a promise. Scholars respond that "first" may mean "foremost" or "primary." It is the first commandment concerning human relationships that includes a direct promise.

## Contradictions

Some scholars point to apparent contradictions between Ephesians and other books.

### Faith and Works

Ephesians 2:8-9 (ESV) says salvation is not a result of works. James 2:24 (ESV) says a person is justified by works and not by faith alone. These statements seem to conflict. Many theologians argue they use "works" and "faith" in different ways. Ephesians focuses on the basis of salvation by grace. James focuses on the evidence of genuine faith through actions.

### The Foundation of the Church

Ephesians 2:20 (ESV) says the church is built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets. 1 Corinthians 3:11 (ESV) says Christ is the only foundation. These can be seen as complementary. Christ is the ultimate foundation, while the apostolic teaching provides the base for the church's structure.

### Present or Future Resurrection

Ephesians says believers are already raised and seated with Christ. Other letters emphasize resurrection as a future event. This represents a tension in New Testament theology between the "already" and the "not yet." Believers experience spiritual life now but await physical resurrection.

## Credibility

### Explainable Issues

Many perceived problems in Ephesians have reasonable scholarly explanations.

* **Omission of "in Ephesus"**: Supports the [circular letter theory](#destination-of-the-letter).
* **Psalm 68 quotation changes**: Reflects ancient [interpretive practices](#quotation-of-psalm-6818) or alternate traditions.
* **Faith and works tension**: Resolved by considering different [contexts and definitions](#faith-and-works).
* **Church foundation**: Viewed as [complementary architectural metaphors](#the-foundation-of-the-church).
* **Resurrection timing**: Reflects the standard New Testament ["already/not yet"](#present-or-future-resurrection) tension.

### Concerns

Some issues remain more challenging for certain perspectives.

* **Authorship**: The significant stylistic and theological differences from Paul's undisputed letters lead many scholars to [question his direct authorship](#authorship).
* **Ecclesiological Shift**: The move toward a universal church and a more settled social order may suggest a [later date](#historical-environment) than Paul's lifetime.
* **Liturgical Sources**: The use of [unknown sources](#unknown-source-in-ephesians-514) indicates influence from established traditions not found in earlier Pauline works.

## Conclusion

The Epistle to the Ephesians provides a [comprehensive vision](#purpose) of the Christian faith. It emphasizes the [unity of all believers](#primary-theme-unity-in-christ) in Christ and the [cosmic significance](#theological-purpose-revealing-gods-eternal-plan) of the church. The letter moves from profound theological truths about [election and grace](#chapters-1-3-theological-foundation) to practical [instructions for daily living](#chapters-4-6-ethical-application) and [spiritual warfare](#spiritual-warfare-purpose). While questions about [authorship](#authorship) and specific [textual details](#textual-variants) continue to be studied, the letter's impact on Christian doctrine and practice remains immense. It continues to inspire believers with its message of reconciliation and the immeasurable love of Christ.
