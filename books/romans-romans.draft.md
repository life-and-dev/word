# Epistle to the Romans

Why would a man who had never visited a city write its believers the most complex and systematic explanation of his faith? The Apostle Paul's letter to the Romans stands as a theological masterpiece. It addresses the fundamental questions of human guilt, divine justice, and the transformative power of the gospel.

## Authorship and Context

The Epistle to the Romans is universally attributed to the Apostle Paul. Modern biblical scholarship maintains an overwhelming consensus on this. It is part of the core group of undisputed Pauline epistles. Paul explicitly identifies himself in the opening verse as a servant of Christ Jesus called to be an apostle. He dictated the letter to his secretary Tertius, who also sends greetings in the final chapter.

Paul wrote this letter from Corinth around AD 56-57 during his third missionary journey. He was staying with Gaius and preparing to deliver a collection for the poor in Jerusalem. Paul hoped to visit Rome on his way to Spain. The Roman church consisted of both Jewish and Gentile believers. They faced tensions regarding the Mosaic Law and their relationship within the body of Christ.

## Purpose of the Letter

Paul had several goals for this letter. His primary theological purpose was to present a systematic exposition of the gospel. He wanted to demonstrate that righteousness comes through faith in Christ alone. This message is available to both Jews and Gentiles on equal terms.

Pastoral concerns also motivated Paul. He addressed conflicts between Jewish and Gentile believers about dietary restrictions and special days. He urged mutual acceptance and unity. Furthermore, Paul had a missionary purpose. He wanted to introduce himself to the Roman church and secure their support for his future mission to Spain. He also needed to clarify his teachings, which some had misrepresented as encouraging lawlessness.

## The Righteousness of God

Paul presents the main theme: the righteousness of God revealed through faith. He argues that God's wrath is revealed against all ungodliness. Although God's existence is evident in creation, people have suppressed this truth and turned to idolatry. As a result, humanity has fallen into various vices.

### Universal Human Sinfulness

Paul addresses those who judge others. He warns that in judging others, they condemn themselves. God's judgment is based on truth and deeds. Merely possessing the Law does not make one righteous. Keeping the Law is what matters. Paul introduces the concept of the law written on the hearts of Gentiles. He uses Old Testament quotations to demonstrate that no one is righteous. All have fallen short of God's [glory](../terms/glory.md).

### Justification by Faith

Righteousness comes through faith in Jesus Christ, not by works of the law. Paul uses Abraham as an example. Abraham was justified by faith before he was circumcised. This makes him the father of all who believe. Righteousness is credited to those who believe in God who raised Jesus from the dead.

Justification brings peace with God and access to grace. Paul compares Adam and Christ as representatives of humanity. Through Adam, sin and death entered the world. Through Christ, grace and righteousness are available to all. Where sin increased, grace abounded all the more.

## Transformation in Christ

Paul denies that Christians should continue in sin so that grace may abound. Believers have died to sin and are raised to new life in Christ. Baptism illustrates this spiritual reality. Christians should consider themselves dead to sin but alive to God.

### The Internal Struggle

The believer has died to the law through Christ. Paul describes an internal struggle with sin. There is a conflict between the desire to do good and the inability to carry it out due to indwelling sin. This leads to a cry for deliverance.

### Life in the Spirit

There is no condemnation for those in Christ Jesus. The Spirit of life sets believers free from the law of sin and death. Those led by the Spirit are children of God. The whole creation awaits the revelation of God's children. The Spirit intercedes for believers in their weakness. Paul concludes with the assurance that nothing can separate us from the love of God.

## God's Plan for Israel

Paul addresses the relationship of the Israelites to God's promises. Not all physical descendants of Israel are part of the promise. Believers are included as children of Abraham. God has the sovereign right to choose whom He wills.

### Salvation for All

Israel's zeal for God is not based on knowledge. They try to establish their own righteousness instead of submitting to God's righteousness through Christ. Saving faith involves confessing Jesus as Lord and believing in His resurrection. There is no distinction between Jew and Greek. Everyone who calls on the name of the LORD will be saved.

### The Remnant and Future Hope

God has not completely rejected His people. A remnant chosen by grace remains. Israel's stumbling brought salvation to the Gentiles. This is meant to make Israel envious. Paul warns Gentiles against arrogance. A partial hardening has come upon Israel until the full number of Gentiles comes in. Then all Israel will be saved. Paul concludes with praise for God's unsearchable wisdom.

## Practical Christian Living

Paul urges believers to offer themselves as living sacrifices. They should be transformed by the renewing of their minds. He discusses spiritual gifts and exhorts believers to love and humility.

### Authority and Love

Believers must submit to governing authorities as God's servants. Love is the fulfillment of the law. Christians should live uprightly in light of Christ's return. Paul addresses conflicts over disputable matters like dietary rules. Stronger believers should not look down on the weak. Each person will give an account to God. Unity is essential. Believers should bear with the failings of the weak and please their neighbors for their good.

## Textual Variations

Romans contains several significant textual variants that scholars have debated.

### Romans 8:1

Some manuscripts end the verse after "those who are in Christ Jesus". Others add "who do not walk according to the flesh, but according to the Spirit". The shorter reading has strong support from the earliest manuscripts like Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. Some argue the longer version was added to harmonize with verse 4.

The theological implication involves the nature of being "in" Christ. Some suggest this refers to a relational position. Others debate if it implies an omnipresent Spirit. This topic is discussed in sections on [human limitations](../interpretations/jesus/interpretations-of-jesus.draft.md) and the [nature of Jesus](../interpretations/jesus/interpretations-of-jesus.draft.md).

### Romans 16:7

The name "Junia" or "Junias" varies. Junia is feminine, while Junias is masculine. Most early church fathers understood the name as feminine. It affects interpretations regarding women in church leadership.

### Romans 16:24

> The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with you all. Amen. — Romans 16:24

This verse is absent from major early Greek manuscripts. It appears to be a later scribal addition. It repeats the blessing found in verse 20. Most modern translations omit it or include it in brackets.

### The Final Doxology

The placement of the doxology in Romans 16:25-27 varies. It appears at the end of chapter 14, 15, or 16 in different manuscripts. Some manuscripts omit it entirely. Most scholars believe it originally ended chapter 16.

The Epistle to the Romans provides a comprehensive view of [Christian theology](#the-righteousness-of-god). It establishes the [universal need for salvation](#universal-human-sinfulness) and the [gift of justification](#justification-by-faith). Paul explains the process of [transformation through the Spirit](#transformation-in-christ) and explores [God's faithfulness to Israel](#gods-plan-for-israel). The letter concludes with practical instructions for [righteous living](#practical-christian-living). Understanding these [textual variations](#textual-variations) helps clarify the transmission of this vital message.
