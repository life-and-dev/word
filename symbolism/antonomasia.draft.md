# Antonomasia

The Bible often uses symbolic language to convey deep spiritual truths. One such rhetorical device is the use of names to attribute specific qualities to a person, even when that name is not their own. This practice helps readers understand the nature or destiny of a biblical figure by comparing them to a well-known person or place from history.

## Definition

Antonomasia is a figure of speech where a proper name is used to represent a certain quality or set of characteristics. This can happen when one person is called by the name of another to ascribe the traits of the second person to the first.

> Antonomasia is the figure of speech in which one person is called by the name of another person in order to ascribe the characteristics of the second person to the one we are addressing. For example, we might say to a child who is jumping on the couch, “Stop that, Tarzan!” We know the child’s name is not “Tarzan,” but by calling him “Tarzan,” we ascribe the jungle behavior of Tarzan to the child. — REV commentary of Spirit & Truth Fellowship International

## Biblical Examples

Scripture contains several instances of this figure of speech. These examples often serve as warnings or to highlight the moral state of a people.

*   **Zimri:** Jezebel called Jehu by the name "Zimri" in 2 Kings 9:31 (ESV). This was a threat implying his reign would be short, as Zimri was a king who only ruled for seven days before dying. Although Jehu did kill Jezebel, he went on to reign for 28 years (2 Kings 10:36 (ESV)).
*   **Sodom and Gomorrah:** In Isaiah 1:10 (ESV), the LORD addresses the rulers and people of Judah as "Sodom" and "Gomorrah." This highlights their extreme wickedness and moral decay by comparing them to the cities destroyed for their sin.
*   **Elijah:** John the Baptist is often associated with the name "Elijah." The angel Gabriel told Zechariah that his son John would go before the Lord "in the spirit and power of Elijah" in Luke 1:17 (ESV). Jesus later confirmed this identification in Matthew 11:14, 17:10-13 (ESV). John's life and ministry so closely paralleled that of Elijah that he was considered the "Elijah" who was to come.

## Messiah as David

The Bible frequently refers to the Messiah using the name "David." This use of antonomasia highlights that the Messiah would be a descendant of David and that his reign would mirror the glory of David's kingdom.

References to the Messiah as "David" can be found in several prophetic books:
*   Jeremiah 30:9 (ESV)
*   Ezekiel 34:23-24, 37:24-25 (ESV)
*   Hosea 3:5 (ESV)

These passages emphasize the restoration of Israel under a king who embodies the ideal characteristics of King David.

## Messiah as Immanuel

Another significant example occurs in the New Testament regarding the name "Immanuel." In Matthew 1:18-25 (ESV), the author quotes the prophecy from Isaiah 7:14 (ESV).

The prophecy states that a virgin will give birth to a son and "they will call his name Immanuel." While the child was named Jesus, the title "Immanuel" (meaning "God with us") describes his role and nature. By applying this name to Jesus, the text ascribes the characteristic of God's presence among his people to the Messiah.

## Conclusion

The use of antonomasia in the Bible provides a powerful way to describe character and mission. By calling individuals or nations by historical names like [Zimri](#biblical-examples), [Sodom](#biblical-examples), [Elijah](#biblical-examples), [David](#messiah-as-david), or [Immanuel](#messiah-as-immanuel), the biblical authors communicate profound messages about identity, morality, and prophecy.
