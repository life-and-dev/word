# Antonomasia

The Bible often employs rich literary devices to convey deep theological truths (the study of the nature of God and religious belief) and character assessments. One such figure of speech is antonomasia, which involves substituting a proper name for another to highlight specific attributes. Understanding this device helps clarify why certain figures are referred to by names that are not literally their own.

## Defining Antonomasia

> Antonomasia is the figure of speech in which one person is called by the name of another person in order to ascribe the characteristics of the second person to the one we are addressing. For example, we might say to a child who is jumping on the couch, “Stop that, Tarzan!” We know the child’s name is not “Tarzan,” but by calling him “Tarzan,” we ascribe the jungle behavior of Tarzan to the child. ## Biblical Examples

> * Jezebel called Jehu, “Zimri” (2 Kings 9:31 (ESV)) as a threat that his reign as king would be short if he killed her. He did eventually kill her and reigned for 28 years (2 Kings 10:36 (ESV)).

## Messianic Titles

> * The Bible calls the Messiah, Jesus Christ, by the name of “David” in [Jeremiah](../books/jeremiah.md) 30:9 (ESV); [Ezekiel](../books/ezekiel.md) 34:23-24, 37:24-25 (ESV); and [Hosea](../books/hosea.md) 3:5 (ESV). The Messiah is called “David” to highlight that the Messiah would be a lineal descendant of David. This was fulfilled through Mary. The title also reflects that the reign of David, especially early on, was a glorious time in Israel, and the Messiah’s reign on earth will likewise be a glorious time.
> * Judah is called “Sodom” and also “Gomorrah” because it was so wicked ([Isaiah](../books/isaiah.md) 1:10 (ESV)).

## John the Baptist as Elijah

> * John the Baptist is called “Elijah” because Elijah’s life and ministry paralleled John’s in many ways.
>
> That John would be like Elijah was made clear to Zechariah by the angel Gabriel. When Zechariah was ministering in the Temple, Gabriel appeared to him and said that Elizabeth would have a son they were to name “John,” and he would go before God “in the spirit and power of Elijah” ([Luke](../books/luke.md) 1:17 (ESV)). For those who remembered the angel’s words years later when John started his ministry, it was clear that John was indeed the “Elijah” who was to come.
>
> — [REV commentary of Spirit & Truth Fellowship International](https://stfi.org/articles/the-figure-of-speech-antonomasia-as-used-in-the-bible/)

## The Name Immanuel

Another prominent example is found in the [Gospel of Matthew](../books/matthew.md). The author cites a prophecy from the book of [Isaiah](../books/isaiah.md) regarding a child to be named Immanuel.

> "Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call his name Immanuel" (which means, God with us). — [Matthew](../books/matthew.md) 1:23 (ESV)

Matthew applied this to Jesus even though His literal name was never Immanuel. In this context, antonomasia is used to ascribe the characteristic of "God with us" to Jesus. The name reflects His identity and role rather than His legal name. This fulfillment demonstrates that Jesus is the ultimate expression of God's presence among His people, as originally prefigured in the sign given to King Ahaz in [Isaiah](../books/isaiah.md) 7:14.

## Conclusion

[Antonomasia](#defining-antonomasia) allows speakers to highlight specific traits by substituting one name for another. Scripture uses this device to provide [moral assessments](#biblical-examples) of individuals and nations based on their actions. [Messianic prophecies](#messianic-titles) employ it to connect Jesus with David's royal heritage and future glory. The identification of [John the Baptist as Elijah](#john-the-baptist-as-elijah) underscores his prophetic mission and spiritual power. Finally, the name [Immanuel](#the-name-immanuel) signifies the divine identity of Jesus as God present with humanity.
