# The Cross

The cross is one of the most recognizable symbols in the world today. While it is synonymous with Christianity, its origins and the exact nature of the instrument used for Jesus' execution remain subjects of historical and linguistic debate. By examining ancient texts, archaeological findings, and the development of early Christian identity, we can gain a clearer understanding of how this symbol came to represent the faith.

![Christian Cross](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Christian_cross.svg)

## Prophetic Silence

No prophecy refers to any cross-like symbol. Instead, the prophecies foretold about the suffering of Christ but never mentioned in what form he would be nailed to.

## Historical Evidence

Archaeological evidence includes nails driven into skeletons by the Romans. This proves that the Romans nailed people onto wooden structures or trees. However, the exact appearance of the structure on which Jesus was nailed remains unknown.

Both John 19:17-18 and the Jewish historian Josephus, who lived in the first century, used the Greek word [stauros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stauros). This term means "a stake or an implement of capital punishment". In older Greek texts, "stauros" means "pole".

Critiques may argue that the early church's use of the "staurogram" (a combination of the Greek letters Tau and Rho) in 2nd-century manuscripts suggests an early focus on the cross. However, these symbols were primarily used as abbreviations for the Greek word *stauros* rather than as objects of veneration or primary identifiers.

## Linguistic Analysis

Many translators commonly translated "stauros" as "cross" in the Bible. Consequently, many were led to believe that "stauros" means "cross". This is circular reasoning and does not prove anything.

The Septuagint is the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible. It was written by 72 Jewish scholars at the request of Ptolemy II Philadelphus, king of Egypt. This translation does not contain any references to "cross" symbols or that the Christ will be "crucified". It only states that Christ will be:

> But he was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; upon him was the chastisement that brought us peace, and with his wounds we are healed. — Isaiah 53:5 (ESV)

Even Peter said:

> He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. By his wounds you have been healed. — 1 Peter 2:24 (ESV)

In this passage, Peter uses the Greek word ["xylon"](https://biblehub.com/greek/3586.htm). This term literally means "wood" or "timber". It can be translated as "tree" (as in the ESV, KJV, and NASB) or more generally as a "wooden" structure. While many modern translations use the word "cross" (NIV, NLT), the original Greek is less specific about the shape of the object.

## Christian Identity

People groups tend to seek symbols or logos to identify themselves. The cross (🕇) is a symbol that identifies Christians. However, besides the modified translations, there is no evidence that Jesus or the apostles used the cross as a symbol for their identity.

Jesus said:

> A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another. — John 13:34-35 (ESV)

God's children are not recognized by crosses, special culture, membership, dress code, hairstyle, or facial expressions. Instead, they are recognized by their unselfish love for one another.

## Symbolism of the Tav

Some Christians point out that the ancient Hebrew letter "Tav" used to look like two crossed sticks. This form resembles a Christian cross.

![Ancient Hebrew Tav](https://www.ancient-hebrew.org/hebrew/heb-anc-lg-tav.jpg)

Today, the modern Hebrew letter "Tav" no longer looks like a cross.

![Modern Hebrew Tav](https://www.ancient-hebrew.org/hebrew/heb-modern-lg-tav.jpg)

The ancient Hebrews likely did not have the Roman crucifixion in mind when they developed their alphabet. While some see a prophetic connection in the shape of the letter, the historical evolution of the alphabet suggests that the change from a cross-like shape to the modern form was a natural linguistic progression rather than a rejection of a specific symbol.

## Biblical Remembrance

Jesus never wore or handed out any crosses.

Instead, Paul wrote that communion should be our remembrance of Christ's bread and new covenant:

> For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you, that the Lord Jesus on the night when he was betrayed took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it, and said, “This is my body which is for you. Do this in remembrance of me.” In the same way also he took the cup, after supper, saying, “This cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this, as often as you drink it, in remembrance of me.” For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes. — 1 Corinthians 11:23-26 (ESV)

* The bread symbolizes righteousness and fellowship with the Father, the Son, and fellow believers.
* The cup symbolizes the new covenant with God that seals our [reconciliation](../terms/crucifixion.draft.md) with Him by the forgiveness of sin.

Jesus focused on his message of love and the reconciliation of humanity with God. Remembering his sacrifice through the communion he established emphasizes his victory and love rather than the shame of the execution method.

## Conclusion

While the cross has become a universal symbol of the Christian faith, [historical evidence](#historical-evidence) and [linguistic analysis](#linguistic-analysis) show that the exact nature of the instrument used for Jesus' execution is not definitively known. Furthermore, Jesus taught that his disciples would be identified by [their love for one another](#christian-identity) rather than by any physical symbol. Ultimately, [biblical remembrance](#biblical-remembrance) focuses on the spiritual significance of Christ's sacrifice and the resulting reconciliation with God.
