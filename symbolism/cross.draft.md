# The Cross

Investigation into the historical and biblical evidence reveals a complex picture regarding the instrument used in the crucifixion of Jesus. While the cross is the universal symbol of Christianity today, ancient texts and archaeological findings provide various perspectives on its original form and the early church's use of symbols.

![Christian Cross](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Christian_cross.svg)

## The cross in prophecies

No biblical prophecy refers to a specific cross-like symbol. Instead, the prophetic texts foretold the suffering of Christ (the Messiah or "Anointed One") without specifying the exact shape of the wooden structure to which he would be nailed.

## Archaeological evidence

Archaeological evidence includes instances of nails driven into human remains from the Roman era. These findings prove that Roman authorities used nails to execute individuals on wooden structures or trees. However, these remains do not definitively indicate the precise shape of the structure used for the execution of Jesus.

Both John 19:17-18 (ESV) and the first-century Jewish historian Josephus used the Greek word stauros (an upright stake or pole). In earlier Greek literature, stauros consistently referred to an upright pale or pole. By the first century, the term could refer to various execution apparatuses used by the Romans.

Critics may argue that the variety of Roman execution methods supports the possibility of a cross shape. Some archaeological finds, such as the Alexamenos graffito (an early piece of graffiti), depict a cross. This suggests that the cross shape was recognized as an execution tool in the early centuries.

## Bible translations

Many Bible translators render the word stauros as "cross" based on long-standing tradition. Some scholars argue that using this translation to prove the shape of the instrument is a form of circular reasoning.

The Septuagint (the ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures) was produced by Jewish scholars for King Ptolemy II Philadelphus. This version does not contain references to cross symbols or state that the Messiah would be "crucified" in the modern sense. Instead, it describes the suffering of the servant in general terms.

> but he was pierced for our transgressions; he was crushed for our iniquities; — Isaiah 53:5 (ESV)

The Apostle Peter also used the term xylon (wood, timber, or a tree) when referring to the instrument of execution.

> He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. — 1 Peter 2:24 (ESV)

The Greek word xylon (a general term for wood or a wooden object) is translated as "tree" in several versions, including the ESV and King James Version. Other translations use "cross" to reflect traditional interpretations of the event.

## Identity

Social groups often adopt symbols or logos to represent their identity. The cross symbol (🕇) identifies Christians (followers of Jesus Christ) globally. However, evidence suggests that neither Jesus nor his immediate apostles used the cross as a primary symbol of their identity.

The Gospel of John records a different standard for identification.

> A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another. — John 13:34-35 (ESV)

This suggests that early believers were meant to be recognized by their love and conduct rather than by external symbols, specific cultures, or dress codes.

## Ancient Tav

Some argue that the ancient Hebrew letter Tav (the final letter of the Hebrew alphabet) provides a link to the cross symbol. In the Paleo-Hebrew script (the earliest form of the Hebrew alphabet), the letter Tav was written as two crossed sticks.

![Ancient Hebrew Tav](https://www.ancient-hebrew.org/hebrew/heb-anc-lg-tav.jpg)

The modern Hebrew letter Tav has evolved and no longer resembles a cross.

![Modern Hebrew Tav](https://www.ancient-hebrew.org/hebrew/heb-modern-lg-tav.jpg)

Historical analysis suggests that ancient Hebrews did not have Roman crucifixion methods in mind when they developed their alphabet. The resemblance between the ancient letter and the Roman execution device is viewed by many as coincidental.

## Remembrance

Records do not show Jesus wearing or distributing cross-like symbols. Instead, the Apostle Paul wrote that communion (a shared meal of bread and wine) should serve as the primary remembrance of the sacrifice of Christ.

> For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you, that the Lord Jesus on the night when he was betrayed took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it, and said, “This is my body, which is for you. Do this in remembrance of me.” In the same way also he took the cup, after supper, saying, “This cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this, as often as you drink it, in remembrance of me.” For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes. — 1 Corinthians 11:23-26 (ESV)

In this practice, the bread symbolizes righteousness (moral uprightness) and fellowship (communal sharing) with God and other believers. The cup symbolizes the new covenant (a divine agreement) that establishes reconciliation (the restoration of a relationship) between God and humanity.

Focusing on these practices emphasizes the love and accomplishment of Christ rather than the instrument of his death. Many theological perspectives suggest that the significance lies in the reconciliation achieved rather than the specific form of the wooden structure used.

## Conclusion

The cross serves as a central symbol in modern Christianity, yet historical and biblical evidence provides a more nuanced picture of its origins. [Prophetic texts](cross.draft.md#the-cross-in-prophecies) focus on the suffering of the Messiah rather than the specific form of the execution device. [Archaeological finds](cross.draft.md#archaeological-evidence) confirm the Roman use of wooden structures for execution but do not prove a single universal shape. [Scriptural terminology](cross.draft.md#bible-translations) such as stauros and xylon can refer to poles, trees, or various wooden apparatuses. Early believers were primarily [identified by their love](cross.draft.md#identity) and [remembered the sacrifice of Christ](cross.draft.md#remembrance) through practices like communion rather than external symbols. While the [ancient Hebrew Tav](cross.draft.md#ancient-tav) bore a resemblance to the cross, its development predates Roman execution methods. Ultimately, these perspectives highlight that the spiritual significance of the event remains independent of the precise shape of the instrument.
