# The Cross

![Christian Cross](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/87/Christian_cross.svg)

The cross is universally recognized as the primary symbol of Christianity. While it adorns churches and jewelry worldwide, its historical and biblical origins are often more complex than they appear. This article explores whether the cross was an established symbol in the early church, what archaeological evidence reveals about Roman crucifixion, and how linguistic nuances in the original Greek texts shape our understanding of the instrument used for the execution of Jesus.

## Prophecies

No biblical prophecies refer to any cross-like symbol. Instead, they foretold [the suffering of Christ](https://eternal.family.net.za/god/covenants/christ). These texts never specified the exact shape of the instrument he would be nailed to.

## Archaeological evidence

Archaeological evidence includes skeletal remains with nails driven into them by the Romans. This proves that Romans nailed victims to wooden structures or trees. While many assume a traditional cross shape, the exact design of the structure used for Jesus remains unknown. Historical records indicate that Roman soldiers often chose the most convenient or terrifying method available, which could include various configurations.

Both John 19:17-18 and the Jewish historian Josephus used the Greek word [stauros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stauros). This term means a stake or an implement of capital punishment. In older Greek texts, "stauros" referred to a simple pole.

## Bible translations

Many translators commonly render "stauros" as "cross" in modern Bibles. This practice led many to believe that the word strictly means "cross". This conclusion often relies on circular reasoning rather than linguistic evidence.

The Septuagint is the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible. It was written by seventy-two Jewish scholars for King Ptolemy II Philadelphus. This translation contains no references to cross symbols or the act of being "crucified". It states that Christ would be:

> pierced for our transgressions; ... crushed for our iniquities; — Isaiah 53:5 (ESV)

Peter also described the execution:

> He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. — 1 Peter 2:24 (ESV)

The Greek word used here is [xylon](https://biblehub.com/greek/3586.htm), which means wood or timber. It can be translated as "tree". Many versions, such as the ESV, KJV, and NKJV, use this translation. Others refer to a "wooden" structure or a "cross".

## Christian Identity

People groups often seek symbols to identify themselves. The cross (🕇) is the primary symbol identifying Christians. However, no evidence suggests that Jesus or the apostles used the cross as an identity marker.

Jesus defined the true mark of his followers:

> A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another. — John 13:34-35 (ESV)

God's children are not recognized by symbols, special culture, or membership. Instead, they are recognized by their unselfish love for one another.

## Ancient Hebrew Letter Tav

Some suggest that the ancient Hebrew letter "Tav" resembled two crossed sticks. While the Paleo-Hebrew form did look like a cross, modern Hebrew has changed.

![Ancient Hebrew Tav](https://www.ancient-hebrew.org/hebrew/heb-anc-lg-tav.jpg)

The modern letter "Tav" no longer carries this appearance.

![Modern Hebrew Tav](https://www.ancient-hebrew.org/hebrew/heb-modern-lg-tav.jpg)

Proponents of the cross symbol sometimes view the ancient Tav as a prophetic sign. However, linguistic history suggests the shape originated as a simple mark or tally. The ancient Hebrews developed these letters long before the Romans introduced crucifixion as a form of capital punishment.

## Remembrance

Jesus did not wear or distribute crosses. Instead, the apostle Paul wrote that communion serves as the remembrance of Christ.

> For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you, that the Lord Jesus on the night when he was betrayed took bread, and when he had given thanks, he broke it, and said, “This is my body, which is for you. Do this in remembrance of me.” In the same way also he took the cup, after supper, saying, “This cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this, as often as you drink it, in remembrance of me.” For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes. Whoever, therefore, eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord in an unworthy manner will be guilty concerning the body and blood of the Lord. — 1 Corinthians 11:23-27 (ESV)

The bread symbolizes righteousness and fellowship with the Father, the Son, and fellow believers. The cup symbolizes the new covenant with God. This covenant seals reconciliation with Him through the forgiveness of sin.

Remembering Jesus through his love and his accomplishment in reconciling humanity to God provides a more profound perspective than focusing on the shame of crucifixion.

## Conclusion

The cross remains a powerful symbol, yet its historical and biblical origins suggest a more nuanced reality. Biblical [prophecies](#prophecies) focus on the suffering of Christ rather than the instrument of his execution. [Archaeological evidence](#archaeological-evidence) confirms the Roman use of nails but leaves the exact shape of the crucifixion structure uncertain. Linguistic study of [Bible translations](#bible-translations) reveals that terms like *stauros* and *xylon* can refer to poles or trees. Furthermore, [Christian identity](#christian-identity) was originally marked by love rather than symbols. Finally, the [remembrance](#remembrance) of Jesus was established through communion rather than the veneration of the cross.
