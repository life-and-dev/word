# Glory

Investigation into what it truly means to "give glory" to someone reveals important nuances. While often used interchangeably with worship in modern English, the biblical concept of glory carries a specific weight and meaning that applies to both God and humanity. Understanding this distinction helps clarify how we relate to the Divine and how we perceive human dignity.

## Linguistic Origins

The concept of glory in the Bible is rooted in specific Hebrew and Greek terms that emphasize weight, significance, and manifest beauty.

### Hebrew: Kabowd

The Hebrew word often translated as glory is *kabowd*.

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/hebrew/3519.htm) (a cross-reference tool for Bible words) |
|-------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Original Word     | כָּבוֹד |
| Part of Speech    | Noun Masculine                                               |
| Transliteration   | kabowd (writing a word using a different alphabet)            |
| Phonetic Spelling | kaw-bode'                                                    |
| Definition        | glorious                                                     |

[Brown-Driver-Briggs](https://biblehub.com/hebrew/3519.htm) (a standard Hebrew lexicon or dictionary) defines it as abundance, honour, and glory. It literally refers to "heaviness" or "weight," suggesting that a person of glory is someone of great significance or importance.

### Greek: Doxa (a Greek word for glory)

In the New Testament, the Greek word *doxa* is used.

## Glory vs. Worship

In modern English, dictionaries sometimes include "worship" in the definition of glory. For instance, the English Oxford Dictionary provides these definitions:

1. High renown or honour won by notable achievements.
2. Magnificence or great beauty.
3. Praise, worship, and thanksgiving offered to a deity.
4. A luminous ring or halo, especially as depicted around the head of Christ or a saint.

This inclusion of "worship" causes confusion because the Bible makes a clear distinction between the two. While only God may be worshipped and served, glory is a status or attribute that can be shared. Worship is the response of prostration or bowing down before something of ultimate worth. Glory, on the other hand, refers to the inherent value, splendor, or "weight" of a person or being.

## Divine and Human Glory

While God possesses the ultimate and uncreated glory, the Bible explicitly states that humans can also possess glory. This does not detract from God's unique status but rather reflects His creative power and grace.

For example, the scriptures mention human glory in several contexts:

* **Psalm 8:5-6 (ESV)** (English Standard Version): "Yet you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor. You have given him dominion over the works of your hands; you have put all things under his feet."
* **John 17:22 (ESV)**: "The glory that you have given me I have given to them, that they may be one even as we are one."
* **1 Corinthians 11:7 (ESV)**: "For a man ought not to cover his head, since he is the image and glory of God, but woman is the glory of man."

Even in the final state of the world, human glory remains significant.

> And the city has no need of sun or moon to shine on it, for the glory of God gives it light, and its lamp is the Lamb. By its light will the nations walk, and the kings of the earth will bring their glory into it, and its gates will never be shut by day — and there will be no night there. — Revelation 21:23-25 (ESV)

Critiques might argue that attributing glory to humans risks idolatry or diminishes God's sovereignty. However, the biblical perspective is that human glory is a derived glory. Just as the moon reflects the light of the sun, human glory reflects the image of God. When we recognize the glory in a human being, we are ultimately acknowledging the "Eternal Weight of Glory" that God has bestowed upon His creation.

## Conclusion

The biblical concept of [glory](#glory) is far richer than simple praise. It represents the [weight and significance](#linguistic-origins) of a being's character and presence. By distinguishing [glory from worship](#glory-vs-worship), we can rightly offer our total devotion to God while still acknowledging the [glory and honor](#divine-and-human-glory) He has bestowed upon humanity.
