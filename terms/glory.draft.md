# Glory

The concept of glory appears frequently in religious texts and everyday language. It is often associated with brilliance, honor, or fame. In the Bible, however, glory carries a deeper weight and significance that distinguishes between the essence of God and the reflected dignity of humanity. Exploring the original languages and biblical contexts reveals how this term bridges the gap between the divine and the created.

## Linguistic Origins

The biblical understanding of glory begins with the original Hebrew and Greek terms used by the authors of Scripture. These words provide a foundation for how glory is defined in a spiritual context.

### Hebrew Translation

| Property | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/hebrew/3519.htm) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Original Word | כָּבוֹד |
| Part of Speech | Noun Masculine |
| Transliteration | kabowd |
| Phonetic Spelling | kaw-bode' |
| Definition | glory, honor, glorious |

The [Brown-Driver-Briggs](https://biblehub.com/hebrew/3519.htm) lexicon further defines *kabowd* (signifying importance and substance) as abundance, honor, and glory. The root of this word implies weightiness or heaviness. This suggests that glory is not just a light or appearance. It is a substantial and significant quality.

### Greek Translation

| Property | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/1391.htm) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Original Word | δόξα, ης, ἡ |
| Part of Speech | Noun, Feminine |
| Transliteration | doxa |
| Phonetic Spelling | dox'-ah |
| Definition | opinion, [praise](praise.draft.md), honor, glory |
| Usage | honor, renown; glory, an especially divine quality, the unspoken manifestation of God, splendor |

In the New Testament, *doxa* (referring to the radiance or honor of a person or being) refers to a state of high honor. It is often used to describe the divine quality and majesty of God. It can also refer to the radiance or splendor that emanates from His presence.

## Defining Glory

Modern dictionaries often provide multiple definitions for glory. The Oxford English Dictionary includes various nuances that can affect how the term is understood in a religious context.

1. High renown or honor won by notable achievements.
2. Magnificence or great beauty.
3. Praise, [worship](worship.draft.md), and thanksgiving offered to a deity.
4. A luminous ring or halo, especially as depicted around the head of Christ or a saint.

Some confusion arises when "worship" is included in the definition of glory. Biblical theology emphasizes that [only God is worthy of worship and service](worship.draft.md). While glory can be a reason for worship, the two terms are not identical. Glory refers to the intrinsic worth or outward manifestation of greatness. Worship is the response of a created being to that greatness.

## Human Glory in Scripture

In the context of the Bible, glory is not reserved exclusively for God. Humans can also possess a form of glory, though it is different in nature and source.

- **Psalm 8:5-6 (ESV):** "Yet you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor. You have given him dominion over the works of your hands; you have put all things under his feet."
- **John 17:22 (ESV):** "The glory that you have given me I have given to them, that they may be one even as we are one."
- **1 Corinthians 11:7 (ESV):** "For a man ought not to cover his head, since he is the image and glory of God, but woman is the glory of man."

These passages indicate that human glory is a bestowed dignity. It is a reflection of being created in the image of God or being united with Christ.

## Glory in the New Jerusalem

The ultimate manifestation of glory is described in the vision of the future city of God. This glory provides the necessary light and environment for the redeemed.

> And the city has no need of sun or moon to shine on it, for the glory of God gives it light, and its lamp is the Lamb. By its light will the nations walk, and the kings of the earth will bring their glory into it, and its gates will never be shut by day—and there will be no night there. — Revelation 21:23-25 (ESV)

In this context, the glory of God is a physical and spiritual illumination. It replaces the need for natural light sources. The glory that the kings of the earth bring into the city likely refers to the best of human achievements and honors. These are now surrendered and dedicated to the service of God.

## Perspective on Divine Glory

Some critics argue that a God who seeks His own glory appears self-centered or ego-centric. However, biblical theology presents a different perspective. If God is the source of all truth, beauty, and goodness, then His glory is the most magnificent thing in existence. For God to point away from Himself would be to point His creatures toward something inferior.

Glorifying God is not for His benefit, as He is already complete. Instead, it is for the benefit of humanity. When people recognize and enjoy the glory of God, they are aligning themselves with the ultimate reality and finding their greatest joy.

## Conclusion

The biblical concept of glory encompasses both the infinite majesty of God and the reflected honor bestowed upon humanity. It is rooted in the [importance and substance](#linguistic-origins) of God's character and the [radiance](#defining-glory) of His presence. While humans can possess glory as a [bestowed dignity](#human-glory-in-scripture), the ultimate purpose of all glory is to illuminate the [presence of God](#glory-in-the-new-jerusalem) and lead creation into worship. Recognizing this glory is not a burden but the [fulfillment of human purpose](#perspective-on-divine-glory).
