# Testimony

The relationship between personal faith and public expression has been a subject of debate for centuries. Some argue that spiritual beliefs should remain a private matter, while others suggest that the very nature of certain faiths requires an outward declaration. Exploring how early Christian writings address the act of testifying (providing a formal statement based on personal experience) can provide insight into the role of public confession (a public acknowledgement of belief) in the life of a believer.

## Scriptural Basis for Public Confession

Some people claim that Christianity is a private matter. However, Jesus said:

> So everyone who acknowledges me before men, I also will acknowledge before my Father who is in heaven, but whoever denies me before men, I also will deny before my Father who is in heaven. — Matthew 10:32-33 (ESV)

And John wrote:

> No one who denies the Son has the Father. Whoever confesses the Son has the Father also. — 1 John 2:23 (ESV)

> And we have seen and **testify** that the Father has sent his Son to be the Savior of the world. Whoever confesses that Jesus is the Son of God, God abides in him, and he in God. — 1 John 4:14-15 (ESV)

## The Connection Between Faith and Testimony

Paul explains the mechanism of salvation, which involves being justified (declared right before God) through heart-felt belief and an outward confession (an admission or acknowledgement) of faith:

> But what does it say? “The word is near you, in your mouth and in your heart” (Deuteronomy 30:14 (ESV)) (that is, the word of faith that we proclaim); because, if you confess with your mouth that Jesus is Lord and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. For with the heart one believes and is justified, and with the mouth one confesses and is saved. For the Scripture says, “Everyone who believes in him will not be put to shame.” (Isaiah 28:16; Joel 2:26,27 (ESV)) For there is no distinction between Jew and Greek; for the same Lord is Lord of all, bestowing his riches on all who call on him. For “everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.” (Joel 2:32 (ESV)) How then will they call on him in whom they have not believed? And how are they to believe in him of whom they have never heard? And how are they to hear without someone preaching? And how are they to preach unless they are sent? As it is written, “How beautiful are the feet of those who preach the good news!” (Isaiah 52:7; Nahum 1:15 (ESV)) So faith comes from hearing, and hearing through the word of Christ. — Romans 10:8-15,17 (ESV)

## Differing Perspectives on Private and Public Faith

While many scriptures emphasize the importance of public confession, some individuals argue that faith is an intensely personal matter that does not require outward display. This view often points to the need for humility and the avoidance of performative religion. For example, Jesus warned against practicing righteousness (living according to God's moral standards) before others to be seen by them, suggesting that some aspects of the spiritual life are best kept between the individual and God (Matthew 6:1 (ESV)).

However, proponents of public testimony argue that these warnings target hypocritical motives rather than the act of sharing one's faith. They suggest that true belief naturally leads to an outward expression, as seen in the life of Paul and other early believers. From this perspective, public testimony is not about seeking human approval, but about fulfilling a commission to be witnesses to the world (Acts 1:8 (ESV)). This outward expression serves as a means of encouragement for other believers and a formal statement of one's allegiance to Christ.

## Challenges of Public Testimony

Sharing one's faith is not always without risk. Even in modern times, some [believers face social or legal consequences when they publicly testify that Jesus is Lord](https://youtu.be/VRsBsitt6rQ). This reality highlights the potential cost of public confession and the personal conviction required to maintain it under pressure.

## Conclusion

The act of testimony serves as a bridge between internal belief and external practice. It involves a [scriptural basis](#scriptural-basis-for-public-confession) that emphasizes the role of acknowledging faith before others. While faith is personal, its [connection to public expression](#the-connection-between-faith-and-testimony) is often presented as a natural result of deep-seated conviction. Despite [differing perspectives](#differing-perspectives-on-private-and-public-faith) and the [challenges](#challenges-of-public-testimony) involved, public confession remains a significant aspect of the Christian experience.
