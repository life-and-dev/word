# The Purpose of Trials

Trials are an inevitable part of the human experience, often bringing pain, confusion, and distress. Yet, from a biblical perspective, these difficult periods are not without meaning or purpose. Scripture frequently uses the imagery of fire to describe how faith is tested and refined, much like gold is purified in a furnace. By exploring concepts such as the "baptism with fire" and the refining nature of life's challenges, one can begin to understand how these experiences serve to prepare a person for an eternal kingdom.

## Baptism with fire

John the Baptist (the prophet who prepared the way for Jesus) spoke of Jesus (Yeshua):

> I indeed baptize you with water unto repentance, but He who is coming after me is mightier than I, whose sandals I am not worthy to carry. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and **fire**.
>
> His winnowing fan (a tool used to toss grain into the air to separate the wheat from the waste) is in His hand, and He will thoroughly clean out His threshing floor, and gather His wheat into the barn; but **He will burn up the chaff (the worthless outer husks of the grain) with unquenchable fire**. — Matthew 3:11-12 (NKJV)

Jesus also stated that the purpose of this "baptism of fire" (a figurative cleansing or judgment) is to separate people according to their faithfulness. This separation can even occur within families.

> “I came to cast **fire** on the earth, and would that it were already kindled! I have a **baptism** to be baptized with, and how great is my distress until it is accomplished! Do you think that I have come to give peace on earth? 
> 
> No, I tell you, but rather **division**. For from now on in one house there will be five divided, three against two and two against three. They will be divided, father against son and son against father, mother against daughter and daughter against mother, mother-in-law against her daughter-in-law and daughter-in-law against mother-in-law.” — Luke 12:49-53 (ESV)

## Purpose of trials

> You have been grieved by various **trials**, so that the **tested genuineness of your faith**<br>
> — more precious than gold that perishes though it is tested by **fire** —<br>
> may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ.
>
> — 1 Peter 1:6-7 (ESV)

Peter refers to these trials in the past tense. These events were already happening during the believer's lifetime. The "test by fire" is not exclusively a future event that happens after death. Paul also adds that this "fire test" is [necessary for the preservation of what is truly valuable](https://wordtruth.org/gods-purposes-in-his-childrens-trials/).

> For no one can lay a foundation other than that which is laid, which is Jesus Christ. Now if anyone builds on the foundation with gold, silver, precious stones, wood, hay, straw — each one's work will become manifest, for the Day will disclose it, because it will be revealed by **fire**, and the **fire will test** what sort of work each one has done:
>
> * If the work that anyone has built on the foundation survives, he will **receive a reward**.
> * If anyone's work is burned up, he will **suffer loss**, though he himself will be saved, but only as through fire.
>
> — 1 Corinthians 3:11-15 (ESV)

The point being made is that all people are building a kingdom. One can build for Jesus Christ, the eternal foundation whose works cannot be destroyed and who rewards His servants. Alternatively, a person might build a structure for themselves. Such a structure will burn up when the "fire test" comes, resulting in a loss for the builder.

The writings of the apostles encourage believers to focus on eternal life rather than temporary pleasure. King Solomon was the richest king who ever lived in Israel. He accomplished everything he wanted, yet he wrote about the [vanity of life](https://www.cru.org/us/en/blog/life-and-relationships/hardships/the-significance-of-trials.html) at the end of his years.

> “Meaningless! Meaningless!” says the Teacher.<br>
> “Utterly meaningless! Everything is meaningless.”<br>
> “What do people gain from all their labors at which they toil under the sun?”
> 
> — Ecclesiastes 1:2-3 (NIV)

Solomon closes his final book with an emphasis on the ultimate accountability of every person.

> Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the duty of all mankind. For **God will bring every deed into judgment**, including every hidden thing, whether it is good or evil. — Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 (NIV)

Ecclesiastes 12:13 reminds the reader to fear God and keep His commandments. This is described as the duty of all mankind. God will bring every deed into judgment, whether good or evil.

## Eternal Perspective

The question of what purpose or benefit a life brings becomes critical when viewed through the lens of eternity. It is important to consider whether the fruits of one's labor will endure or be lost.

* Treasures gathered for oneself may be destroyed or stolen, offering no benefit in the afterlife (Matthew 6:19-21). Wasted opportunities may be difficult to explain.
* Alternatively, a life focused on leading others to repentance may result in a joyous welcome by Jesus Christ (Matthew 8:11; 1 Thessalonians 4:17). Such a life inherits the kingdom of the Father (Matthew 25:34).

## Conclusion

The purpose of trials is to test and refine the genuineness of a person's faith, much like fire purifies gold. This [refining process](#purpose-of-trials) prepares the individual for the eternal kingdom. The "baptism with fire" serves as a [divine separation](#baptism-with-fire) between the faithful and the unfaithful, emphasizing the need for an [eternal perspective](#eternal-perspective) on life's labors. Ultimately, these [challenges faced today](#purpose-of-trials) are intended to result in praise, glory, and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ.
