# Trials

Life often presents challenges that seem overwhelming, yet many traditions view these difficulties as more than just random occurrences. In the biblical narrative, these experiences are frequently described through the metaphor of fire. This is a process that purifies and reveals the true quality of what lies beneath the surface. Understanding this perspective can provide a different lens through which to view one's personal journey and the ultimate significance of life's labors.

## Baptism with fire

John the Baptist (a first-century Jewish prophet who prepared the way for Jesus) said of Jesus (Yeshua):

> “I baptize you with water for repentance, but he who is coming after me is mightier than I, whose sandals I am not worthy to carry. He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire. His winnowing fork (a tool used to toss grain into the air so the wind can blow away the lighter husks) is in his hand, and he will clear his threshing floor and gather his wheat into the barn, but the chaff (the worthless outer husks of the grain) he will burn with unquenchable fire.” — Matthew 3:11-12 (ESV)

Jesus also explained that the purpose of the baptism with fire (a spiritual process of purification and judgment) is to separate people, even within the same families, according to their faithfulness (Luke 12:42).

> “I came to cast fire on the earth, and would that it were already kindled! I have a baptism to be baptized with, and how great is my distress until it is accomplished! Do you think that I have come to give peace on earth? No, I tell you, but rather division. For from now on in one house there will be five divided, three against two and two against three. They will be divided, father against son and son against father, mother against daughter and daughter against mother, mother-in-law against her daughter-in-law and daughter-in-law against mother-in-law.” — Luke 12:49-53 (ESV)

## Purpose of trials

> In this you rejoice, though now for a little while, if necessary, you have been grieved by various trials, so that the tested genuineness of your faith — more precious than gold that perishes though it is tested by fire — may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ.
>
> — 1 Peter 1:6-7 (ESV)

The apostle Peter refers to these trials (difficult experiences that test one's character) as something occurring during the believer's lifetime. James also encourages believers to "count it all joy" when meeting various trials, as the testing of faith produces steadfastness (James 1:2-4 (ESV)). The "test by fire" is not exclusively a future event that happens after death.

The prophet Malachi described the coming of the LORD as a "refiner's fire," purifying his people like gold and silver (Malachi 3:2-3 (ESV)). Paul also adds that this "fire test" is necessary so that some could be saved.

> For no one can lay a foundation other than that which is laid, which is Jesus Christ. Now if anyone builds on the foundation with gold, silver, precious stones, wood, hay, straw — each one's work will become manifest, for the Day will disclose it, because it will be revealed by **fire**, and the **fire will test** what sort of work each one has done:
>
> * If the work that anyone has built on the foundation survives, he will receive a reward.
> * If anyone's work is burned up, he will suffer loss, though he himself will be saved, but only as through fire.
> — 1 Corinthians 3:11-15 (ESV)

The apostle Paul explains that all people are building a kingdom. One may build for Jesus Christ, who is the eternal foundation. These works cannot be destroyed, and the builder will receive a reward. Alternatively, one may build a structure for themselves. Such work will burn up during the testing fire, resulting in a loss for the individual.

Studies of the apostolic gospels indicate a clear encouragement for believers to focus on eternal life rather than temporary pleasures during a short lifetime. King Solomon, credited as the wealthiest king of Israel who accomplished his many goals, expressed a sobering perspective at the end of his life in the book of Ecclesiastes:

> “Vanity of vanities, says the Preacher, vanity of vanities! All is vanity. What does man gain by all the toil at which he toils under the sun?” — Ecclesiastes 1:2-3 (ESV)

Then King Solomon, closes his final book with the words:

> “The end of the matter; all has been heard. Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man. For God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil.” — Ecclesiastes 12:13-14 (ESV)

## Assessing Life's Impact

![A refiner purifying gold in a furnace](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Gold_refining.jpg)

The significance of a person's life is revealed by its lasting impact. One might consider whether the treasures gathered and the structures built will eventually be destroyed or stolen (Matthew 6:19-21). Such temporary achievements offer no benefit in an eternal context. In contrast, leading others to repentance can result in eternal rewards (1 Thessalonians 2:19; Matthew 8:11). Those who focus on eternal purposes are welcomed into the kingdom of God (Matthew 25:34).

## Conclusion

The biblical concept of trials emphasizes that life's challenges serve a greater purpose. Through the [baptism with fire](#baptism-with-fire), individuals experience a process of purification and separation. These [trials](#purpose-of-trials) are not merely obstacles but are essential for refining faith and preparing for eternal rewards. Ultimately, [assessing life's impact](#assessing-lifes-impact) encourages a focus on eternal goals rather than temporary achievements.
