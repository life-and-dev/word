# Mediator

The concept of a mediator (one who acts as a go-between to resolve conflicts or establish agreements) is central to understanding how humanity relates to the Divine. In various cultures and legal systems, a mediator facilitates communication between two parties. This article examines the biblical definition of a mediator and explores the unique role that Jesus Christ plays in bridging the gap between a holy God and imperfect humans.

## Greek Definition

The New Testament uses the Greek word *mesités* to describe a mediator. This term highlights the function of someone who stands in the middle to bring two parties together.

|                   | [Strong's Lexicon](https://biblehub.com/greek/3316.htm) |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Original Word     | μεσίτης                                                 |
| Part of Speech    | Noun, Masculine                                         |
| Transliteration   | mesités                                                 |
| Pronunciation     | meh-SEE-tace                                            |
| Phonetic Spelling | (mes-ee'-tace)                                          |
| Definition        | Mediator                                                |

> Word Origin: Derived from μέσος (mesos), meaning "middle" or "between."
> 
> Corresponding Greek / Hebrew Entries: While there is no direct Hebrew equivalent for "mesités," the concept of a mediator can be related to the role of a priest (כֹּהֵן, kohen) or an intercessor (פָּגַע, paga).
> 
> Usage: The term "mesités" refers to a mediator, one who intervenes between two parties to restore peace, make a covenant, or ratify an agreement. In the New Testament, it is primarily used to describe Jesus Christ as the mediator between God and humanity, highlighting His role in reconciling sinful humans to a holy God through His sacrificial death and resurrection.
> 
> Cultural and Historical Background: In ancient Greek culture, a mediator was often a neutral party who facilitated negotiations or resolved disputes. In the context of the New Testament, the concept of a mediator is deeply rooted in the Jewish understanding of a priestly role, where the priest acted as an intermediary between God and the people. Jesus, as the ultimate High Priest, fulfills this role perfectly, bridging the gap caused by sin. — [Strong's Lexicon](https://biblehub.com/greek/3316.htm)

## The Role of Jesus Christ

Jesus is the unique mediator between God and humanity. The apostle Paul emphasizes this singular role in his letter to Timothy.

> For there is one [God](god.md), and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus — 1 Timothy 2:5 (ESV)

The choice to refer to Jesus as the one mediator is appropriate because he made it possible for humans to access God. His [sacrifice](sacrifice.md) (offering of himself) redeemed (purchased freedom for) people from their sin (transgression against God's law). This act of mediation is necessary because God's holiness and human sinfulness cannot coexist without a reconciler.

## The New Covenant

The role of Jesus as a mediator is closely linked to the establishment of a new covenant (a formal and binding agreement) between God and his people. The book of Hebrews explains how this new arrangement surpasses the previous one.

> But as it is, Christ has obtained a ministry that is as much more excellent than the old as the covenant he mediates is better, since it is enacted on better promises. — Hebrews 8:6 (ESV)

This new covenant makes [forgiveness](forgiveness.draft.md) (the cancellation of debt or guilt) possible and allows for a direct relationship with God. The mediation of Jesus is effective because it is based on his own blood rather than the blood of animals.

> For if the blood of goats and bulls, and the sprinkling of defiled persons with the ashes of a heifer, sanctify for the purification of the flesh, how much more will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without blemish to God, purify our conscience from dead works to serve the living God. Therefore he is the mediator of a new covenant, so that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance, since a death has occurred that redeems them from the transgressions committed under the first covenant. — Hebrews 9:13-15 (ESV)

Jesus is also described as the mediator of this covenant in the context of the heavenly Jerusalem.

> and to Jesus, the mediator of a new covenant, and to the sprinkled blood that speaks a better word than the blood of Abel. — Hebrews 12:24 (ESV)

## Addressing Critiques

Some people question whether Jesus is the only mediator. They often point to the role of saints or religious leaders who intercede (pray on behalf of others) for believers. However, it is important to distinguish between unique mediation and communal intercession.

While the Bible encourages Christians to pray for one another, Jesus is the only one who can reconcile humanity to God through a sacrificial death. Other figures may act as facilitators of communication or prayer, but they do not provide the basis for the covenant itself. Critiques may argue that this view is too exclusive. In response, the text clarifies that the exclusivity of Jesus is based on his dual nature (being both divine and human), which allows him to represent both parties perfectly. Further information on this topic can be found on reputable biblical resource sites like [Got Questions](https://www.gotquestions.org/Jesus-mediator-new-covenant.html).

## Conclusion

A mediator serves as an essential go-between to reconcile two parties. Jesus Christ fulfills this role by bridging the gap between God and humanity through the [Greek Definition](#greek-definition) of a *mesités*. As the sole mediator, he enables access to God and establishes [The Role of Jesus Christ](#the-role-of-jesus-christ) as the reconciler. This mediation is the foundation of [The New Covenant](#the-new-covenant) which offers forgiveness and eternal life. While different views exist regarding [Addressing Critiques](#addressing-critiques) of this exclusivity, the biblical text maintains that Jesus remains the unique intermediary between the Creator and his creation.
