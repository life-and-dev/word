# Mediator

The concept of a mediator is central to understanding how a relationship between two parties can be restored when a significant barrier exists between them. In various cultures and legal systems, a mediator acts as an impartial bridge, facilitating communication and resolving disputes to achieve peace. The biblical narrative employs this concept to describe a profound reconciliation that addresses the separation between the divine and humanity. This article explores the linguistic roots of the term and its application to the unique role attributed to Jesus Christ.

## Lexical Definition

|                   | [Strong's Lexicon](https://biblehub.com/greek/3316.htm) |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Original Word     | μεσίτης                                                 |
| Part of Speech    | Noun, Masculine                                         |
| Transliteration   | mesités                                                 |
| Pronunciation     | meh-SEE-tace                                            |
| Phonetic Spelling | (mes-ee'-tace)                                          |
| Definition        | Mediator                                                |

> Word Origin: Derived from μέσος (mesos), meaning "middle" or "between."
> 
> Corresponding Greek / Hebrew Entries: While there is no direct Hebrew equivalent for "mesités," the concept of a mediator can be related to the role of a priest (כֹּהֵן, kohen) or an intercessor (פָּגַע, paga).
> 
> Usage: The term "mesités" refers to a mediator, one who intervenes between two parties to restore peace, make a covenant, or ratify an agreement. In the New Testament, it is primarily used to describe Jesus Christ as the mediator between God and humanity, highlighting His role in reconciling sinful humans to a holy God through His sacrificial death and resurrection.
> 
> Cultural and Historical Background: In ancient Greek culture, a mediator was often a neutral party who facilitated negotiations or resolved disputes. In the context of the New Testament, the concept of a mediator is deeply rooted in the Jewish understanding of a priestly role, where the priest acted as an intermediary between God and the people. Jesus, as the ultimate High Priest, fulfills this role perfectly, bridging the gap caused by sin.
>

## New Testament Usage

Jesus is identified as a mediator between God and humanity, according to the apostle Paul:

> For there is one [God](god.md), and there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus — 1 Timothy 2:5 (ESV)

The choice to refer to Jesus as "one mediator between God and men" is considered appropriate because Jesus made it possible for humanity to access God. This was achieved through his sacrifice, which redeemed (the act of buying back or rescuing from captivity) humanity from sin (actions or attitudes that fall short of God's perfect standard). This covenant (a formal and binding agreement between parties) makes forgiveness possible and allows for a relationship with God. Consequently, Jesus is regarded as a mediator.

> For if the blood of goats and bulls, and the sprinkling of defiled persons with the ashes of a heifer, sanctify for the purification of the flesh, how much more will the blood of Christ, who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without blemish to God, purify our conscience from dead works to serve the living God. **Therefore he is the mediator of a new covenant**, so that those who are called may receive the promised eternal inheritance, since a death has occurred that redeems them from the transgressions committed under the first covenant. — Hebrews 9:13-15 (ESV)

## Common Critiques

Some argue that the claim of Jesus being the "one mediator" excludes the possibility of believers praying for one another or the role of saints in intercession. This perspective suggests that if there is only one mediator, any other form of intercessory prayer would be redundant or even scripturally prohibited.

However, the biblical context of "one mediator" in 1 Timothy 2:5 refers specifically to the unique role of Christ in providing atonement and establishing the new covenant. While believers are encouraged to intercede for one another (1 Timothy 2:1), this type of intercession is distinct from the mediatorial role of Christ. Christ's mediation is unique because it is based on his dual nature as both God and man and his once-for-all sacrificial death. Other forms of intercession do not provide redemption or bridge the fundamental separation caused by sin, but rather serve as an expression of the community of faith working together under Christ's primary mediation.

## Conclusion

The term mediator describes one who stands between two parties to facilitate reconciliation. This role is rooted in both [ancient lexical definitions](#lexical-definition) and the biblical narrative of the [new covenant](#new-testament-usage). Jesus Christ is presented as the sole mediator who bridges the gap between God and humanity through his unique sacrifice. While other forms of intercession exist within the community of faith, they do not replace the [singular mediatorial work of Christ](#common-critiques).
