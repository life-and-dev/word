# Forgiveness

The English word "forgive" carries a broad range of meanings, from simply letting go of a grudge to formally cancelling a massive debt. In the original Greek of the New Testament, however, several distinct words are used to describe these different actions. Understanding the specific nuances of these terms provides a clearer picture of how authors intended to communicate the concept of forgiveness in various contexts.

## Translations

Different Greek words with different meanings are translated as "forgive" in modern English Bibles. This can sometimes lead to confusion between the distinct concepts that the original authors intended to express.

### apoluó

The Greek word "apoluó" (to set free or release) means to formally release someone from his/her obligations, debt or imprisonment.

|                   | [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/630.htm)    |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Original Word     | ἀπολύω                                                        |
| Part of Speech    | Verb                                                          |
| Transliteration   | apoluó                                                        |
| Phonetic Spelling | (ap-ol-oo'-o)                                                 |
| Definition        | to set free, release                                          |
| Usage             | I release, let go, send away, divorce, am rid; mid: I depart. |

> * 630 apolýō (from 575 /apó, "away from" and 3089 /lýō, "to loose, release") – properly, to let go; release (discharge), dismiss. This term implies the release (annulment) of an existing bond.
> * 630 /apolýō ("to release") is specifically used of divorcing a marital partner (Mt 1:19, 5:31,32, 19:7-9 – parallels at Mk 10:2-12; Lk 16:18).
> — [HELPS Word-studies](https://biblehub.com/greek/630.htm)

"apoluó" is translated as "forgive" in:

> Judge not, and ye shall not be judged: condemn not, and ye shall not be condemned: ***forgive*** *(apoluó)*, and ye shall be ***forgiven*** *(apoluó)*. — Luke 6:37 (KJV)

In this context Jesus was referring to enemies and refraining from spitefulness (Luke 6:27-36 (ESV)). Jesus was asking his disciples to release their enemies from the revenge that was due to them. This is more clear in a parallel scripture recorded by Matthew 5:38-48 (ESV).

Other translations of the same word "apoluó":

| send away / dismiss | Matthew 14:15,22-23, 15:23,32; Mark 6:36,45, 8:3,9 (ESV)       |
| release (prisoner)  | Matthew 18:27, 27:15,17,21,26 (ESV)                            |

### charizomai

The Greek word "charizomai" (to graciously give or show favor) goes further than just dismissing the wrong other did. It refers to the act of showing underserved favour or kindness instead of revenge.

| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Original Word     | χαρίζομαι                                                        |
| Part of Speech    | Verb                                                             |
| Transliteration   | charizomai                                                       |
| Phonetic Spelling | (khar-id'-zom-ahee)                                              |
| Definition        | to show favor, give freely                                       |
| Usage             | (a) I show favor to, (b) I pardon, forgive, (c) I show kindness. |

> * Cognate: 5483 xarízomai (from 5485 /xáris, "grace, extending favor") – properly, to extend favor ("grace"), freely give favor to grant forgiveness (pardon).
> * 5483 /xarízomai ("favor that cancels") is used of God giving His grace to pardon. This is freely done and therefore not based on any merit of the one receiving forgiveness.
> * [5483 (xarízomai) literally means, "to exercise grace, freely show favor," i.e. willingly ("graciously") bestow.]
> — [HELPS Word-studies](https://biblehub.com/greek/5483.htm)

"charizomai" is translated as "forgive" in the context of:

* Forgiving your enemy (2 Corinthians 2:7-10 (ESV)).
* Forgiving Paul (2 Corinthians 12:13 (ESV)).
* Forgiving believers (Ephesians 4:32; Colossians 3:13 (ESV)).
* Christ forgiving his disciples (Colossians 2:13, 3:13 (ESV)).

Other translations of the same word "charizomai":

| translation           | scriptures                                        |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| bestowed              | Luke 7:21; Philippians 2:9 (ESV)                  |
| cancelled debt        | Luke 7:42-43 (ESV)                                |
| granted               | Acts 3:14, 27:24 (ESV)                            |
| "give up" (hand over) | Acts 25:11,16; Romans 8:32 (ESV)                  |
| give / gave (freely)  | 1 Corinthians 2:12; Galatians 3:18; Philemon 1:22 (ESV) |

The Greek word "aphiémi" is translated as "forgive" in several contexts:

* God the Father forgiving the sins and debts of repentant people (Acts 8:22; Romans 4:7; 1 John 1:9 (ESV)).
    * This forgiveness is often conditional on the person also forgiving others (Matthew 6:12-15; Mark 11:25; Luke 11:4 (ESV)).
* The warning that blasphemy against the Holy Spirit will not be forgiven (Matthew 12:31-32; Mark 3:28-29 (ESV)).
* Jesus forgiving sins on behalf of the Father (Matthew 9:2-6; Mark 2:6-10; Luke 5:20-23 (ESV)).
* Jesus forgiving sins committed against himself (Matthew 12:32 (ESV)).
* Jesus requesting that the Father forgive people (Luke 23:34 (ESV)).
* Jesus expecting his disciples to repeatedly forgive the sins of others (Matthew 18:21; Luke 17:3-4 (ESV)).
* The disciples receiving the power to forgive or withhold the sins of others (John 20:23 (ESV)).

## Nuances in Comparison

While these Greek words are often translated as "forgive," they each emphasize a different aspect of the action:

* **apoluó** emphasizes the legal or physical release from an obligation, debt, or bond (Luke 6:37 (ESV)).
* **charizomai** emphasizes the gracious and unmerited nature of the favor shown to the offender (Ephesians 4:32 (ESV)).
* **aphiémi** emphasizes the act of sending away or dismissing the offense itself, often in a judicial or relational sense (1 John 1:9 (ESV)).

## Conclusion

Forgiveness in the New Testament is a multi-faceted concept expressed through several distinct Greek terms. The word [apoluó](#apoluó) emphasizes the legal or physical release from an obligation or bond. In contrast, [charizomai](#charizomai) highlights the act of showing unmerited favor and graciousness toward an offender. The term [aphiémi](#aphiémi) is frequently used to describe the dismissal or sending away of sins and debts, particularly in the context of divine mercy. Recognizing these [nuances](#nuances-in-comparison) provides a richer understanding of the biblical call to forgive and the nature of God's own forgiveness.
