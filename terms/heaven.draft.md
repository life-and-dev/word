# Heaven

The concept of heaven often conjures images of golden gates, fluffy clouds, and a mysterious realm in the sky. While these depictions are common in art and folklore, they frequently differ from the descriptions found within the biblical text. Many people assume that upon death, an "immortal" soul continues to live in such a place. These ideas may have been influenced by Greek mythology, which described Elysium as a realm where heroes and gods resided after death. By examining the cultural context and original languages of the scriptures, one can uncover a perspective that may challenge modern assumptions about the ultimate future.

However, the Bible states that when people die, they remain dead. This is the reason for the biblical emphasis on the need for a resurrection like that of Jesus.

## Elijah and the Whirlwind

Many people believe that Elijah went directly to a heavenly realm without dying, based on a passage in 2 Kings:

> And as they still went on and talked, behold, chariots of fire and horses of fire separated the two of them. And Elijah went up by a whirlwind into heaven. And Elisha saw it and he cried, “My father, my father! The chariots of Israel and its horsemen!” And he saw him no more. — 2 Kings 2:11-12 (ESV)

The Israelites who lived during Elijah's time were not influenced by Greek mythology. To them, "heaven" often referred simply to the sky. For example:

> He blotted out every living thing that was on the face of the ground, man and animals and creeping things and birds of **the heavens**. — Genesis 7:23 (ESV)

Another example describes the sky during a storm:

> And at the seventh time he said, “Behold, a little cloud like a man's hand is rising from the sea.”
> And he said, “Go up, say to Ahab, ‘Prepare your chariot and go down, lest the rain stop you.’”
>
> And in a little while **the heavens** grew black with clouds and wind, and there was a great rain.
>
> — 1 Kings 18:44-45 (ESV)

The prophet Isaiah also used the term in this way:

> For as the rain and the snow come down from **heaven** and do not return there but water the earth... — Isaiah 55:10 (ESV)

The apostles also referred to "heaven" as the sky. For instance, in the book of Acts:

> We also are men, of like nature with you, and we bring you good news, that you should turn from these vain things to a living God, who made **the heaven** and the earth and the sea and all that is in them. In past generations he allowed all the nations to walk in their own ways. Yet he did not leave himself without witness, for he did good by giving you rains from **heaven**... — Acts 14:15-17 (ESV)

Later in the book of 2 Chronicles, it is recorded that Elijah sent a letter to King Jehoram of Judah:

> And a letter came to him from Elijah the prophet, saying, “Thus says the LORD, the God of David your father, ‘Because you have not walked in the ways of Jehoshaphat your father, or in the ways of Asa king of Judah... — 2 Chronicles 21:12 (ESV)

This event occurred several years after Elijah's ascent in the whirlwind. This provides further evidence that he remained on Earth and continued his prophetic ministry in a different location.

Critics may argue that 2 Kings 2:11 explicitly states Elijah went "into heaven." However, as shown by the various uses of the term in scripture, this most likely refers to the sky. Furthermore, Jesus stated in John 3:13 that "no one has ascended into heaven except he who descended from heaven, the Son of Man." This supports the view that Elijah did not enter the dwelling place of God. The prophets of that time also did not believe Elijah had entered a divine realm. Instead, they thought he might have been set down somewhere else. This is why they searched for him.

> While others wanted to search for Elijah, Elisha understood that if God had wanted Elijah there, He would not have moved him. Understanding the customs of the time further helps to clarify this section of Scripture. God had told Elijah to anoint Elisha to succeed him (1 Kings 19:16). According to custom, Elisha could not assume leadership of the prophets as long as Elijah was present. Therefore, in order to allow Elisha to lead, God had to find a way to graciously remove Elijah. He chose to do it by a whirlwind. Elijah was not taken up to the place where God lives, but he was taken up into the air and placed somewhere else. God simply moved Elijah from one place to another and he lived out his days somewhere else. Elijah could not have been taken to heaven to live with God because he lived before the time of Christ. It is Christ’s shed blood that is the only valid payment for all men’s sin, including Elijah’s. If Elijah were already enjoying everlasting life in heaven, there was no need for Christ to come and to die for his sin. — [Spirit and Truth International](https://spiritandtruthonline.org/verses-sometimes-used-to-support-the-idea-that-our-everlasting-future-is-in-heaven/)

## Rewards in Heaven

### The Gospel of Matthew

Several verses mention a reward in heaven. For example:

> Rejoice and be glad, for **your reward is great in heaven**... — Matthew 5:12 (ESV)

Another passage instructs:

> Do not lay up for yourselves treasures on earth, where moth and rust destroy and where thieves break in and steal, but lay up for yourselves **treasures in heaven**, where neither moth nor rust destroys and where thieves do not break in and steal. — Matthew 6:19-20 (ESV)

Taken out of context, these verses might suggest that people go to heaven immediately upon death to receive rewards. However, Matthew frequently refers to God's kingdom as the "Kingdom of Heaven." In this context, "in heaven" may be understood as synonymous with "the Kingdom of Heaven." Matthew 5 begins with this focus:

> Seeing the crowds, he went up on the mountain, and when he sat down, his disciples came to him. And he opened his mouth and taught them, saying:
>
> "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is **the kingdom of heaven**."
>
> ...
>
> "Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake, for theirs is **the kingdom of heaven**."
>
> — Matthew 5:1-3,10 (ESV)

Jesus continues to teach about the kingdom of heaven throughout this discourse. The use of "heaven" as a shorthand for the "kingdom of heaven" is a common feature in Matthew's writing.

### The Apostolic Epistles

The apostles also wrote about hope and inheritance kept in heaven:

> ... the hope laid up for you in **heaven**. — Colossians 1:5 (ESV)

Another letter mentions a living hope:

> According to His great mercy, He has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead, to an inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, **kept in heaven for you**... — 1 Peter 1:3-4 (ESV)

Before the time of the apostles, the prophet Malachi spoke of a record kept by God:

> Then those who feared the LORD spoke with one another. The LORD paid attention and heard them, and **a book of remembrance** was written before Him of those who feared the LORD and esteemed His name.
>
> "They shall be Mine", says the LORD of hosts, **in the day** when I make up My treasured possession, and I will spare them as a man spares his son who serves him. **Then** once more you shall see the distinction between the righteous and the wicked, between one who serves God and one who does not serve Him.
>
> — Malachi 3:16-18 (ESV)

The Israelites understood that a day of judgment would precede the receipt of rewards. Solomon also warned about a coming judgment:

> For God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil. — Ecclesiastes 12:14 (ESV)

The book of Revelation similarly describes a future judgment (Revelation 20:12). There is no scripture that explicitly states people receive instant heavenly rewards at the moment of death. The idea of immediate heavenly gratification may be influenced more by folk tales and Greek mythology than by biblical texts.

Several explanations have been proposed for the phrase "in heaven":

* "Heaven" may be a Hebrew idiom (a characteristic expression of a language) for something that is good. A reward "in heaven" would thus be a very high-quality reward. This interpretation is explored by some biblical scholars who focus on linguistic patterns in the ancient near east.
* The reward may be the relationship with God. Since Jesus referred to God as "Our Father in Heaven," the reward is to be with Him.
* "Heaven" could be understood as a secure place where God preserves rewards until the time they are to be received.

In these views, the reward is safe because it is with God. It is not subject to the theft, damage, or decay found on Earth.

## The Father's House

The New International Version (NIV) translates a passage in John 14 as:

> My Father’s house has many rooms; if that were not so, would I have told you that I am going there to prepare a place for you? And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come back and take you to be with me that you also may be where I am. — John 14:2-3 (NIV)

This translation can give the impression that Jesus returns to take believers to heaven at the moment of death. However, this interpretation presents a challenge to the biblical doctrine of the second coming. If Jesus had to return for every individual believer at death, the prophecies of a single, future second coming would be difficult to reconcile.

The King James Version (KJV) provides a different emphasis:

> In my Father's house are many mansions: if it were not so, I would have told you. I go to prepare a place for you. And if I go and prepare a place for you, I will come again, and receive you unto myself; that where I am, there ye may be also. — John 14:2-3 (KJV)

According to [Strong's Concordance](https://biblehub.com/greek/3438.htm) (a tool used to find the original Greek and Hebrew meanings of biblical words), a more literal translation of "rooms" or "mansions" is "dwelling places." The Greek word is *moné*. Some scholars suggest that Jesus was not describing literal buildings in a far-off realm. Instead, they propose that believers themselves are the Father's dwelling place. This concept is mirrored in John 14:23, where Jesus says that He and the Father will make their "home" (*moné*) with the one who loves Him. In this view, Jesus is preparing a "place" (Greek: *topos*) for believers, which may refer to a position of honor or a specific role within God's purpose.

A common critique of this interpretation is that the traditional view of heavenly mansions has been held by many for centuries. Advocates of the traditional view point to the comfort it provides to the bereaved. However, proponents of the "dwelling place" interpretation argue that the promise of God's intimate presence and a specific role in His kingdom offers a different, yet profound, kind of hope. They emphasize that this view aligns more closely with the overall biblical narrative of God's kingdom coming to Earth.

## God's Kingdom on Earth

Even after his resurrection, Jesus' disciples did not seem to expect a future in a celestial heaven. They anticipated that he would establish his kingdom on Earth. This is evident in their question to him before his ascension:

> So when they had come together, they asked him, “Lord, will you at this time restore the kingdom to Israel?” — Acts 1:6 (ESV)

## The Concept of the World

The Greek word [*aión*](https://biblehub.com/greek/165.htm) is sometimes translated as "the world." This can give the impression of transcending into a different physical world. However, the term often refers to an "age" or a period of time. This suggests a transition to a new era on Earth rather than a relocation to a different realm.

## Conclusion

The biblical concept of "heaven" is multifaceted, often referring to the physical sky rather than a mysterious afterlife realm. Many popular ideas about heaven may be influenced by external mythologies rather than the scriptures themselves. Evidence suggests that [figures like Elijah remained on Earth](#elijah-and-the-whirlwind) and that [rewards are preserved by God](#rewards-in-heaven) for a future time. Furthermore, the [promises made by Jesus](#the-fathers-house) and the [expectations of the disciples](#gods-kingdom-on-earth) point toward a future hope centered on [a coming age](#the-concept-of-the-world) on Earth.
