# Counterarguments

Different people interpret the Bible in various ways, often leading to disagreements about its meaning. When one group presents an argument for a specific interpretation, others may offer rebuttals to challenge that view. These counterarguments often focus on logical reasoning, the accuracy of translations, or the context of the scriptures being cited. By understanding these common rebuttals, one can better evaluate the different theological perspectives that exist today.

## Assumption
The argument assumes that a certain aspect is the truth while there is no convincing evidence to support it.

For example:

> A is B because of X, however
> X is not proven

## Bad Correlation
The argument incorrectly correlates unrelated truths.

For example:

> A is B *(correct)*, and
> C is D *(correct)*
>
> therefore they assume
>
> A is D

## Bad Quotation
A missing or wrong scripture is quoted to support the argument.

For example:

> A is B, because of quote
> *(but quote is invalid)*

The counter to this counterargument is that often people incorrectly quote scripture because they are paraphrasing. Paraphrases that change the meaning of the text do not support an argument.

## Bad Translation
There is no strong evidence to support the translation which changes the meaning of the scripture.

For example:

> Original text: A is A
> Translated text: A is B

## Biased Translation
The argument is supported by a biased translator who incorrectly chose the wrong version of a word that has multiple meanings.

> Definition of A: Could mean B or C (depending on context)
>
> Original text:   A is B *(original intention based on context)*
> Translated text: A is C *(supports argument better)*

## Confusing Events
The argument assumes that distinct events refer to the same event.

For example:

> 1. A happened to a, then separately
> 2. B happened to b, but only afterward
>
> but they interpret
>
> A and B happened simultaneously to a and b or a = b

## Confused by Name Removal
The intended meaning of the text changed because [God's name was removed from the Bible's text](../terms/name.md#name-as-an-authority).

For example:

> "The son is our **lord** and **YHVH** is our God."
>
> but is translated:
>
> "The son is our ***Lord*** and ***the Lord*** is our God."

Critics might argue that "Lord" is a sufficient title for God and does not obscure His identity. However, when the same title is used for both the Father and the Son in the same passage, it can lead to confusion about which "Lord" is being referenced.

## Confusing Subjects
The argument confuses distinct subjects as if they all apply to the same subject:

For example:

> 1. A applies to subject a, and
> 2. B applies to subject b, but intended distinctively
>
> However, because they are part of the same sentence, people would view
>
> a = b (subject a is synonymous to b),
>
> then they would reason that the statement A and the statement B both apply to the subject a and b

## Confusing Translation
The scripture is incorrectly translated when compared with other verses which use the same word but for a different purpose.

> A could also mean B (but it depends on the context)
>
> Original text: A is X and A is X *(in same context)*
> Translated text: A is X and B is X *(in same context but confusing meanings)*

Alternatively:

> 1. A translates to X or Z, and
> 2. B translates to Y or Z
>
> but they translate it:
>
> Original text: A is allowed, but B is forbidden
> Confusing translation: Z is allowed, but Z is forbidden
> Clear translation: X is allowed, but Y is forbidden

## Context
The scripture is quoted out of context. The original intended meaning of the author's words does not support the argument.

For example:

> The author wrote: Only when X, then A is B, otherwise A is C.
>
> but the argument only quotes the "A is C" portion and the rest of the condition is ignored.

## Creed
The creed of the early church fathers proves nothing as these statements are not inspired by God. While creeds represent early Christian consensus, they are human formulations and are subject to error if they deviate from the biblical text.

## Demo
Jesus was not interacting with a different god. Jesus was demonstrating how his disciples must interact with him.

## Godhead
The argument ignores the complex Trinitarian (the belief in one God in three persons) doctrine of the Godhead (the nature of God). This view considers God as the Father, Jesus, or the Holy Spirit, or any combination of them. This argument requires a study of Christian theology and a solid understanding of ancient Hebrew and Greek manuscripts to understand the reasoning.

According to this doctrine, the terms God, Father, Jesus, and Holy Spirit may be used interchangeably as necessary so that the scripture does not contradict itself. This is because the doctrine holds that they are all equal in essence.

## Interpretation
The same scripture could be interpreted in different ways and more evidence is required to prove the argument.

## Limited Vocabulary
Although a word may have been invented to account for limited vocabulary, the concept itself is considered ancient.

## Majority
Something does not become the truth just because the majority agrees. For example, in the book of Acts, Christianity was considered a minority sect as the majority of the Jews believed Jesus was a fraud (Acts 24:5, 28:22). The Jews had a legacy of thousands of years of traditions to back them up, while Christianity at that time had only existed for a few years.

## Human Nature of Jesus
The scripture only refers to the human nature of Jesus. This argument avoids the complexities of the Godhead concept.

## Misunderstood Idiom
The argument stems from a misunderstanding of a proverb or idiom.

## Not Jesus
The argument assumes that the scripture applies to Jesus, but there is no concrete evidence in the quoted scriptures alone that supports the argument.

## Overgeneralized
The argument assumes that because one aspect is true, all similar aspects are also the same.

For example:

> The author wrote: A is X.
>
> but argument assumes:
>
> All is X.

## Proven Wrong
The argument makes an assumption which can be proven wrong with evidence.

For example:

> It is argued that:
>
> A is X
>
> but it is proven with evidence that
>
> A is not X

## Reflecting
Jesus was not really having a conversation with God. He was reflecting, thinking aloud, or sharing information with his disciples by providing an example of how he expected them to have a conversation with him. When Jesus asked a question to God, it is interpreted as a rhetorical question.

## Restriction of Jesus
Some scholars reason that Jesus is God, but he restricted or incarnated (embodied in human form) himself to have the same human limitations as an ordinary human. According to this view, he removed his human restrictions or became fully divine again only at a later moment, such as his baptism, crucifixion, resurrection, or ascension.

## Roles
A single person can be a father to his children, a son to his own father, a husband to his wife, an employee to his employer, and a friend to his friend. Likewise, the argument states that God can relate to humanity through different roles.

## Strive
Some scholars reason that Jesus meant people should only strive to become like him as far as humanly possible. They argue that the focus should be on his teachings, such as love, compassion, forgiveness, and humility, rather than trying to perform miracles.

## Symbolic
The argument is based on symbolic or prophetic language that was taken literally. The word of a church father, no matter how respected, does not make a statement true without clear evidence.

## Unknown Author
The argument quotes an unknown author to prove a point. For example, [the book of Hebrews](../books/hebrews.md) was written by an unknown author.

## Vision
The argument confuses a vision with reality. In a vision, God can show anything at any time without physical constraints (Acts 10:9-16).

## Wrong Conclusion
Although some facts might be true, the conclusion is wrong because it is based on false evidence or assumptions.

For example:

> A is B *(correct)*,
> B is C *(incorrect)*
>
> therefore they conclude
>
> A is C

## Conclusion

Biblical interpretation is a complex field where various rebuttals are used to challenge different viewpoints. These counterarguments often address issues such as [logical assumptions](#assumption), [bad correlations](#bad-correlation), and [quotation errors](#bad-quotation). They also highlight the impact of [translation biases](#biased-translation) and the importance of [context](#context). Furthermore, understanding doctrines like the [Godhead](#godhead) and the [human nature of Jesus](#human-nature-of-jesus) is essential for evaluating these rebuttals. By examining these [counterarguments](#counterarguments), one can gain a deeper appreciation for the diversity of thought in biblical scholarship.
